Glossary

Glossary

This glossary defines statistical and CQC-specific terms used throughout the guidance. Terms are organised alphabetically. See the relevant sections for fuller explanations and examples.

A

Assumption checking: The process of verifying that your data meets the requirements of a statistical method before applying it. See Z-scoring and t-tests for method-specific assumptions.

Accredited official statistics: The highest tier of official statistics in the UK. Accreditation is granted by the Office for Statistics Regulation (OSR) and certifies that a statistical series meets the full standards of the Code of Practice for Statistics. It is not a permanent status: the OSR monitors ongoing compliance and can remove accreditation where standards are not sustained. CQC’s accredited statistical series are subject to compliance with all three pillars of the Code. See QA Principles.

Accuracy: How close an estimate is, on average, to the true value it aims at – the absence of bias. Distinct from precision, which is how tightly repeated estimates group regardless of where their centre sits. An estimate can be precise but inaccurate (a tight group, off target) or accurate but imprecise (a loose group, centred on target). See Statistical Inference.

Alternative hypothesis: The possibility a statistical test weighs against the null – the scenario under consideration, such as that a provider’s true rate differs from the benchmark. A test never proves the alternative; a small p-value is only evidence that something other than business as usual may be at work. See Statistical Inference.

Analyst: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC’s QASQA Framework. The analyst designs and executes the analysis, including the assurance plan, carries out self-assurance, and responds to feedback from the assurer. See QA Principles.

Analytical lifecycle: The complete sequence of stages through which a piece of analytical work passes, from the initial scoping conversation with a commissioner through to delivery and sign-off. The AQuA Book and CQC’s QASQA Framework treat quality as a property of the whole cycle, not a gate at the end: problems originate at specific stages and cannot be rescued by later care. See QA Principles.

AQuA Book: Government guidance on producing robust, fit-for-purpose analysis. The CQC QA Framework aligns with AQuA principles.

Approver: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC’s QASQA Framework. The approver provides final accountability by scrutinising the work of analyst and assurer and confirming it has been appropriately assured before sign-off. See QA Principles.

Assurer: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC’s QASQA Framework. The assurer independently reviews and validates the analyst’s work, reviews the analyst’s self-assurance, and confirms to the approver that the work is sound. Must be independent from the analyst – not involved in the analysis being reviewed. See QA Principles.

Azure DevOps: The Microsoft suite of development and collaboration services used at CQC, the relevant part of which for analysts is Azure Repos. See Analytical Tooling.

Azure Repos: The repository (version-control) service within Azure DevOps, used at CQC for managing code. Fabric workspaces can be linked directly to Azure Repos, enabling analysts to version-control notebooks and scripts without leaving the Fabric environment. See Analytical Tooling.

Average: An everyday umbrella term for a measure of central tendency – a single value standing in for the typical one. The mean, the median, and the mode are all averages. In common use “the average” usually means the arithmetic mean, but this guidance keeps the words apart: mean for that calculation (and its family), median and mode for the other two, and average only as the umbrella over all three. See Describing Data.

B

Bar chart: A chart that represents the frequency or magnitude of each category in a dataset as a rectangular bar. Used for nominal and ordinal data. When categories have a natural order, bars should be arranged in that order (an ordered bar chart). Touching bars are used for continuous data (histograms); separated bars signal discrete categories. See Visualising Data.

Banding: Grouping providers into performance categories (e.g., significantly above/below average, within expected range) based on statistical analysis. Common in z-scoring applications.

Bin width: In a histogram, the range of values each bar spans. Choosing the bin width controls how much detail the chart shows: wide bins smooth over variation; narrow bins reveal local structure but can create noise. Bin width is an analytical choice, not a fact about the data. See Visualising Data.

Benjamini–Hochberg correction: See Multiple testing.

Bessel’s correction: Dividing by \(n-1\) instead of \(n\) in the sample variance and standard deviation, which corrects a downward bias when a sample is used to estimate a population’s spread. For describing the data in hand (treated as the whole population) divide by \(n\) and write \(\sigma\); the correction belongs to the sample/estimation setting, where the notation switches to \(s\). See Variation & Uncertainty and Statistical Inference.

Bias: Systematic error that makes results consistently wrong in one direction – an estimate whose aim is pulled off the true value, distinct from imprecision (a steady aim that simply scatters). Can arise from missing data (MNAR), measurement error, or inappropriate methods. Unlike imprecision, bias is not reduced by gathering more data and is not revealed by a confidence interval’s width. See Statistical Inference and Missing Data Foundations.

Bonferroni correction: A multiple testing adjustment that divides the significance level (α) by the number of tests performed (e.g., for 20 tests at α = 0.05, each individual test uses α = 0.0025). Conservative – reduces false positives at the cost of increased false negatives. Rarely applied in CQC routine profiling, where screening rather than confirmatory inference is the goal; instead, raise action thresholds or require flagging on multiple indicators. For formal evaluations, raise with the Guild. See Multiple testing.

C

C-chart: A control chart for count data (e.g., number of incidents per month). Assumes constant opportunity for events. See SPC Basics.

Callout box: Visual elements in this guidance that provide additional context (Rethinking boxes) or technical details (Overthinking boxes).

Categorical data: See Qualitative data.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT): Statistical principle stating that sample means approximate a normal distribution as sample size increases, even if the underlying data isn’t normal. Justifies using methods that assume normality for large samples (typically n ≥ 30). See Statistical Inference.

Code of Practice for Statistics 3.0: The UK Statistics Authority’s framework (Autumn 2025 edition) setting standards for statistics that serve the public good. Organised around three pillars – Trustworthiness (Standards 1–4), Quality (Standards 5–7), and Value (Standards 8–10) – with ten numbered standards. CQC’s QA Framework is designed to meet the Quality pillar obligations, particularly Standard 6 (Be rigorous: use suitable data sources and sound methods) and Standard 7 (Be open about quality: explain strengths, limitations and uncertainty). Note: older CoP editions used letter-prefixed codes (Q1, Q2, Q3, V2, etc.) that do not appear in Edition 3.0. See QA Principles.

Code review: The process of having someone not involved in writing an analysis read the code to check for errors, ambiguous logic, and departures from documented decisions. A distinct form of verification that guards against mistakes invisible to the original author. Requires the code to be in a shared repository. See QA Principles and Analytical Tooling.

Commissioner: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC’s QASQA Framework. The commissioner defines the analytical need, agrees that the proposed approach will meet requirements before work starts, and accepts the final analysis and assurance. At CQC, commissioners often act on behalf of end-users. See QA Principles.

Coefficient of Variation (CV): Normalised measure of spread calculated as SD / Mean (often as percentage). Makes variability comparable across different scales or units. Undefined when mean = 0. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Common cause variation: Random, expected fluctuation in a process. Contrast with special cause variation. See SPC Basics.

Complete case analysis: Analysing only observations with no missing data. Simple but can introduce bias if data is not MCAR. See Missing Data Foundations.

Confidence interval (CI): A range of values likely to contain the true parameter. A 95% CI means if we repeated the analysis many times, 95% of intervals would contain the true value. See Statistical Inference.

Continuous data: Quantitative data that can take any value within a range, including decimals (e.g., waiting times, percentages, rates). See Data Types.

Credible interval: The Bayesian counterpart of a confidence interval: a range that, given the data and a stated prior, genuinely has (say) a 95% probability of containing the true value – the interpretation often wrongly attached to a confidence interval. Requires a prior to be declared. See Statistical Inference.

Control chart: A time-series plot with control limits that signal when a process has changed. See SPC Basics.

Control limits: Thresholds on a control chart (typically ±3 standard deviations) that indicate special cause variation. Not the same as specification limits.

Correlation: A statistical measure of the relationship between two variables. Ranges from -1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive). See Correlation.

Correlation coefficient: The numerical value (r for Pearson, ρ for Spearman) that quantifies the strength and direction of a correlation. See Correlation.

CQC Guild (Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild): A community space where analysts can ask questions, share approaches, and learn from each other – through Teams channels, drop-in sessions, and wider networking. For complex cases, escalate to the Practice Lead for Quantitative Analytics and Statistics.

CUSUM (Cumulative Sum chart): A control chart more sensitive to small persistent shifts than standard Shewhart I-charts. Plots cumulative deviations from a target value. Appropriate for mortality monitoring and other contexts requiring early detection of small changes. Requires specialist implementation – raise with the Guild before use. See SPC Basics.

D

Data type: The nature of your data. Two main categories: Quantitative (continuous or discrete) and Qualitative (nominal or ordinal). Determines which statistical methods are appropriate. See Data Types.

Discrete data: Quantitative data that can only take specific values, typically whole numbers (e.g., number of incidents, bed count). See Data Types.

Distribution: The pattern of how values are spread out. Common distributions include normal (bell curve), skewed, and uniform.

E

Effect size: The magnitude of a difference or relationship, independent of sample size. Examples include Cohen’s d for t-tests. See t-tests.

Empirical rule: Under a roughly symmetric, bell-shaped distribution, approximately 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean, about 95% within two, and about 99.7% within three. It gives the standard deviation a concrete meaning – a value more than two standard deviations from the centre is roughly a one-in-twenty event under the bell. Heavy-tailed (leptokurtic) distributions exceed these percentages: extreme values arise more often than the rule predicts. See Distribution Shape and Z-scoring.

Estimand: The unknown quantity an analysis is trying to learn – a fixed feature of the population, such as a provider’s true falls rate. Distinguished from the estimator (the recipe used to approximate it) and the estimate (the number that recipe returns on a given sample). See Statistical Inference.

Estimate: A number computed from a sample to stand in for an unknown feature of the population (the parameter). A point estimate is a single such value; pairing it with a confidence interval expresses how precise it is. The activity of producing estimates is estimation. See Statistical Inference.

Estimator: The rule or recipe applied to sample data to approximate an estimand – for example, “take the sample mean falls rate.” A procedure fixed before the data are seen; the number it returns on a particular sample is the estimate. Its quality is judged by accuracy (bias) and precision. See Statistical Inference.

Ecological fallacy: Reading a group-level association as if it described individuals – inferring, from areas with higher deprivation having worse average access, that individual deprived patients have worse access. Group-level and individual-level relationships can differ in size and even direction. State the level your analysis applies to. See Correlation.

EWMA (Exponentially Weighted Moving Average chart): A control chart that gives more weight to recent observations, making it sensitive to gradual drifts. Requires specialist implementation – raise with the Guild before use. See SPC Basics.

Expected value: The average or predicted value based on a model or national benchmark. Used as the comparison point in z-scoring.

F

False negative: Missing a real problem (Type II error). In regulation, failing to identify a poorly performing provider.

False positive: Flagging something as a problem when it isn’t (Type I error). In regulation, investigating a provider whose performance is actually acceptable.

Funnel plot: A scatter plot showing provider performance against sample size, with control limits that widen for smaller samples to reflect the greater uncertainty in small-provider estimates. Providers outside the outer (99.8%) limits are equivalent to those with |z| > 3.09. Preferred over tables of z-scores when presenting results to stakeholders. See Funnel Plots.

G

Generalised mean (Power mean): A family of averaging methods that includes arithmetic, geometric, and harmonic means as special cases. All are unified by a power parameter p. Different values of p give different means, each appropriate for different data structures (additive, multiplicative, rates). See Describing Data.

Generative AI: A family of tools that produce text, code, or images on demand in response to a prompt, typically built on a large language model. Genuinely useful for drafting, explaining, and brainstorming, but it generates fluent output whether or not that output is correct, so every use requires verification and the analyst – never the tool – remains accountable for the work. See Generative AI.

Geometric mean: A type of generalised mean (p → 0). The nth root of the product of n values. Appropriate for multiplicative processes, growth rates, and ratios. Less sensitive to extreme values than arithmetic mean. See Describing Data.

Gini’s heterogeneity index (D): A diversity measure for nominal data, \(D = 1 - \sum p_i^2\) – the probability that two randomly chosen cases fall in different categories. \(D = 0\) when all cases share one category, rising to \((k-1)/k\) for an even split across \(k\) categories. The same quantity is also called the Gini–Simpson index or Simpson’s diversity index. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Guild: See CQC Guild.

H

Hallucination: Confident, plausible-sounding output from a generative AI tool that is in fact wrong – an invented citation, a misstated fact, a method that does not fit the data. Not a malfunction but a consequence of how the tool works: it predicts likely-sounding text, which usually but not always coincides with the truth. See Generative AI.

Harmonic mean: A type of generalised mean (p = -1). The reciprocal of the average of reciprocals. Appropriate for averaging rates or ratios where denominators vary (e.g., incidents per 1,000 bed-days across providers of different sizes). Gives more weight to smaller values. See Describing Data.

Histogram: A chart that displays the distribution of a quantitative variable by dividing the range of values into bins and drawing a bar for each bin whose height (or area) represents the frequency of values in that range. Unlike a bar chart for categories, bars in a histogram touch because the underlying variable is continuous. The choice of bin width shapes the story the chart tells. See Visualising Data.

Hypothesis test: A procedure that weighs the data against a null hypothesis to ask whether what is seen could be nothing but noise, using the p-value as its measure of surprise. Narrower than estimation, and liable to two kinds of error – a false positive (Type I) and a false negative (Type II). See Statistical Inference and t-tests.

I

I-chart (Individuals chart): A control chart for continuous data where each point represents a single measurement. See SPC Basics.

Indicator: A metric used to measure provider performance (e.g., waiting times, incident rates, quality scores).

Ignorable: Rubin’s term for a missingness mechanism (MAR plus a mild technical condition) that lets you analyse the observed data without modelling the missingness process itself. The label promises convenience, not safety: a mechanism is ignorable only if MAR genuinely holds, which is assumed rather than confirmed. See Missing Data Foundations.

Inverse probability fallacy: Reading “the data would be unlikely if the provider were at the average” as “the provider is unlikely to be at the average” – reversing the conditional that a p-value or z-score actually answers. Converting one probability into the other requires information the test never supplies. The most common misreading of flagged providers. See Statistical Inference and Z-scoring.

IQR (Interquartile Range): The range between the 25th and 75th percentiles (Q3 - Q1). A measure of spread that describes variability without being affected by extreme outliers. Used in outlier detection. See Variation & Uncertainty, Unusual Observations, and Outlier Methods.

K

Kurtosis: Measure of tail heaviness in a distribution. Kurtosis = 3 for normal distribution. Higher kurtosis indicates heavier tails (more extreme values), lower kurtosis indicates lighter tails. Less commonly used than skewness in CQC work. See Distribution Shape.

L

Large language model (LLM): The statistical model inside a generative AI chatbot, trained on a very large quantity of text to predict a likely next word and to generate a response by doing so repeatedly. Fluent by construction; it holds no separate store of true facts and has no built-in way to signal when it is guessing. See Generative AI.

Listwise deletion: See Complete case analysis – the practice of dropping any record with a missing value and analysing only the complete ones. Unbiased under MCAR, but loses precision and can bias the result under MAR or MNAR. See Missing Data Foundations.

M

MAR: (Missing At Random) Missingness related to observed data but not the missing value itself, once those observed variables are accounted for. Example: smaller providers less likely to report, but missingness doesn’t depend on the value being reported. The information needed to correct for it lies in the observed data, but whether data is truly MAR rather than MNAR cannot be tested from the data alone. See Missing Data Foundations.

MCAR: (Missing Completely At Random) Missingness unrelated to any data, observed or unobserved. Rare in practice but the benign case: complete cases are a fair sample, so analysis loses precision but not accuracy. See Missing Data Foundations.

Masking: The way outliers can hide themselves from detection rules built on non-robust statistics: extreme values inflate the mean and standard deviation, dragging a z-score threshold outward until the extremes – and lesser outliers – fall inside it. The reason robust detectors (modified z-score, Tukey’s fences) are preferred for screening. See Outlier Detection Methods.

Mean (Arithmetic mean): A type of generalised mean (p = 1). The sum of values divided by count. The most common measure of central tendency, and one kind of average (see Average) – not the only one. Sensitive to outliers and skewness. Part of the generalised means family. See Describing Data.

Median: The middle value when data is sorted. A quantile – specifically, the 50th percentile (Q2). More robust to outliers than the mean. Works for both quantitative and ordinal data. Part of the quantile family. See Describing Data.

Mode: The most frequently occurring value. The only location measure that works for qualitative (categorical) data. Can be bimodal (two modes) or multimodal (multiple modes). See Describing Data.

Mahalanobis distance: A multivariate measure of how far an observation is from the centre of a distribution, accounting for correlations between variables. Used in multivariate outlier detection. See Outlier Methods.

Modified z-score: A robust alternative to z-scores that uses median and median absolute deviation (MAD) instead of mean and standard deviation. Less influenced by outliers themselves. See Outlier Methods.

MNAR: (Missing Not At Random) Missingness related to the unobserved value itself, even after accounting for observed variables. Example: poor performers don’t report. Creates bias that no routine method can remove, because the correction would need the missing values themselves; in regulation the pattern of missingness is often itself the finding. See Missing Data Foundations and Missing Data Methods.

Missing data: Intended measurements that could not all be obtained – an empty cell or NA. The reason for the absence (the missingness mechanism), not the amount, determines whether it biases an analysis. See Missing Data Foundations.

Missingness mechanism: The process governing which values are missing, classified by what the chance of being absent depends on: nothing (MCAR), observed data (MAR), or the missing value itself (MNAR). The classification, due to Rubin (1976), decides which handling methods are valid. See Missing Data Foundations.

Multiple testing: The problem that arises when running many statistical tests simultaneously – the more tests, the higher the chance of at least one false positive, even when nothing is wrong. For example, running 20 tests at p < 0.05 would expect 1 false positive even under the null. In CQC profiling (z-scoring across many indicators), the standard approach is to raise action thresholds (e.g., require z > 3 or flagging on multiple indicators) rather than applying formal corrections. For high-stakes confirmatory analysis, consider Bonferroni correction or Benjamini–Hochberg (false discovery rate) correction. Raise with the Guild when correction strategy is unclear. See Z-scoring.

Multiple Imputation (MI): Advanced method for handling missing data by creating multiple plausible complete datasets. Requires specialist implementation. See Missing Data Methods.

Maximum likelihood: A model-based way to handle missing data that estimates parameters directly from the observed-data likelihood, without building a filled-in dataset. A principled alternative to multiple imputation under MAR; needs specialist tooling. See Missing Data Foundations.

Microsoft Fabric: CQC’s cloud-based analytical computing environment. Provides R and Python notebooks that run in a browser, integrated storage connected to the Enterprise Data Platform (EDP), and built-in links to Azure DevOps for version control. Operates in two modes: sandbox workspaces for learning and experimentation, and project workspaces for real analytical work. See Analytical Tooling.

N

Normal distribution: A symmetric, bell-shaped distribution. Many statistical methods assume approximate normality. Also called Gaussian distribution.

Normalisation (min–max): Rescaling a measure to a common 0–1 scale so values from different scales can be compared, using \((\text{value} - \text{min}) / (\text{max} - \text{min})\). The move behind normalised entropy (\(H / \log_2 k\)) and the coefficient of variation (which normalises against the mean instead). Sensitive to extremes, since it leans on the minimum and maximum. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Null hypothesis: The position a statistical test provisionally adopts – the ordinary state of affairs, or business as usual, as the world would be if nothing had changed (for example, that a provider’s true rate equals the benchmark). Not “nothing could be interesting” but the default against which a claim is weighed. The p-value measures how surprising the data would be if the null hypothesis were true; it is never the probability that the null hypothesis itself is true. See Statistical Inference and t-tests.

O

Nominal data: Qualitative data with no natural order between categories (e.g., provider type, region, incident category). See Data Types.

Numerical data: See Quantitative data.

Office for Statistics Regulation (OSR): The regulatory arm of the UK Statistics Authority. Responsible for maintaining and enforcing the Code of Practice for Statistics, assessing statistical producers for compliance, and granting or withdrawing the accredited official statistics designation. See QA Principles.

Ordinal data: Qualitative data with a natural order between categories, but unequal distances between them (e.g., CQC ratings: Outstanding > Good > Requires Improvement > Inadequate). See Data Types.

Outlier: A value far from the rest of the data. May be an error, a genuine extreme value, or indicate a different population. No universal definition – context determines what’s “unusual.” See Unusual Observations and Outlier Methods.

Overdispersion: When data shows more variation than expected by the statistical model. Common in count data. Requires adjustment in z-scoring. See Z-scoring.

Overthinking box: Callout boxes (yellow/orange) containing technical details, edge cases, and when to seek specialist support. Optional reading for most analysts.

P

P-chart: A control chart for proportions (e.g., percentage of patients meeting a target). See SPC Basics.

Package: A bundle of additional, ready-made functions written, tested, and published for reuse, extending the built-in capabilities of R or Python. Also called a library. CQC analysts should draw packages from the approved, trusted sources available in Fabric. See Analytical Tooling.

Parameter: A fixed numerical feature of a population – the quantity an estimate aims at, such as the average falls rate across all homes, or one home’s true underlying rate. Unknown in practice, and distinct both from the population itself (the set of units) and from the estimate (the number a sample yields). See Statistical Inference.

Partial pooling: Also called shrinkage. Estimating many units together so each one’s estimate borrows strength from the others – a small, noisy provider estimate is pulled towards the typical pattern of its peers, by an amount the data decide (heavily when its own data are thin, barely at all when they are rich). A principled form of regularisation that tames small-sample volatility, central to Bayesian and multilevel models. See Statistical Inference.

Precision: How certain or repeatable an estimate is, reflected in the width of its confidence interval – distinct from bias, which is whether the estimate is centred on the right value. A smaller sample (from missing data or otherwise) reduces precision without necessarily causing bias: the aim stays true but less steady. See Statistical Inference, Missing Data Foundations and Variation & Uncertainty.

Prompt: The text instruction or question given to a generative AI tool. The output is a predicted continuation of the prompt, so different prompts yield different answers; a prompt is a way to query the tool for input to the analyst’s judgement, not a way to obtain a finished result. See Generative AI.

P-value: The probability of observing data at least as extreme as yours if the null hypothesis were true. Not the probability that the null hypothesis is true, not the probability the result is “due to chance”, and not a measure of effect size or importance. See Statistical Inference and t-tests.

Pearson’s correlation (r): Measures the strength of linear relationships between continuous variables. Assumes normality and is sensitive to outliers. See Correlation.

Penny Dash review: 2024 review of CQC’s analytical quality, which found variable standards and led to the QA Framework. See QA Principles.

Percentile: A type of quantile that divides data into 100 groups. The 90th percentile means 90% of values are below this point. Used for ranking providers and setting thresholds. Part of the quantile family (which includes median, quartiles, deciles). See Describing Data.

Point estimate: A single number computed from a sample as the best guess of an unknown population value (e.g., the sample mean as an estimate of the population mean). Best reported with a confidence interval, which shows how precise it is. See Statistical Inference.

Population: The complete set of cases you want to describe or draw conclusions about (e.g., every provider in a region). When the data in hand is the population, descriptive measures are written with Greek letters – \(\mu\) for the mean, \(\sigma\) for the standard deviation. Contrast with Sample. See Describing Data and Statistical Inference.

Posterior: In Bayesian inference, the updated probability distribution for an unknown quantity after combining a prior with the data. The source of a credible interval and of direct probability statements such as “the chance this provider exceeds the benchmark.” See Statistical Inference.

Power (Statistical power): The probability of detecting a real effect. Depends on sample size, effect size, and significance level. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Prior: In Bayesian inference, what is believed about an unknown quantity before seeing the current data – often drawn from how comparable units behave. Combined with the data’s likelihood to give the posterior. Because results depend on it, a prior must be stated openly and justified, not smuggled in. See Statistical Inference.

Project workspace: A dedicated Fabric workspace configured for a specific analytical use case, with data access (via EDP shortcuts), permissions, and version-control integration. The environment for work that will inform regulatory outputs. Requested through the IT ticket system after discussion with the Practice Lead. See Analytical Tooling.

Provider: A healthcare organisation regulated by CQC (e.g., hospital, care home, GP practice).

Q

QASQA Framework (Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Quality Assurance Framework): CQC’s internal framework for ensuring analytical quality, aligned to the AQuA Book. Structured around five key areas that span the analytical lifecycle – Engagement and Scoping, Design, Analysis, Delivery and Communication, and Fitness for Purpose – each with an exemplifying question and a set of indicators, and each addressed for every piece of quantitative analysis that informs a regulatory decision. See QA Principles.

Qualitative data: Data representing categories or qualities, not quantities. Cannot perform meaningful arithmetic. Two types: Nominal (no order) and Ordinal (ordered categories). Also called categorical data. See Data Types.

Quantile: A family of position-based measures that divide data into equal-sized groups. All quantiles answer “where does this value sit in the distribution?” Types include: percentiles (100 groups), quartiles (4 groups), quintiles (5 groups), deciles (10 groups), and median (50th percentile). Differ only in how finely you divide the data. Work for both quantitative and ordinal data (only require ordering, not arithmetic). Used for ranking and position-based comparisons. See Describing Data.

Quantitative data: Data representing measurable quantities or amounts. Can perform arithmetic operations. Two types: Continuous (any value in range) and Discrete (whole numbers only). Also called numerical data. See Data Types.

Quartile: A type of quantile that divides data into 4 groups. Q1 (25th percentile), Q2 (median/50th percentile), Q3 (75th percentile). Used for creating performance bands and identifying which quarter of the distribution a provider falls into. Part of the quantile family. See Describing Data.

Quartile coefficient of dispersion (QCD): A robust, unit-free measure of spread, \((Q_3 - Q_1)/(Q_3 + Q_1)\), which normalises the IQR against its own centre. A safer choice than the coefficient of variation for skewed numeric data. Needs quantitative data. See Variation & Uncertainty.

R

Random variable: A quantity whose value is determined by a random process and can therefore be described by a probability distribution. In frequentist inference the population parameter is treated as a fixed (if unknown) number, not a random variable; in Bayesian inference it is modelled as a random variable, which is what enables direct probability statements about it. See Statistical Inference.

Range: The spread between the smallest and largest values. The word does double duty: the interval form (“values run from a to b”) needs only an ordering, while the length form, the single number \(\text{max} - \text{min}\), needs quantitative data because it is a subtraction. Pinned entirely to the two extremes, so a single freak value sets it. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Regression to the mean: The statistical phenomenon where extreme measurements tend to be followed by values closer to the average, even without any genuine change in underlying performance. A provider flagged as an outlier in one period may appear improved in the next partly due to regression to the mean rather than real improvement. Particularly relevant when interpreting year-on-year z-score changes. See Z-scoring.

Reproducible Analytical Pipelines (RAP): The standard for analytical work in the public sector, recommended by the AQuA Book and the Government Analytical Function. RAP requires that an analysis can be reproduced in full by running documented code from the same data – no manual steps, no copy-and-paste between tools. The Government Analytical Function describes three maturity levels: Minimum RAP (open-source tooling, version control, peer review), Silver RAP (functions, unit tests, documentation), and Gold RAP (automated deployment and testing). CQC is building towards Minimum RAP. See Analytical Tooling.

Researcher degrees of freedom: The many analytical choices made during analysis – variable selection, outlier handling, time period, transformations – that can collectively influence results even when each individual choice is defensible. Addressed by documenting all decisions and their rationale, running sensitivity analyses for critical choices, and pre-specifying the approach before seeing data for high-stakes work. See QA Principles.

Rethinking box: Callout boxes (blue) providing CQC context, professional judgement guidance, and regulatory considerations.

S

Sample: A subset of cases drawn from a larger population, used to estimate what is true of that population. Quantities computed from a sample are written with Roman letters – \(\bar{x}\) for the mean, \(s\) for the standard deviation – and the standard deviation carries Bessel’s correction (\(n-1\)). Contrast with Population. See Statistical Inference.

Sample size: The number of observations. Larger samples give more precise estimates but don’t guarantee practical importance.

Sampling distribution: The distribution an estimate would trace out if you drew the sample again and again and recomputed it each time. Its spread measures the precision of the estimate, and its standard deviation is the standard error – both of which can be worked out from a single sample. See Statistical Inference.

Sandbox workspace: A Fabric workspace available to all analysts in a D&I function for learning, experimentation, and skill development. Does not connect to EDP data by default. Each function has a dedicated sandbox accessible once permissions are in place. See Analytical Tooling.

Sensitivity analysis: Testing how results change when you vary assumptions or methods. Essential for understanding robustness.

Shannon’s Entropy (H): Measure of diversity or heterogeneity in categorical data. H = 0 when all observations in one category, H = log₂(k) for uniform distribution across k categories. Higher values indicate more diversity. Works for nominal data. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Significance level: The threshold for declaring a result statistically significant (commonly 0.05). Represents acceptable false positive rate.

Single imputation: Filling each missing value with one replacement – the mean, median, mode, or last value carried forward. Quick, but treats a guess as a known fact: it shrinks the standard error and overstates confidence, unlike multiple imputation, which carries the uncertainty through. See Missing Data Foundations.

Simpson’s diversity index: See Gini’s heterogeneity index (the same formula, \(D = 1 - \sum p_i^2\), under a different name).

Simpson’s paradox: The reversal or disappearance of an association when grouped data are pooled (or vice versa) – each provider type can show no staffing–incidents relationship while the combined dataset shows a strong one, because the groups differ on both variables. Check within-group patterns before trusting a pooled correlation. See Correlation.

Skewness: Asymmetry in a distribution. Positive skew has a long right tail; negative skew has a long left tail. See Distribution Shape.

Spearman’s correlation (ρ): Rank-based correlation measure. More robust to outliers and non-linear relationships than Pearson’s r. See Correlation.

Special cause variation: Non-random change in a process that warrants investigation. Contrast with common cause variation. See SPC Basics.

SPC (Statistical Process Control): Methods for monitoring processes over time to detect genuine changes. See SPC Basics.

Standard deviation (SD): A measure of spread that quantifies the average distance of values from the mean. In original units (e.g., hours, incidents). Related to variance by SD = √variance. Used in z-scoring and control charts. Works for quantitative data only. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Standard error (SE): The standard deviation of a sample statistic (e.g., the mean) – the spread of the estimate across hypothetical repeated samples, and so a measure of its precision. Decreases with larger sample size, but only in proportion to its square root. See Statistical Inference.

Standardisation: Transforming data to a common scale for comparison. Z-scoring is a form of standardisation.

Statistical inference: Reasoning from a sample to a conclusion about the population it represents, while quantifying the uncertainty involved. Covers estimation (point estimates and confidence intervals) and hypothesis testing. In regulatory work it guides where to investigate rather than supplying proof. See Statistical Inference.

Sycophancy: The measured tendency of a generative AI tool to tell the user what they appear to want to hear – agreeing, reassuring, endorsing – because agreeable answers were rated favourably when the model was trained. It makes the tool a weak adversary for red-teaming: agreement from it is not evidence. See Generative AI.

T

t-test: A statistical test comparing means between groups or against a target. Appropriate for small samples or when population SD is unknown. See t-tests.

Threshold: A cut-off value for decision-making. This guidance emphasises investigation over mechanical thresholds.

Tukey’s fences: A rule for identifying potential outliers based on the interquartile range (IQR). Values beyond 1.5 × IQR from the first or third quartile are flagged. The rule box plots use. See Unusual Observations and Outlier Methods.

Type I error: False positive. Rejecting a true null hypothesis.

Type II error: False negative. Failing to reject a false null hypothesis.

U

U-chart: A control chart for rates (counts per unit, e.g., incidents per 1000 bed days). See SPC Basics.

Uncertainty: The range of plausible values for a parameter. Quantified by confidence intervals or standard errors. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Unusual observation: A value that stands out from the rest of the data. Preferred term to “outlier” because it acknowledges there’s no universal definition – what’s unusual depends on context, question, and method. See Unusual Observations.

Upset plot: A visualisation for exploring patterns of missingness across multiple variables. Shows which variables are missing together, revealing patterns that simple percentages might miss. See Missing Data Methods.

V

Variable: Any characteristic that can take different values from one case to the next – a provider’s rating, its waiting time, the number of incidents it reported. A variable’s type (quantitative or qualitative; continuous, discrete, ordinal, or nominal) determines which methods are valid. See Data Types.

Variance: A measure of spread equal to the square of the standard deviation (variance = SD²). The mean of the squared deviations from the mean: divide by \(n\) when describing the data in hand (\(\sigma^2\)), or by \(n-1\) when estimating a population from a sample (\(s^2\), see Bessel’s correction). In squared units (e.g., hours², incidents²), making it less interpretable than SD but mathematically convenient for many statistical methods. Works for quantitative data only. See Variation & Uncertainty.

Validation: The process of checking whether analytical results make substantive sense – whether they are plausible, consistent with prior knowledge, and internally coherent. Validation catches errors that are technically correct but wrong in substance (e.g., a perfectly calculated estimate of the wrong quantity). Distinct from verification, which checks technical correctness. Both are needed. See QA Principles.

Variation: Differences between observations. Can be expected (common cause) or signal a change (special cause). Understanding variation is central to statistical thinking. See Variation & Uncertainty and SPC Basics.

Verification: The process of checking whether an analytical calculation is technically correct – catching coding errors, formula mistakes, copy-and-paste errors, and software bugs. Code review, automated testing, and independent replication of key results are all forms of verification. Distinct from validation, which checks whether results make substantive sense. Both are needed. See QA Principles.

Version control: The practice of tracking every change made to code, with a record of who made the change, when, and why. Makes it possible to see what changed between reviewed and final versions of an analysis, recover previous versions if needed, and maintain a complete audit trail. At CQC implemented through Azure Repos linked to Fabric workspaces. See Analytical Tooling.

W

Weighted mean (Weighted average): A type of generalised mean where different values have different importance (weights). Used when aggregating across providers of different sizes or with different precision. Common weights include provider size (bed-days, patient count) for patient-centred metrics, or inverse variance for combining uncertain estimates. Part of the generalised means family. See Describing Data.

Welch’s t-test: The two-sample t-test that estimates each group’s variance separately rather than pooling them, with adjusted degrees of freedom. Costs almost nothing when variances are equal and protects the error rates when they are not, which is why it is the modern default (and R’s). See t-tests.

Winsorising: Limiting the influence of extreme values by replacing them with less extreme ones – for example, setting everything above the 95th percentile to the 95th-percentile value, and everything below the 5th to the 5th. Unlike trimming, it keeps the sample size. A legitimate, established technique – CQC’s z-scoring guidance winsorises extreme z-scores when estimating overdispersion – useful for stabilising an estimate or reshaping data to meet a method’s assumptions. Use it deliberately and declare it: because it alters the very extremes you may need to investigate, it is the wrong tool for deciding which providers are unusual. See Unusual Observations and Z-scoring.

Working z-score: CQC’s alternative to standard z-scoring when data fail the minimum requirements (small counts or denominators, untransformably non-normal values). A p-value is obtained for each provider – from a fitted distribution suited to the data type (Poisson, negative binomial, Beta) or from ranks – and converted to the z-score carrying the same probability under a standard normal distribution. Requires specialist implementation. See Z-scoring.

Z

z-score: A standardised measure of how far an observation is from the expected value, in standard deviation units. CQC’s primary method for provider comparison. See Z-scoring.

z-test: A statistical test that assumes known population standard deviation. At CQC, z-scoring (for provider comparison) is more common than z-tests (for hypothesis testing).

# Glossary {#sec-glossary}

This glossary defines statistical and CQC-specific terms used throughout the guidance. Terms are organised alphabetically. See the relevant sections for fuller explanations and examples.

---

## A

**Assumption checking**: The process of verifying that your data meets the requirements of a statistical method before applying it. See [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd) and [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd) for method-specific assumptions.

**Accredited official statistics**: The highest tier of official statistics in the UK. Accreditation is granted by the Office for Statistics Regulation (OSR) and certifies that a statistical series meets the full standards of the Code of Practice for Statistics. It is not a permanent status: the OSR monitors ongoing compliance and can remove accreditation where standards are not sustained. CQC's accredited statistical series are subject to compliance with all three pillars of the Code. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Accuracy**: How close an estimate is, on average, to the true value it aims at – the absence of bias. Distinct from precision, which is how tightly repeated estimates group regardless of where their centre sits. An estimate can be precise but inaccurate (a tight group, off target) or accurate but imprecise (a loose group, centred on target). See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Alternative hypothesis**: The possibility a statistical test weighs against the null – the scenario under consideration, such as that a provider's true rate differs from the benchmark. A test never proves the alternative; a small p-value is only evidence that something other than business as usual may be at work. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Analyst**: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC's QASQA Framework. The analyst designs and executes the analysis, including the assurance plan, carries out self-assurance, and responds to feedback from the assurer. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Analytical lifecycle**: The complete sequence of stages through which a piece of analytical work passes, from the initial scoping conversation with a commissioner through to delivery and sign-off. The AQuA Book and CQC's QASQA Framework treat quality as a property of the whole cycle, not a gate at the end: problems originate at specific stages and cannot be rescued by later care. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**AQuA Book**: Government guidance on producing robust, fit-for-purpose analysis. The CQC QA Framework aligns with AQuA principles.

**Approver**: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC's QASQA Framework. The approver provides final accountability by scrutinising the work of analyst and assurer and confirming it has been appropriately assured before sign-off. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Assurer**: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC's QASQA Framework. The assurer independently reviews and validates the analyst's work, reviews the analyst's self-assurance, and confirms to the approver that the work is sound. Must be independent from the analyst – not involved in the analysis being reviewed. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Azure DevOps**: The Microsoft suite of development and collaboration services used at CQC, the relevant part of which for analysts is Azure Repos. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Azure Repos**: The repository (version-control) service within Azure DevOps, used at CQC for managing code. Fabric workspaces can be linked directly to Azure Repos, enabling analysts to version-control notebooks and scripts without leaving the Fabric environment. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Average**: An everyday umbrella term for a measure of central tendency – a single value standing in for the typical one. The mean, the median, and the mode are *all* averages. In common use "the average" usually means the arithmetic mean, but this guidance keeps the words apart: *mean* for that calculation (and its family), *median* and *mode* for the other two, and *average* only as the umbrella over all three. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

---

## B

**Bar chart**: A chart that represents the frequency or magnitude of each category in a dataset as a rectangular bar. Used for nominal and ordinal data. When categories have a natural order, bars should be arranged in that order (an ordered bar chart). Touching bars are used for continuous data (histograms); separated bars signal discrete categories. See [Visualising Data](../chapters/02-data-visualisation.qmd).

**Banding**: Grouping providers into performance categories (e.g., significantly above/below average, within expected range) based on statistical analysis. Common in z-scoring applications.

**Bin width**: In a histogram, the range of values each bar spans. Choosing the bin width controls how much detail the chart shows: wide bins smooth over variation; narrow bins reveal local structure but can create noise. Bin width is an analytical choice, not a fact about the data. See [Visualising Data](../chapters/02-data-visualisation.qmd).

**Benjamini–Hochberg correction**: See Multiple testing.

**Bessel's correction**: Dividing by $n-1$ instead of $n$ in the sample variance and standard deviation, which corrects a downward bias when a sample is used to estimate a population's spread. For *describing* the data in hand (treated as the whole population) divide by $n$ and write $\sigma$; the correction belongs to the sample/estimation setting, where the notation switches to $s$. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd) and [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Bias**: Systematic error that makes results consistently wrong in one direction – an estimate whose aim is pulled off the true value, distinct from imprecision (a steady aim that simply scatters). Can arise from missing data (MNAR), measurement error, or inappropriate methods. Unlike imprecision, bias is not reduced by gathering more data and is not revealed by a confidence interval's width. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd) and [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Bonferroni correction**: A multiple testing adjustment that divides the significance level (α) by the number of tests performed (e.g., for 20 tests at α = 0.05, each individual test uses α = 0.0025). Conservative – reduces false positives at the cost of increased false negatives. Rarely applied in CQC routine profiling, where screening rather than confirmatory inference is the goal; instead, raise action thresholds or require flagging on multiple indicators. For formal evaluations, raise with the Guild. See Multiple testing.

---

## C

**C-chart**: A control chart for count data (e.g., number of incidents per month). Assumes constant opportunity for events. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Callout box**: Visual elements in this guidance that provide additional context (Rethinking boxes) or technical details (Overthinking boxes).

**Categorical data**: See Qualitative data.

**Central Limit Theorem (CLT)**: Statistical principle stating that sample means approximate a normal distribution as sample size increases, even if the underlying data isn't normal. Justifies using methods that assume normality for large samples (typically n ≥ 30). See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Code of Practice for Statistics 3.0**: The UK Statistics Authority's framework (Autumn 2025 edition) setting standards for statistics that serve the public good. Organised around three pillars – Trustworthiness (Standards 1–4), Quality (Standards 5–7), and Value (Standards 8–10) – with ten numbered standards. CQC's QA Framework is designed to meet the Quality pillar obligations, particularly Standard 6 (Be rigorous: use suitable data sources and sound methods) and Standard 7 (Be open about quality: explain strengths, limitations and uncertainty). Note: older CoP editions used letter-prefixed codes (Q1, Q2, Q3, V2, etc.) that do not appear in Edition 3.0. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Code review**: The process of having someone not involved in writing an analysis read the code to check for errors, ambiguous logic, and departures from documented decisions. A distinct form of verification that guards against mistakes invisible to the original author. Requires the code to be in a shared repository. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd) and [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Commissioner**: One of the four analytical roles defined in the AQuA Book and adopted by CQC's QASQA Framework. The commissioner defines the analytical need, agrees that the proposed approach will meet requirements before work starts, and accepts the final analysis and assurance. At CQC, commissioners often act on behalf of end-users. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Coefficient of Variation (CV)**: Normalised measure of spread calculated as SD / Mean (often as percentage). Makes variability comparable across different scales or units. Undefined when mean = 0. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Common cause variation**: Random, expected fluctuation in a process. Contrast with special cause variation. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Complete case analysis**: Analysing only observations with no missing data. Simple but can introduce bias if data is not MCAR. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Confidence interval (CI)**: A range of values likely to contain the true parameter. A 95% CI means if we repeated the analysis many times, 95% of intervals would contain the true value. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Continuous data**: Quantitative data that can take any value within a range, including decimals (e.g., waiting times, percentages, rates). See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Credible interval**: The Bayesian counterpart of a confidence interval: a range that, given the data and a stated prior, genuinely has (say) a 95% probability of containing the true value – the interpretation often wrongly attached to a confidence interval. Requires a prior to be declared. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Control chart**: A time-series plot with control limits that signal when a process has changed. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Control limits**: Thresholds on a control chart (typically ±3 standard deviations) that indicate special cause variation. Not the same as specification limits.

**Correlation**: A statistical measure of the relationship between two variables. Ranges from -1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive). See [Correlation](../chapters/15-correlation.qmd).

**Correlation coefficient**: The numerical value (r for Pearson, ρ for Spearman) that quantifies the strength and direction of a correlation. See [Correlation](../chapters/15-correlation.qmd).

**CQC Guild** (Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild): A community space where analysts can ask questions, share approaches, and learn from each other – through Teams channels, drop-in sessions, and wider networking. For complex cases, escalate to the Practice Lead for Quantitative Analytics and Statistics.

**CUSUM** (Cumulative Sum chart): A control chart more sensitive to small persistent shifts than standard Shewhart I-charts. Plots cumulative deviations from a target value. Appropriate for mortality monitoring and other contexts requiring early detection of small changes. Requires specialist implementation – raise with the Guild before use. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

---

## D

**Data type**: The nature of your data. Two main categories: Quantitative (continuous or discrete) and Qualitative (nominal or ordinal). Determines which statistical methods are appropriate. See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Discrete data**: Quantitative data that can only take specific values, typically whole numbers (e.g., number of incidents, bed count). See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Distribution**: The pattern of how values are spread out. Common distributions include normal (bell curve), skewed, and uniform.

---

## E

**Effect size**: The magnitude of a difference or relationship, independent of sample size. Examples include Cohen's d for t-tests. See [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd).

**Empirical rule**: Under a roughly symmetric, bell-shaped distribution, approximately 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean, about 95% within two, and about 99.7% within three. It gives the standard deviation a concrete meaning – a value more than two standard deviations from the centre is roughly a one-in-twenty event under the bell. Heavy-tailed (leptokurtic) distributions exceed these percentages: extreme values arise more often than the rule predicts. See [Distribution Shape](../chapters/05-distribution-shape.qmd) and [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**Estimand**: The unknown quantity an analysis is trying to learn – a fixed feature of the population, such as a provider's true falls rate. Distinguished from the *estimator* (the recipe used to approximate it) and the *estimate* (the number that recipe returns on a given sample). See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Estimate**: A number computed from a sample to stand in for an unknown feature of the population (the parameter). A *point estimate* is a single such value; pairing it with a confidence interval expresses how precise it is. The activity of producing estimates is *estimation*. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Estimator**: The rule or recipe applied to sample data to approximate an estimand – for example, "take the sample mean falls rate." A procedure fixed before the data are seen; the number it returns on a particular sample is the estimate. Its quality is judged by accuracy (bias) and precision. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Ecological fallacy**: Reading a group-level association as if it described individuals – inferring, from areas with higher deprivation having worse average access, that individual deprived patients have worse access. Group-level and individual-level relationships can differ in size and even direction. State the level your analysis applies to. See [Correlation](../chapters/15-correlation.qmd).

**EWMA** (Exponentially Weighted Moving Average chart): A control chart that gives more weight to recent observations, making it sensitive to gradual drifts. Requires specialist implementation – raise with the Guild before use. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Expected value**: The average or predicted value based on a model or national benchmark. Used as the comparison point in z-scoring.

---

## F

**False negative**: Missing a real problem (Type II error). In regulation, failing to identify a poorly performing provider.

**False positive**: Flagging something as a problem when it isn't (Type I error). In regulation, investigating a provider whose performance is actually acceptable.

**Funnel plot**: A scatter plot showing provider performance against sample size, with control limits that widen for smaller samples to reflect the greater uncertainty in small-provider estimates. Providers outside the outer (99.8%) limits are equivalent to those with |z| > 3.09. Preferred over tables of z-scores when presenting results to stakeholders. See [Funnel Plots](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd#sec-funnel-plots).

---

## G

**Generalised mean** (Power mean): A family of averaging methods that includes arithmetic, geometric, and harmonic means as special cases. All are unified by a power parameter *p*. Different values of *p* give different means, each appropriate for different data structures (additive, multiplicative, rates). See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Generative AI**: A family of tools that produce text, code, or images on demand in response to a prompt, typically built on a large language model. Genuinely useful for drafting, explaining, and brainstorming, but it generates fluent output whether or not that output is correct, so every use requires verification and the analyst – never the tool – remains accountable for the work. See [Generative AI](../chapters/11-generative-ai.qmd).

**Geometric mean**: A type of generalised mean (*p* → 0). The *n*th root of the product of *n* values. Appropriate for multiplicative processes, growth rates, and ratios. Less sensitive to extreme values than arithmetic mean. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Gini's heterogeneity index (D)**: A diversity measure for nominal data, $D = 1 - \sum p_i^2$ – the probability that two randomly chosen cases fall in different categories. $D = 0$ when all cases share one category, rising to $(k-1)/k$ for an even split across $k$ categories. The same quantity is also called the *Gini–Simpson index* or *Simpson's diversity index*. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Guild**: See CQC Guild.

---

## H

**Hallucination**: Confident, plausible-sounding output from a generative AI tool that is in fact wrong – an invented citation, a misstated fact, a method that does not fit the data. Not a malfunction but a consequence of how the tool works: it predicts likely-sounding text, which usually but not always coincides with the truth. See [Generative AI](../chapters/11-generative-ai.qmd).

**Harmonic mean**: A type of generalised mean (*p* = -1). The reciprocal of the average of reciprocals. Appropriate for averaging rates or ratios where denominators vary (e.g., incidents per 1,000 bed-days across providers of different sizes). Gives more weight to smaller values. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Histogram**: A chart that displays the distribution of a quantitative variable by dividing the range of values into bins and drawing a bar for each bin whose height (or area) represents the frequency of values in that range. Unlike a bar chart for categories, bars in a histogram touch because the underlying variable is continuous. The choice of bin width shapes the story the chart tells. See [Visualising Data](../chapters/02-data-visualisation.qmd).

**Hypothesis test**: A procedure that weighs the data against a null hypothesis to ask whether what is seen could be nothing but noise, using the p-value as its measure of surprise. Narrower than estimation, and liable to two kinds of error – a false positive (Type I) and a false negative (Type II). See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd) and [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd).

---

## I

**I-chart** (Individuals chart): A control chart for continuous data where each point represents a single measurement. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Indicator**: A metric used to measure provider performance (e.g., waiting times, incident rates, quality scores).

**Ignorable**: Rubin's term for a missingness mechanism (MAR plus a mild technical condition) that lets you analyse the observed data without modelling the missingness process itself. The label promises convenience, not safety: a mechanism is ignorable only if MAR genuinely holds, which is assumed rather than confirmed. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Inverse probability fallacy**: Reading "the data would be unlikely if the provider were at the average" as "the provider is unlikely to be at the average" – reversing the conditional that a p-value or z-score actually answers. Converting one probability into the other requires information the test never supplies. The most common misreading of flagged providers. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd) and [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**IQR** (Interquartile Range): The range between the 25th and 75th percentiles (Q3 - Q1). A measure of spread that describes variability without being affected by extreme outliers. Used in outlier detection. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd), [Unusual Observations](../chapters/06-unusual-observations.qmd), and [Outlier Methods](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

---

## K

**Kurtosis**: Measure of tail heaviness in a distribution. Kurtosis = 3 for normal distribution. Higher kurtosis indicates heavier tails (more extreme values), lower kurtosis indicates lighter tails. Less commonly used than skewness in CQC work. See [Distribution Shape](../chapters/05-distribution-shape.qmd).

---

## L

**Large language model (LLM)**: The statistical model inside a generative AI chatbot, trained on a very large quantity of text to predict a likely next word and to generate a response by doing so repeatedly. Fluent by construction; it holds no separate store of true facts and has no built-in way to signal when it is guessing. See [Generative AI](../chapters/11-generative-ai.qmd).

**Listwise deletion**: See Complete case analysis – the practice of dropping any record with a missing value and analysing only the complete ones. Unbiased under MCAR, but loses precision and can bias the result under MAR or MNAR. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

---

## M

**MAR**: (Missing At Random) Missingness related to observed data but not the missing value itself, once those observed variables are accounted for. Example: smaller providers less likely to report, but missingness doesn't depend on the value being reported. The information needed to correct for it lies in the observed data, but whether data is truly MAR rather than MNAR cannot be tested from the data alone. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**MCAR**: (Missing Completely At Random) Missingness unrelated to any data, observed or unobserved. Rare in practice but the benign case: complete cases are a fair sample, so analysis loses precision but not accuracy. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Masking**: The way outliers can hide themselves from detection rules built on non-robust statistics: extreme values inflate the mean and standard deviation, dragging a z-score threshold outward until the extremes – and lesser outliers – fall inside it. The reason robust detectors (modified z-score, Tukey's fences) are preferred for screening. See [Outlier Detection Methods](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

**Mean** (Arithmetic mean): A type of generalised mean (*p* = 1). The sum of values divided by count. The most common measure of central tendency, and one kind of average (see Average) – not the only one. Sensitive to outliers and skewness. Part of the generalised means family. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Median**: The middle value when data is sorted. A quantile – specifically, the 50th percentile (Q2). More robust to outliers than the mean. Works for both quantitative and ordinal data. Part of the quantile family. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Mode**: The most frequently occurring value. The only location measure that works for qualitative (categorical) data. Can be bimodal (two modes) or multimodal (multiple modes). See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Mahalanobis distance**: A multivariate measure of how far an observation is from the centre of a distribution, accounting for correlations between variables. Used in multivariate outlier detection. See [Outlier Methods](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

**Modified z-score**: A robust alternative to z-scores that uses median and median absolute deviation (MAD) instead of mean and standard deviation. Less influenced by outliers themselves. See [Outlier Methods](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

**MNAR**: (Missing Not At Random) Missingness related to the unobserved value itself, even after accounting for observed variables. Example: poor performers don't report. Creates bias that no routine method can remove, because the correction would need the missing values themselves; in regulation the pattern of missingness is often itself the finding. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd) and [Missing Data Methods](../chapters/17-missing-data-methods.qmd).

**Missing data**: Intended measurements that could not all be obtained – an empty cell or `NA`. The reason for the absence (the missingness mechanism), not the amount, determines whether it biases an analysis. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Missingness mechanism**: The process governing which values are missing, classified by what the chance of being absent depends on: nothing (MCAR), observed data (MAR), or the missing value itself (MNAR). The classification, due to Rubin (1976), decides which handling methods are valid. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Multiple testing**: The problem that arises when running many statistical tests simultaneously – the more tests, the higher the chance of at least one false positive, even when nothing is wrong. For example, running 20 tests at p < 0.05 would expect 1 false positive even under the null. In CQC profiling (z-scoring across many indicators), the standard approach is to raise action thresholds (e.g., require z > 3 or flagging on multiple indicators) rather than applying formal corrections. For high-stakes confirmatory analysis, consider Bonferroni correction or Benjamini–Hochberg (false discovery rate) correction. Raise with the Guild when correction strategy is unclear. See [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**Multiple Imputation (MI)**: Advanced method for handling missing data by creating multiple plausible complete datasets. Requires specialist implementation. See [Missing Data Methods](../chapters/17-missing-data-methods.qmd).

**Maximum likelihood**: A model-based way to handle missing data that estimates parameters directly from the observed-data likelihood, without building a filled-in dataset. A principled alternative to multiple imputation under MAR; needs specialist tooling. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Microsoft Fabric**: CQC's cloud-based analytical computing environment. Provides R and Python notebooks that run in a browser, integrated storage connected to the Enterprise Data Platform (EDP), and built-in links to Azure DevOps for version control. Operates in two modes: sandbox workspaces for learning and experimentation, and project workspaces for real analytical work. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

## N

**Normal distribution**: A symmetric, bell-shaped distribution. Many statistical methods assume approximate normality. Also called Gaussian distribution.

**Normalisation (min–max)**: Rescaling a measure to a common 0–1 scale so values from different scales can be compared, using $(\text{value} - \text{min}) / (\text{max} - \text{min})$. The move behind normalised entropy ($H / \log_2 k$) and the coefficient of variation (which normalises against the mean instead). Sensitive to extremes, since it leans on the minimum and maximum. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Null hypothesis**: The position a statistical test provisionally adopts – the ordinary state of affairs, or business as usual, as the world would be if nothing had changed (for example, that a provider's true rate equals the benchmark). Not "nothing could be interesting" but the default against which a claim is weighed. The p-value measures how surprising the data would be if the null hypothesis were true; it is never the probability that the null hypothesis itself is true. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd) and [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd).

## O

**Nominal data**: Qualitative data with no natural order between categories (e.g., provider type, region, incident category). See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Numerical data**: See Quantitative data.

**Office for Statistics Regulation (OSR)**: The regulatory arm of the UK Statistics Authority. Responsible for maintaining and enforcing the Code of Practice for Statistics, assessing statistical producers for compliance, and granting or withdrawing the accredited official statistics designation. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Ordinal data**: Qualitative data with a natural order between categories, but unequal distances between them (e.g., CQC ratings: Outstanding > Good > Requires Improvement > Inadequate). See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Outlier**: A value far from the rest of the data. May be an error, a genuine extreme value, or indicate a different population. No universal definition – context determines what's "unusual." See [Unusual Observations](../chapters/06-unusual-observations.qmd) and [Outlier Methods](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

**Overdispersion**: When data shows more variation than expected by the statistical model. Common in count data. Requires adjustment in z-scoring. See [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**Overthinking box**: Callout boxes (yellow/orange) containing technical details, edge cases, and when to seek specialist support. Optional reading for most analysts.

## P

**P-chart**: A control chart for proportions (e.g., percentage of patients meeting a target). See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Package**: A bundle of additional, ready-made functions written, tested, and published for reuse, extending the built-in capabilities of R or Python. Also called a library. CQC analysts should draw packages from the approved, trusted sources available in Fabric. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Parameter**: A fixed numerical feature of a population – the quantity an estimate aims at, such as the average falls rate across all homes, or one home's true underlying rate. Unknown in practice, and distinct both from the population itself (the set of units) and from the estimate (the number a sample yields). See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Partial pooling**: Also called *shrinkage*. Estimating many units together so each one's estimate borrows strength from the others – a small, noisy provider estimate is pulled towards the typical pattern of its peers, by an amount the data decide (heavily when its own data are thin, barely at all when they are rich). A principled form of regularisation that tames small-sample volatility, central to Bayesian and multilevel models. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Precision**: How certain or repeatable an estimate is, reflected in the width of its confidence interval – distinct from bias, which is whether the estimate is centred on the right value. A smaller sample (from missing data or otherwise) reduces precision without necessarily causing bias: the aim stays true but less steady. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd), [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd) and [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Prompt**: The text instruction or question given to a generative AI tool. The output is a predicted continuation of the prompt, so different prompts yield different answers; a prompt is a way to query the tool for input to the analyst's judgement, not a way to obtain a finished result. See [Generative AI](../chapters/11-generative-ai.qmd).

**P-value**: The probability of observing data at least as extreme as yours *if the null hypothesis were true*. Not the probability that the null hypothesis is true, not the probability the result is "due to chance", and not a measure of effect size or importance. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd) and [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd).

**Pearson's correlation (r)**: Measures the strength of linear relationships between continuous variables. Assumes normality and is sensitive to outliers. See [Correlation](../chapters/15-correlation.qmd).

**Penny Dash review**: 2024 review of CQC's analytical quality, which found variable standards and led to the QA Framework. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Percentile**: A type of quantile that divides data into 100 groups. The 90th percentile means 90% of values are below this point. Used for ranking providers and setting thresholds. Part of the quantile family (which includes median, quartiles, deciles). See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Point estimate**: A single number computed from a sample as the best guess of an unknown population value (e.g., the sample mean as an estimate of the population mean). Best reported with a confidence interval, which shows how precise it is. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Population**: The complete set of cases you want to describe or draw conclusions about (e.g., every provider in a region). When the data in hand *is* the population, descriptive measures are written with Greek letters – $\mu$ for the mean, $\sigma$ for the standard deviation. Contrast with Sample. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd) and [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Posterior**: In Bayesian inference, the updated probability distribution for an unknown quantity after combining a prior with the data. The source of a credible interval and of direct probability statements such as "the chance this provider exceeds the benchmark." See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Power** (Statistical power): The probability of detecting a real effect. Depends on sample size, effect size, and significance level. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Prior**: In Bayesian inference, what is believed about an unknown quantity before seeing the current data – often drawn from how comparable units behave. Combined with the data's likelihood to give the posterior. Because results depend on it, a prior must be stated openly and justified, not smuggled in. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Project workspace**: A dedicated Fabric workspace configured for a specific analytical use case, with data access (via EDP shortcuts), permissions, and version-control integration. The environment for work that will inform regulatory outputs. Requested through the IT ticket system after discussion with the Practice Lead. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Provider**: A healthcare organisation regulated by CQC (e.g., hospital, care home, GP practice).

## Q

**QASQA Framework** (Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Quality Assurance Framework): CQC's internal framework for ensuring analytical quality, aligned to the AQuA Book. Structured around five key areas that span the analytical lifecycle – Engagement and Scoping, Design, Analysis, Delivery and Communication, and Fitness for Purpose – each with an exemplifying question and a set of indicators, and each addressed for every piece of quantitative analysis that informs a regulatory decision. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Qualitative data**: Data representing categories or qualities, not quantities. Cannot perform meaningful arithmetic. Two types: Nominal (no order) and Ordinal (ordered categories). Also called categorical data. See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Quantile**: A family of position-based measures that divide data into equal-sized groups. All quantiles answer "where does this value sit in the distribution?" Types include: percentiles (100 groups), quartiles (4 groups), quintiles (5 groups), deciles (10 groups), and median (50th percentile). Differ only in how finely you divide the data. Work for both quantitative and ordinal data (only require ordering, not arithmetic). Used for ranking and position-based comparisons. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Quantitative data**: Data representing measurable quantities or amounts. Can perform arithmetic operations. Two types: Continuous (any value in range) and Discrete (whole numbers only). Also called numerical data. See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Quartile**: A type of quantile that divides data into 4 groups. Q1 (25th percentile), Q2 (median/50th percentile), Q3 (75th percentile). Used for creating performance bands and identifying which quarter of the distribution a provider falls into. Part of the quantile family. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Quartile coefficient of dispersion (QCD)**: A robust, unit-free measure of spread, $(Q_3 - Q_1)/(Q_3 + Q_1)$, which normalises the IQR against its own centre. A safer choice than the coefficient of variation for skewed numeric data. Needs quantitative data. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

## R

**Random variable**: A quantity whose value is determined by a random process and can therefore be described by a probability distribution. In frequentist inference the population parameter is treated as a fixed (if unknown) number, not a random variable; in Bayesian inference it is modelled as a random variable, which is what enables direct probability statements about it. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Range**: The spread between the smallest and largest values. The word does double duty: the *interval* form ("values run from a to b") needs only an ordering, while the *length* form, the single number $\text{max} - \text{min}$, needs quantitative data because it is a subtraction. Pinned entirely to the two extremes, so a single freak value sets it. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Regression to the mean**: The statistical phenomenon where extreme measurements tend to be followed by values closer to the average, even without any genuine change in underlying performance. A provider flagged as an outlier in one period may appear improved in the next partly due to regression to the mean rather than real improvement. Particularly relevant when interpreting year-on-year z-score changes. See [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**Reproducible Analytical Pipelines (RAP)**: The standard for analytical work in the public sector, recommended by the AQuA Book and the Government Analytical Function. RAP requires that an analysis can be reproduced in full by running documented code from the same data – no manual steps, no copy-and-paste between tools. The Government Analytical Function describes three maturity levels: Minimum RAP (open-source tooling, version control, peer review), Silver RAP (functions, unit tests, documentation), and Gold RAP (automated deployment and testing). CQC is building towards Minimum RAP. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Researcher degrees of freedom**: The many analytical choices made during analysis – variable selection, outlier handling, time period, transformations – that can collectively influence results even when each individual choice is defensible. Addressed by documenting all decisions and their rationale, running sensitivity analyses for critical choices, and pre-specifying the approach before seeing data for high-stakes work. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Rethinking box**: Callout boxes (blue) providing CQC context, professional judgement guidance, and regulatory considerations.

## S

**Sample**: A subset of cases drawn from a larger population, used to estimate what is true of that population. Quantities computed from a sample are written with Roman letters – $\bar{x}$ for the mean, $s$ for the standard deviation – and the standard deviation carries Bessel's correction ($n-1$). Contrast with Population. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Sample size**: The number of observations. Larger samples give more precise estimates but don't guarantee practical importance.

**Sampling distribution**: The distribution an estimate would trace out if you drew the sample again and again and recomputed it each time. Its spread measures the precision of the estimate, and its standard deviation is the standard error – both of which can be worked out from a single sample. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Sandbox workspace**: A Fabric workspace available to all analysts in a D&I function for learning, experimentation, and skill development. Does not connect to EDP data by default. Each function has a dedicated sandbox accessible once permissions are in place. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

**Sensitivity analysis**: Testing how results change when you vary assumptions or methods. Essential for understanding robustness.

**Shannon's Entropy (H)**: Measure of diversity or heterogeneity in categorical data. H = 0 when all observations in one category, H = log₂(k) for uniform distribution across k categories. Higher values indicate more diversity. Works for nominal data. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Significance level**: The threshold for declaring a result statistically significant (commonly 0.05). Represents acceptable false positive rate.

**Single imputation**: Filling each missing value with one replacement – the mean, median, mode, or last value carried forward. Quick, but treats a guess as a known fact: it shrinks the standard error and overstates confidence, unlike multiple imputation, which carries the uncertainty through. See [Missing Data Foundations](../chapters/08-missing-data-foundations.qmd).

**Simpson's diversity index**: See Gini's heterogeneity index (the same formula, $D = 1 - \sum p_i^2$, under a different name).

**Simpson's paradox**: The reversal or disappearance of an association when grouped data are pooled (or vice versa) – each provider type can show no staffing–incidents relationship while the combined dataset shows a strong one, because the groups differ on both variables. Check within-group patterns before trusting a pooled correlation. See [Correlation](../chapters/15-correlation.qmd).

**Skewness**: Asymmetry in a distribution. Positive skew has a long right tail; negative skew has a long left tail. See [Distribution Shape](../chapters/05-distribution-shape.qmd).

**Spearman's correlation (ρ)**: Rank-based correlation measure. More robust to outliers and non-linear relationships than Pearson's r. See [Correlation](../chapters/15-correlation.qmd).

**Special cause variation**: Non-random change in a process that warrants investigation. Contrast with common cause variation. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**SPC** (Statistical Process Control): Methods for monitoring processes over time to detect genuine changes. See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Standard deviation (SD)**: A measure of spread that quantifies the average distance of values from the mean. In original units (e.g., hours, incidents). Related to variance by SD = √variance. Used in z-scoring and control charts. Works for quantitative data only. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Standard error (SE)**: The standard deviation of a sample statistic (e.g., the mean) – the spread of the estimate across hypothetical repeated samples, and so a measure of its precision. Decreases with larger sample size, but only in proportion to its square root. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Standardisation**: Transforming data to a common scale for comparison. Z-scoring is a form of standardisation.

**Statistical inference**: Reasoning from a sample to a conclusion about the population it represents, while quantifying the uncertainty involved. Covers estimation (point estimates and confidence intervals) and hypothesis testing. In regulatory work it guides where to investigate rather than supplying proof. See [Statistical Inference](../chapters/07-statistical-inference.qmd).

**Sycophancy**: The measured tendency of a generative AI tool to tell the user what they appear to want to hear – agreeing, reassuring, endorsing – because agreeable answers were rated favourably when the model was trained. It makes the tool a weak adversary for red-teaming: agreement from it is not evidence. See [Generative AI](../chapters/11-generative-ai.qmd).

## T

**t-test**: A statistical test comparing means between groups or against a target. Appropriate for small samples or when population SD is unknown. See [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd).

**Threshold**: A cut-off value for decision-making. This guidance emphasises investigation over mechanical thresholds.

**Tukey's fences**: A rule for identifying potential outliers based on the interquartile range (IQR). Values beyond 1.5 × IQR from the first or third quartile are flagged. The rule box plots use. See [Unusual Observations](../chapters/06-unusual-observations.qmd) and [Outlier Methods](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

**Type I error**: False positive. Rejecting a true null hypothesis.

**Type II error**: False negative. Failing to reject a false null hypothesis.

## U

**U-chart**: A control chart for rates (counts per unit, e.g., incidents per 1000 bed days). See [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Uncertainty**: The range of plausible values for a parameter. Quantified by confidence intervals or standard errors. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Unusual observation**: A value that stands out from the rest of the data. Preferred term to "outlier" because it acknowledges there's no universal definition – what's unusual depends on context, question, and method. See [Unusual Observations](../chapters/06-unusual-observations.qmd).

**Upset plot**: A visualisation for exploring patterns of missingness across multiple variables. Shows which variables are missing together, revealing patterns that simple percentages might miss. See [Missing Data Methods](../chapters/17-missing-data-methods.qmd).

## V

**Variable**: Any characteristic that can take different values from one case to the next – a provider's rating, its waiting time, the number of incidents it reported. A variable's type (quantitative or qualitative; continuous, discrete, ordinal, or nominal) determines which methods are valid. See [Data Types](../chapters/01-data-types.qmd).

**Variance**: A measure of spread equal to the square of the standard deviation (variance = SD²). The mean of the squared deviations from the mean: divide by $n$ when describing the data in hand ($\sigma^2$), or by $n-1$ when estimating a population from a sample ($s^2$, see Bessel's correction). In squared units (e.g., hours², incidents²), making it less interpretable than SD but mathematically convenient for many statistical methods. Works for quantitative data only. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd).

**Validation**: The process of checking whether analytical results make substantive sense – whether they are plausible, consistent with prior knowledge, and internally coherent. Validation catches errors that are technically correct but wrong in substance (e.g., a perfectly calculated estimate of the wrong quantity). Distinct from verification, which checks technical correctness. Both are needed. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Variation**: Differences between observations. Can be expected (common cause) or signal a change (special cause). Understanding variation is central to statistical thinking. See [Variation & Uncertainty](../chapters/04-variation-uncertainty.qmd) and [SPC Basics](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd).

**Verification**: The process of checking whether an analytical calculation is technically correct – catching coding errors, formula mistakes, copy-and-paste errors, and software bugs. Code review, automated testing, and independent replication of key results are all forms of verification. Distinct from validation, which checks whether results make substantive sense. Both are needed. See [QA Principles](../chapters/09-qa-principles.qmd).

**Version control**: The practice of tracking every change made to code, with a record of who made the change, when, and why. Makes it possible to see what changed between reviewed and final versions of an analysis, recover previous versions if needed, and maintain a complete audit trail. At CQC implemented through Azure Repos linked to Fabric workspaces. See [Analytical Tooling](../chapters/10-tooling.qmd).

## W

**Weighted mean** (Weighted average): A type of generalised mean where different values have different importance (weights). Used when aggregating across providers of different sizes or with different precision. Common weights include provider size (bed-days, patient count) for patient-centred metrics, or inverse variance for combining uncertain estimates. Part of the generalised means family. See [Describing Data](../chapters/03-describing-data.qmd).

**Welch's t-test**: The two-sample t-test that estimates each group's variance separately rather than pooling them, with adjusted degrees of freedom. Costs almost nothing when variances are equal and protects the error rates when they are not, which is why it is the modern default (and R's). See [t-tests](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd).

**Winsorising**: Limiting the influence of extreme values by replacing them with less extreme ones – for example, setting everything above the 95th percentile to the 95th-percentile value, and everything below the 5th to the 5th. Unlike trimming, it keeps the sample size. A legitimate, established technique – CQC's z-scoring guidance winsorises extreme z-scores when estimating overdispersion – useful for stabilising an estimate or reshaping data to meet a method's assumptions. Use it deliberately and declare it: because it alters the very extremes you may need to investigate, it is the wrong tool for deciding which providers are unusual. See [Unusual Observations](../chapters/06-unusual-observations.qmd) and [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**Working z-score**: CQC's alternative to standard z-scoring when data fail the minimum requirements (small counts or denominators, untransformably non-normal values). A p-value is obtained for each provider – from a fitted distribution suited to the data type (Poisson, negative binomial, Beta) or from ranks – and converted to the z-score carrying the same probability under a standard normal distribution. Requires specialist implementation. See [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

## Z

**z-score**: A standardised measure of how far an observation is from the expected value, in standard deviation units. CQC's primary method for provider comparison. See [Z-scoring](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd).

**z-test**: A statistical test that assumes known population standard deviation. At CQC, z-scoring (for provider comparison) is more common than z-tests (for hypothesis testing).
