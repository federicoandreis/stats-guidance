CQC Internal Resources

Quality Assurance

Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Quality Assurance Framework (2025)
Internal framework for ensuring analytical quality. Aligns with the AQuA Book. Defines roles, responsibilities, and quality standards for quantitative work.
Contact: Practice Leads or federico.andreis@cqc.org.uk

Defines the Guild community’s role in offering a space for statistical questions, sharing approaches, and building analytical capability.
Access: Internal CQC document

Outlines analytical competencies across career levels. Maps skills to training needs.
Access: Internal CQC document

Method-Specific Guidance

Comprehensive internal guidance on standard and working z-scores. Covers data types, minimum requirements, formulas, and trouble-shooting.
Access: Internal CQC document

Z-Score Bandings: What They Tell Us and What They Don’t (Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Practice, 2026)
Methodological note on the correct interpretation of z-score performance bandings: the distinction between observed distance, statistical significance, and practical importance; the inverse probability fallacy; and revised, evidential band labels. Underpins the interpretation guidance in the Z-scoring chapter.
Access: Internal CQC document

SPC Implementation Guidance (if available)
Internal guidance on applying SPC methods at CQC.
Contact: Practice Lead for current resources

Academic and Professional Literature

Z-scoring and Provider Comparison

Spiegelhalter, D. J. (2005). Funnel plots for comparing institutional performance. Statistics in Medicine, 24(8), 1185-1202.
Seminal paper on funnel plots for provider comparison. Explains relationship to z-scoring and overdispersion adjustment.

Spiegelhalter, D., et al. (2012). Statistical methods for healthcare regulation: rating, screening and surveillance. Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series A, 175(1), 1-47.
Comprehensive review of statistical methods for healthcare regulation, including z-scoring, funnel plots, and risk adjustment.

Spiegelhalter, D. J. (2005). Handling over-dispersion of performance indicators. Quality and Safety in Health Care, 14, 347-351.
On why institutional performance data are usually over-dispersed, and how to widen control limits to avoid over-flagging.

Interpreting Significance and Uncertainty

Wasserstein, R. L., & Lazar, N. A. (2016). The ASA statement on p-values: context, process, and purpose. The American Statistician, 70(2), 129-133.
The American Statistical Association’s principles on p-values: significance is not graduated, and a p-value measures neither effect size nor the probability that a hypothesis is true.

Goodman, S. (2008). A dirty dozen: twelve p-value misconceptions. Seminars in Hematology, 45(3), 135-140. Catalogues the most common errors in interpreting p-values, including the inverse probability fallacy the Z-scoring chapter warns against.

Greenland, S., et al. (2016). Statistical tests, P values, confidence intervals, and power: a guide to misinterpretations. European Journal of Epidemiology, 31(4), 337-350. A careful, authoritative catalogue of what p-values, confidence intervals and power do and do not mean. The reference for getting the confidence-interval wording exactly right, behind the Statistical Inference chapter.

Cumming, G. (2014). The new statistics: why and how. Psychological Science, 25(1), 7-29. The case for leading with estimation – effect sizes and confidence intervals – rather than null-hypothesis significance testing. Underpins the estimation-first framing of the Statistical Inference chapter.

Tong, C. (2019). Statistical inference enables bad science; statistical thinking enables good science. The American Statistician, 73(sup1), 246-261. Argues that formal inference is strictly valid only for prespecified (confirmatory) analysis, while most real work is exploratory and better served by description, triangulation and judgement. The basis for the exploratory-versus-confirmatory distinction in the Statistical Inference chapter.

Office for Statistics Regulation. How to communicate uncertainty in statistics.
Conveying uncertainty honestly, including the distinction between “no evidence of a difference” and “evidence of no difference”.
Link: https://osr.statisticsauthority.gov.uk/

How Other Organisations Communicate Variation

NHS England. Summary Hospital-level Mortality Indicator (SHMI).
Uses deliberately neutral language (“higher than expected”, “as expected”, “lower than expected”) with overdispersion-adjusted control limits.
Link: https://digital.nhs.uk/data-and-information/publications/statistical/shmi

Office for Health Improvement and Disparities (OHID). Fingertips RAG rating technical guidance.
Classifies indicator values against confidence intervals and warns explicitly that, even at the 95% level, some flags will be false.

Statistical Process Control

Thor, J., et al. (2007). Application of statistical process control in healthcare improvement: systematic review. Quality and Safety in Health Care, 16(5), 387-399.
Systematic review of SPC in healthcare. Documents benefits, limitations, barriers, and facilitating factors.

Mohammed, M. A., Worthington, P., & Woodall, W. H. (2008). Plotting basic control charts: tutorial notes for healthcare practitioners. Quality and Safety in Health Care, 17(2), 137-145.
Practical tutorial on creating and interpreting control charts in healthcare.

Benneyan, J. C., Lloyd, R. C., & Plsek, P. E. (2003). Statistical quality control methods in infection control and hospital epidemiology, Part I: Introduction and basic theory. Infection Control & Hospital Epidemiology, 24(1), 7-16.
Introduction to SPC methods for healthcare applications, with focus on infection control.

Missing Data

Rubin, D. B. (1976). Inference and missing data. Biometrika, 63(3), 581-592.
The seminal paper introducing the MCAR/MAR/MNAR framework and the notion of an ignorable missingness mechanism. The origin of how the field classifies missing data.

Little, R. J. A., & Rubin, D. B. (2019). Statistical Analysis with Missing Data (3rd ed.). John Wiley & Sons.
Authoritative textbook on missing data theory and methods. Covers MCAR/MAR/MNAR framework and advanced techniques.

Schafer, J. L., & Graham, J. W. (2002). Missing data: our view of the state of the art. Psychological Methods, 7(2), 147-177.
Accessible, widely cited review. Clears up common misconceptions about MAR and explains why the choice of mechanism is a matter of judgement rather than test.

van Buuren, S. (2018). Flexible Imputation of Missing Data (2nd ed.). CRC Press.
Practical guide to multiple imputation with R examples. Accessible introduction to modern missing data methods.

Laaksonen, S. (2018). Survey Methodology and Missing Data: Tools and Techniques for Practitioners. Springer.
Applied perspective on missing data in surveys and administrative data.

Sterne, J. A. C., et al. (2009). Multiple imputation for missing data in epidemiological and clinical research: potential and pitfalls. BMJ, 338, b2393. Practical guidance and a reporting checklist for multiple imputation in health research. The reference point for doing imputation defensibly.

McElreath, R. (2020). Statistical Rethinking: A Bayesian Course with Examples in R and Stan (2nd ed.). CRC Press. Chapter 15, “Missing Data and Other Opportunities”. Develops the causal/generative view of missingness: treat missingness as its own variable on a causal diagram, and impute by treating each missing value as an unknown the model reasons about, carrying its uncertainty through. A complementary lens to Rubin’s three labels.

Thoemmes, F., & Mohan, K. (2015). Graphical representation of missing data problems. Structural Equation Modeling, 22(4), 631-642. An accessible introduction to graphical (m-graph) models for missingness, the Mohan–Pearl framework that generalises MCAR/MAR/MNAR and shows when a target is recoverable despite the gaps.

Methodological Research Hub, Office for National Statistics (2024). How do you solve a problem like missing data? UK Government Analysis Function blog, 21 March 2024. Official-statistics perspective: favours principled imputation over discarding records, while stressing that any method’s success depends on the quality of the data available to inform it. Link: https://analysisfunction.civilservice.gov.uk/blog/how-do-you-solve-a-problem-like-missing-data/
