How to Use This Book

This page orients you to the guidance – what it covers, how it’s built, and how to find what you need. Read it once; after that, the Quick Reference is your day-to-day entry point.

What It Is, and What It Isn’t

This book is practical statistical guidance for CQC analysts, whether you’re working to a deadline or building confidence with quantitative methods. Use it to choose an appropriate method for a regulatory question, execute it correctly, recognise the edge cases that need specialist input, and document your decisions to meet quality assurance (QA) requirements.

It is not a statistics textbook. It won’t teach theory from first principles – external courses do that – nor cover cutting-edge methods that need specialist expertise, nor stand in for software tutorials, which other technical guides can provide. Most importantly, it does not hand you mechanical rules that remove the need for judgement. Statistical decisions in a regulatory context ask you to balance technical correctness, operational feasibility, and stakeholder trust; this guidance helps you make those judgements, but it does not make them for you.

NoteProfessional judgement is the point

You will meet situations where several methods are each defensible but point to different interpretations; where technical correctness pulls against operational feasibility; where stakeholder trust calls for different communication than statistical precision; and where fairness to providers needs different thresholds than significance alone would set. This guidance helps you navigate those trade-offs – explaining CQC’s reasoning, showing methods in regulatory context, and being explicit about the limits of current knowledge – but the call is yours.

This is not a CQC idiosyncrasy. The UK Code of Practice for Statistics rests on three pillars – Trustworthiness, Quality, and Value – and none of them reduces to a formula. Quality means choosing sound methods for the question in front of you; Value means producing something that genuinely informs a decision; Trustworthiness means being honest about limitations and uncertainty. Each is a matter of judgement, exercised well. This guidance is written to help you meet the Code in everyday analytical work, and flags throughout where a particular method engages a particular principle.

When in doubt, document your reasoning and consult. Good judgement comes from experience, and experience comes from making decisions and learning from them.

The Three Layers

You will find boxes alongside the text throughout this guidance that borrow a device from Richard McElreath’s Statistical Rethinking, separating the mechanics of a method from two deeper kinds of engagement with it:

  • Main text – the minimum knowledge you need to apply the method correctly in standard cases.
  • Rethinking boxes (🤔) – the conceptual layer: common misconceptions, what a result does and does not mean, how methods connect to one another, and the reasoning behind CQC’s choices. They are about thinking clearly, not extra difficulty.
  • Overthinking boxes (⚙️) – the technical layer: the mathematics, edge cases, and assumption violations for when the standard approach breaks down.

The main text stands on its own; both kinds of box can be skipped without losing the thread.

Three Ways to Read It

There is no single right way through – it depends on what you need right now.

  • Building confidence? Work through Part I: Foundations in order, including the Rethinking boxes, then move into Core Methods and Worked Examples as your work requires. Return to the Overthinking boxes when you’re ready to go deeper.
  • Under deadline? Go straight to the method via the Quick Reference or a similar Worked Example, skim the main text to refresh your memory, and check the Overthinking boxes only if you hit an edge case.
  • Reviewing or going deep? Read in full, including every Overthinking box, and use the Rethinking boxes to understand why CQC made the methodological choices it did.

How the Guidance Is Organised

The substantive content falls into three parts, with this page and the Quick Reference as orientation up front.

Part I: Foundations covers the ideas the rest of the discipline rests on – how data behaves, how to describe it, how variation and uncertainty work, and what quality assurance demands. It closes with the computational context: the tools CQC analysts use, why the function is moving toward cloud-based scripted analysis, and what you need to get started in Microsoft Fabric. Code examples appear throughout the book from Part I onwards; if you encounter one before reaching that chapter, it tells you what environment to use and how to access it. If the later methods are the tools, Foundations is the understanding that tells you when and why to reach for each one.

Part II: Core Methods is the working toolkit: the techniques CQC analysts reach for most often. Each chapter is self-contained – you don’t need to read them in order, and they lean on the Foundations rather than on each other. Every one carries its quality-assurance scaffolding (what to check before you start, what assumptions to test, what to record) so the audit trail is built as you work, not bolted on afterwards.

Part III: Worked Examples shows the methods in motion: complete analyses on realistic synthetic data, from preparing the data through to reporting it to stakeholders.

The Appendices hold the Glossary and a list of further resources. If a term or acronym is ever unfamiliar, or you suspect it’s being used in a precise sense, look it up there – acronyms are expanded on first use, but the Glossary is the backstop.

Start With Your Question

The most common mistake is to start from the method – “should I use a t-test?” – rather than from the question. Begin instead with what you are actually trying to find out, and the method usually follows.

Suppose you want to know whether a provider’s referral rate is unusually high. Framed that way, the shape of the answer is already visible: you are comparing one provider against all the others, so you need a way to express how far from typical it sits – which can, for example, point to z-scoring. Now change the question slightly: “has this provider’s rate been drifting upward over the past year?” You are no longer comparing against peers but watching a single series over time, which is the territory of statistical process control. Same provider, same data – but a different question leads to a different tool.

So when you’re unsure where to begin, sharpen the question first. Once it’s clear what you want to find out, the Quick Reference maps common analytical questions to the method that fits.

When to Seek Specialist Support

This guidance goes well beyond the bare mechanics – the Rethinking and Overthinking boxes are there precisely to help with the awkward cases you’ll meet in practice, but they can’t cover every possible scenario. Seeking specialist support isn’t an admission that the guidance failed you. It’s a judgement you make when a problem starts to press against the edges of what you know and the tools you feel confident using.

That judgement is the skill worth developing, and the signs are recognisable once you look for them. A method’s stated assumptions don’t hold, and the relevant Overthinking box doesn’t cover your particular violation. You’ve followed the steps correctly, but the results don’t make sense and you can’t see why. You’re working on something genuinely new for CQC, with no established pattern to follow. Or the stakes are simply high enough – a major regulatory decision, a public-facing report – that a second pair of expert eyes is the prudent thing before you commit. None of these is a box to tick; each is a prompt to ask whether you’ve reached the limit of what you can stand behind.

The aim is not to offload the thinking, but to recognise when you’ve done enough of it alone. Whatever your situation, don’t wait until you’re stuck too deep: early consultation prevents wasted effort, and a five-minute conversation at the right moment often saves days.

How to Get Help

The Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) offers a space where analysts can ask questions, share approaches, and learn from each other – through Teams channels, drop-in sessions, and wider networking.

When reaching out, please keep in mind that the Guild is a community, not a delivery team. If your request is time-sensitive, or concerns a complex methodological question requiring formal review, escalate to the Practice Lead for Quantitative Analytics and Statistics, who will help identify the best source of support.
