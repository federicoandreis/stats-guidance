24  Example 6: Ambulance Handover Delays

SPC with Trend Analysis and Seasonality

24.2 Scenario

A hospital trust is monitoring ambulance handover delays (>30 minutes) over 18 months. National guidance states handovers should occur within 15 minutes, with 30 minutes as the maximum acceptable threshold.

Analytical Question: Are handover delays increasing over time? Are there special causes requiring investigation?

Context: This is operational performance monitoring with clear system pressures. The trust needs to understand whether delays are part of common cause variation or indicate special causes requiring intervention.

24.3 Learning Objectives

By completing this example, you will learn how to:

  1. Recognise when a process is not stable enough for standard control limits
  2. See how a trend masks itself: limits computed across a trend inflate until nothing signals
  3. Quantify a trend with regression, and read its uncertainty honestly
  4. Weigh documented events against formal rules when deciding what to investigate
  5. Handle seasonality (winter pressures) as context, not noise
  6. Communicate deteriorating performance without overclaiming what the chart proves
  7. Know when to escalate to trend-aware monitoring methods

Finding: nothing is flagged – no point breaches the limits, not even month 15’s spike to 310. That should surprise you, and the reason is instructive. The centre line has been computed across a rising series, so it sits above the early months and below the late ones; and the month-to-month jumps that feed the moving range include the trend and the spikes themselves, inflating the limits to a width (roughly 98 to 323) that nothing short of a catastrophe would breach. A trending process smears its own signal into the limits meant to detect it. The SPC chapter’s baseline discipline – limits from a stable period only – exists precisely to prevent this, and here no stable period exists.

Finding: the fitted trend is about 4.6 additional delays per month (p ≈ 0.02, r² ≈ 0.28). Read those honestly: the upward drift is statistically discernible, but the month-to-month noise is large – the trend explains barely a quarter of the variation, and 18 points cannot pin its slope down precisely. What the regression does establish is that the process is not stable, which is exactly the condition standard control limits require.

It also gives the spikes their proper context: months 8 and 15 sit about 75 delays above the fitted trend – roughly twice the typical month’s deviation, and the two largest in the series. Neither breaches a formal 3-sigma rule, even after detrending: with this few points and this much noise, the rules have little power, and the spikes inflate the very moving ranges used to set the residual limits. The case for investigating those months rests on the combination of elevated values and documented operational events (an IT failure in month 8, a flu outbreak in month 15) – which is how it should be: rules order attention; they do not monopolise it.

24.10 Step 6: Conclusion and Recommendations

Statistical conclusion: the headline finding is the trend – handover delays are rising by roughly 4 to 5 a month, and the process is not in statistical control. Months 8 and 15 are the largest deviations from that trend and coincide with documented operational events; no formal SPC rule convicts them, and the write-up should say so rather than borrow the chart’s authority for a judgement that actually rests on operational knowledge.

Operational interpretation: a rising background level points at system capacity, not at single bad months; winter (month 12 is the next-highest value) compounds it; and the documented events add spikes on top of an already-deteriorating process. The distinction matters for action: events get incident reviews, trends get capacity planning.

Recommendations:

  1. Immediate: investigate the two documented-event months; review capacity planning and staffing against the rising baseline; prepare for winter peaks.
  2. Monitoring: standard limits cannot work on this series – monitor deviations from the fitted trend, or escalate to trend-aware methods (CUSUM, a detrended chart with a longer run of data) with the Guild; recalculate conventional limits only if the trend is arrested and a stable baseline emerges.
  3. System: address the capacity constraints behind the trend; strengthen winter resilience.

Limitations: - Only 18 months of data – little power for formal rules, and too short to model seasonality properly - Single trust (generalisability unknown) - External factors (regional demand, ambulance service changes) not captured - The trend may not stay linear

24.11 Summary

This example is a deliberate counter-example: SPC applied where its core assumption – a stable baseline – does not hold. The full-series limits flag nothing, not because nothing is wrong but because the trend inflates the limits; the regression, not the chart, establishes the deterioration; and the months worth investigating earn that status from documented events plus elevated values, not from a rule. When a process trends, say what the chart cannot prove, quantify the trend honestly, and escalate to trend-aware monitoring. The QA record below closes the loop.

6. **Communicate deteriorating performance** without overclaiming what the chart proves
7. **Know when to escalate** to trend-aware monitoring methods

## Step 3: Calculate Control Limits (and Watch Them Fail)

**Purpose**: demonstrate what happens when standard limits are computed across a trending series – a deliberate misstep, made to show why it misleads.

---

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex6-calc-limits-r}
# Calculate centre line (mean)
centre_line <- mean(handover_data$delays_over_30min)

# Calculate moving range
mr <- abs(diff(handover_data$delays_over_30min))
mean_mr <- mean(mr)

# Calculate control limits
ucl <- centre_line + 2.66 * mean_mr
lcl <- centre_line - 2.66 * mean_mr

cat("Control Limits (full series):\n")
cat("  Centre Line:", round(centre_line, 1), "\n")
cat("  UCL:", round(ucl, 1), "\n")
cat("  LCL:", round(lcl, 1), "\n")

# Identify points outside limits
handover_data$outside_limits <- handover_data$delays_over_30min > ucl | 
                                 handover_data$delays_over_30min < lcl

cat("\nPoints outside limits:\n")
print(handover_data[handover_data$outside_limits, c("month", "date", "delays_over_30min")])
```

### Python

```{python ex6-calc-limits-py}
# Calculate centre line
centre_line = handover_data['delays_over_30min'].mean()

# Calculate moving range
mr = handover_data['delays_over_30min'].diff().abs().dropna()
mean_mr = mr.mean()

# Calculate control limits
ucl = centre_line + 2.66 * mean_mr
lcl = centre_line - 2.66 * mean_mr

print("Control Limits (full series):")
print(f"  Centre Line: {centre_line:.1f}")
print(f"  UCL: {ucl:.1f}")
print(f"  LCL: {lcl:.1f}")

# Identify points outside limits
handover_data['outside_limits'] = (handover_data['delays_over_30min'] > ucl) | \
                                   (handover_data['delays_over_30min'] < lcl)

print("\nPoints outside limits:")
print(handover_data[handover_data['outside_limits']][['month', 'date', 'delays_over_30min']])
```

:::

**Finding**: *nothing* is flagged – no point breaches the limits, not even month 15's spike to 310. That should surprise you, and the reason is instructive. The centre line has been computed across a rising series, so it sits above the early months and below the late ones; and the month-to-month jumps that feed the moving range include the trend and the spikes themselves, inflating the limits to a width (roughly 98 to 323) that nothing short of a catastrophe would breach. A trending process smears its own signal into the limits meant to detect it. [The SPC chapter's baseline discipline](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd) – limits from a *stable* period only – exists precisely to prevent this, and here no stable period exists.

## Step 5: Assess Trend

**Purpose**: Quantify the increasing trend using regression.

---

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex6-trend-r}
# Linear regression
trend_model <- lm(delays_over_30min ~ month, data = handover_data)
summary(trend_model)

# Extract trend
slope <- coef(trend_model)[2]
intercept <- coef(trend_model)[1]

cat("\nTrend Analysis:\n")
cat("  Slope:", round(slope, 2), "delays per month\n")
cat("  Intercept:", round(intercept, 1), "\n")
cat("  R-squared:", round(summary(trend_model)$r.squared, 3), "\n")

# Predict values
handover_data$predicted <- predict(trend_model)
```

### Python

```{python ex6-trend-py}
# Linear regression
from scipy.stats import linregress

slope, intercept, r_value, p_value, std_err = linregress(
    handover_data['month'], 
    handover_data['delays_over_30min']
)

print("\nTrend Analysis:")
print(f"  Slope: {slope:.2f} delays per month")
print(f"  Intercept: {intercept:.1f}")
print(f"  R-squared: {r_value**2:.3f}")
print(f"  p-value: {p_value:.4f}")

# Predict values
handover_data['predicted'] = intercept + slope * handover_data['month']
```

:::

**Finding**: the fitted trend is about 4.6 additional delays per month (p ≈ 0.02, r² ≈ 0.28). Read those honestly: the upward drift is statistically discernible, but the month-to-month noise is large – the trend explains barely a quarter of the variation, and 18 points cannot pin its slope down precisely. What the regression does establish is that the process is *not stable*, which is exactly the condition standard control limits require.

It also gives the spikes their proper context: months 8 and 15 sit about 75 delays above the fitted trend – roughly twice the typical month's deviation, and the two largest in the series. Neither breaches a formal 3-sigma rule, even after detrending: with this few points and this much noise, the rules have little power, and the spikes inflate the very moving ranges used to set the residual limits. The case for investigating those months rests on the *combination* of elevated values and documented operational events (an IT failure in month 8, a flu outbreak in month 15) – which is how it should be: [rules order attention; they do not monopolise it](../chapters/16-outlier-methods.qmd).

## Step 6: Conclusion and Recommendations

**Statistical conclusion**: the headline finding is the *trend* – handover delays are rising by roughly 4 to 5 a month, and the process is not in statistical control. Months 8 and 15 are the largest deviations from that trend and coincide with documented operational events; no formal SPC rule convicts them, and the write-up should say so rather than borrow the chart's authority for a judgement that actually rests on operational knowledge.

**Operational interpretation**: a rising background level points at system capacity, not at single bad months; winter (month 12 is the next-highest value) compounds it; and the documented events add spikes on top of an already-deteriorating process. The distinction matters for action: events get incident reviews, trends get capacity planning.

**Recommendations**:

1. **Immediate**: investigate the two documented-event months; review capacity planning and staffing against the rising baseline; prepare for winter peaks.
2. **Monitoring**: standard limits cannot work on this series – monitor *deviations from the fitted trend*, or escalate to trend-aware methods (CUSUM, a detrended chart with a longer run of data) with the Guild; recalculate conventional limits only if the trend is arrested and a stable baseline emerges.
3. **System**: address the capacity constraints behind the trend; strengthen winter resilience.

**Limitations**:
- Only 18 months of data – little power for formal rules, and too short to model seasonality properly
- Single trust (generalisability unknown)
- External factors (regional demand, ambulance service changes) not captured
- The trend may not stay linear

---

## Summary

This example is a deliberate counter-example: SPC applied where its core assumption – a stable baseline – does not hold. The full-series limits flag nothing, not because nothing is wrong but because the trend inflates the limits; the regression, not the chart, establishes the deterioration; and the months worth investigating earn that status from documented events plus elevated values, not from a rule. When a process trends, say what the chart cannot prove, quantify the trend honestly, and escalate to trend-aware monitoring. The QA record below closes the loop.
