23  Example 5: Regional A&E Performance

Two-Sample t-test

23.1 Scenario Overview

Method: two-sample (independent) t-test Complexity: Medium Time to complete: 2-3 hours QASQA questions: Q2 (Design); Q3 (Analysis)

23.2 Scenario

You are comparing A&E 4-hour wait performance between two Integrated Care Systems (ICS): North Region ICS and South Region ICS. Each ICS contains 100 hospital trusts. Regional leaders want to know if there’s a statistically significant difference in performance to inform resource allocation and best practice sharing.

Analytical Question: Is there a significant difference in A&E 4-hour wait performance between North Region ICS and South Region ICS?

Context: The NHS target is for 95% of A&E patients to be seen within 4 hours. Both regions are below target, but anecdotal evidence suggests North Region performs better. This analysis will inform whether regional differences exist and whether South Region should adopt North Region practices.

23.3 Learning Objectives

By working through this example, you will learn to:

  1. Understand independent samples design vs paired design
  2. Check assumptions for a two-sample t-test, in order of importance
  3. Apply Welch’s t-test as the default, without a preliminary variance test
  4. Interpret confidence intervals for the difference between means
  5. Consider confounding variables (deprivation) before reading the gap causally
  6. Assess practical vs statistical significance
  7. Communicate findings with appropriate caveats

23.4 Dataset Description

Synthetic Data: data/ae_performance_data.csv

200 hospital trusts (100 per ICS) with the following variables:

Variable Description Type Notes
trust_code Trust identifier String Format: RXX001-RXX200
trust_name Trust name String Synthetic names
ics Integrated Care System String North / South
ae_performance % seen within 4 hours Float 60-85%
monthly_attendances Monthly A&E attendances Integer 5000-25000
deprivation_score Area deprivation (IMD) Float 10-40
beds Hospital beds Integer 200-1000

Data Characteristics

Realistic patterns: - North Region: slightly better performance (mean ~75%) - South Region: slightly worse performance (mean ~72%) - Variation within each region - Deprivation correlation (more deprived = worse performance) - Trust size variation

Data generation: Synthetic data generated using R (set.seed(42)). North Region has 3% better performance on average. Both R and Python load the same CSV file, ensuring identical results.

Assessment: both distributions are consistent with normality and the spreads are nearly identical. Welch’s t-test is the right choice either way – it costs essentially nothing when variances are equal (as here) and protects the error rates when they are not, which is why the t-tests chapter recommends it as the default rather than as a fallback after a variance test.

23.10 Step 6: Conclusion

Statistical conclusion: North performs about 3 percentage points better (95% CI 1.9 to 4.1; p < 0.001; Cohen’s d ≈ 0.7, medium-to-large), the rank-based cross-check agrees, and the gap is not explained by deprivation, which is essentially identical across the two regions.

Practical significance: at a typical trust’s roughly 12,500 monthly attendances, 3 percentage points is about 370 more patients seen within four hours per trust per month – across 100 trusts, tens of thousands of patients. Operationally meaningful by any reading.

Limitations: - Cross-sectional comparison, not causal – “North does something better” is one explanation among several - Other confounders unmeasured (staffing, facilities, case-mix) - The within-region variation dwarfs the between-region gap: many South trusts outperform many North trusts - Both regions sit well below the 95% standard

Recommendations: 1. Investigate what high performers do – in both regions; the within-region spread is the larger signal 2. Treat “adopt North’s practices” as a hypothesis, not a conclusion – the gap is real but its cause is unestablished 3. Consider additional factors – staffing, resources, patient acuity – before any causal story is acted on 4. Monitor the gap over time with control charts – is it widening or narrowing?

## Scenario

You are comparing A&E 4-hour wait performance between two Integrated Care Systems (ICS): North Region ICS and South Region ICS. Each ICS contains 100 hospital trusts. Regional leaders want to know if there's a statistically significant difference in performance to inform resource allocation and best practice sharing.

**Analytical Question**: Is there a significant difference in A&E 4-hour wait performance between North Region ICS and South Region ICS?

**Context**: The NHS target is for 95% of A&E patients to be seen within 4 hours. Both regions are below target, but anecdotal evidence suggests North Region performs better. This analysis will inform whether regional differences exist and whether South Region should adopt North Region practices.

---

## Learning Objectives

By working through this example, you will learn to:

1. **Understand independent samples design** vs paired design
2. **Check assumptions** for a two-sample t-test, in order of importance
3. **Apply Welch's t-test** as the default, without a preliminary variance test
4. **Interpret confidence intervals** for the difference between means
5. **Consider confounding variables** (deprivation) before reading the gap causally
6. **Assess practical vs statistical significance**
7. **Communicate findings** with appropriate caveats

## Step 3: Check Assumptions

**Purpose**: Verify two-sample t-test assumptions.

### Assumption 1: Normality

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

```{r ex5-variance-r}
# Describe the spreads. We use Welch's t-test regardless - running a
# preliminary variance test to choose the t-test distorts the error rates
# the test is supposed to guarantee.
north_perf <- ae_data$ae_performance[ae_data$ics == "North"]
south_perf <- ae_data$ae_performance[ae_data$ics == "South"]
north_sd <- sd(north_perf)
south_sd <- sd(south_perf)

cat("North standard deviation:", round(north_sd, 2), "\n")
cat("South standard deviation:", round(south_sd, 2), "\n")
cat("Ratio:", round(max(north_sd, south_sd) / min(north_sd, south_sd), 2), "\n")
```

### Python

```{python ex5-variance-py}

# preliminary variance test to choose the t-test distorts the error rates

south_sd = south_data.std()
```

:::

**Assessment**: both distributions are consistent with normality and the spreads are nearly identical. Welch's t-test is the right choice either way – it costs essentially nothing when variances are equal (as here) and protects the error rates when they are not, which is why [the t-tests chapter](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd) recommends it as the default rather than as a fallback after a variance test.

## Step 4: Welch Two-Sample t-test

**Purpose**: Test whether the regional difference survives the noise, and estimate its size.

---

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex5-ttest-r}
# Welch two-sample t-test (R's default)
t_result <- t.test(ae_performance ~ ics, data = ae_data)
print(t_result)

# Rank-based cross-check (no normality assumption)
w_result <- wilcox.test(ae_performance ~ ics, data = ae_data, exact = FALSE)
cat("Mann-Whitney U cross-check: p =", format.pval(w_result$p.value), "\n")

# Effect size (Cohen's d)
north_mean <- mean(ae_data$ae_performance[ae_data$ics == "North"])
south_mean <- mean(ae_data$ae_performance[ae_data$ics == "South"])
n_north <- sum(ae_data$ics == "North")
n_south <- sum(ae_data$ics == "South")
pooled_sd <- sqrt(((n_north - 1) * north_sd^2 + (n_south - 1) * south_sd^2) / 
                  (n_north + n_south - 2))
cohens_d <- (north_mean - south_mean) / pooled_sd

cat("\nEffect size (Cohen's d):", round(cohens_d, 2), "\n")
cat("Interpretation:", 
    ifelse(abs(cohens_d) < 0.2, "Negligible",
    ifelse(abs(cohens_d) < 0.5, "Small",
    ifelse(abs(cohens_d) < 0.8, "Medium", "Large"))), "\n")

cat("\nMean difference:", round(north_mean - south_mean, 2), "percentage points\n")
cat("95% CI for difference: [", round(t_result$conf.int[1], 2), ",", 
    round(t_result$conf.int[2], 2), "]\n")
```

### Python

```{python ex5-ttest-py}
# Welch two-sample t-test (equal_var=False)
res = stats.ttest_ind(north_data, south_data, equal_var=False)
t_stat, p_value = res.statistic, res.pvalue

print(f"Welch two-sample t-test results:")
print(f"t-statistic: {t_stat:.3f}")
print(f"p-value: {p_value:.2e}")

# Rank-based cross-check (no normality assumption)
u_stat, u_p = stats.mannwhitneyu(north_data, south_data)
print(f"Mann-Whitney U cross-check: p = {u_p:.2e}")

# Effect size (Cohen's d)
north_mean = north_data.mean()
south_mean = south_data.mean()
pooled_sd = np.sqrt(((len(north_data) - 1) * north_sd**2 + (len(south_data) - 1) * south_sd**2) / 
                    (len(north_data) + len(south_data) - 2))
cohens_d = (north_mean - south_mean) / pooled_sd

print(f"\nEffect size (Cohen's d): {cohens_d:.2f}")
if abs(cohens_d) < 0.2:
    interpretation = "Negligible"
elif abs(cohens_d) < 0.5:
    interpretation = "Small"
elif abs(cohens_d) < 0.8:
    interpretation = "Medium"
else:
    interpretation = "Large"
print(f"Interpretation: {interpretation}")

print(f"\nMean difference: {north_mean - south_mean:.2f} percentage points")

# 95% CI for the difference (from the Welch test itself)
ci = res.confidence_interval()
print(f"95% CI for difference: [{ci.low:.2f}, {ci.high:.2f}]")
```

:::

**Results**: North performs about 3 percentage points better (95% CI roughly 1.9 to 4.1), a clearly non-chance difference (p < 0.001, and the rank-based cross-check agrees). Cohen's d ≈ 0.7 – a medium-to-large standardised effect, though the interval is the more useful number: the data are consistent with a regional gap anywhere from about two points to about four.

## Step 6: Conclusion

**Statistical conclusion**: North performs about 3 percentage points better (95% CI 1.9 to 4.1; p < 0.001; Cohen's d ≈ 0.7, medium-to-large), the rank-based cross-check agrees, and the gap is not explained by deprivation, which is essentially identical across the two regions.

**Practical significance**: at a typical trust's roughly 12,500 monthly attendances, 3 percentage points is about 370 more patients seen within four hours per trust per month – across 100 trusts, tens of thousands of patients. Operationally meaningful by any reading.

**Limitations**:
- Cross-sectional comparison, not causal – "North does something better" is one explanation among several
- Other confounders unmeasured (staffing, facilities, case-mix)
- The within-region variation dwarfs the between-region gap: many South trusts outperform many North trusts
- Both regions sit well below the 95% standard

**Recommendations**:
1. **Investigate what high performers do** – in both regions; the within-region spread is the larger signal
2. **Treat "adopt North's practices" as a hypothesis**, not a conclusion – the gap is real but its cause is unestablished
3. **Consider additional factors** – staffing, resources, patient acuity – before any causal story is acted on
4. **Monitor the gap over time** with [control charts](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd) – is it widening or narrowing?
