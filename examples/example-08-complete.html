26  Example 8: DoLS Application Trends

Analysing Trends Across Local Authorities

26.1 Scenario Overview

Method: Trend and level-shift (interrupted time series) analysis of panel data Complexity: High Time to complete: 3-4 hours QASQA questions: Q3 (Analysis); Q4 (Delivery & Communication)

NoteMethod Reference

This example separates a pre-existing trend from a policy-driven step change – the same separation-of-signals discipline as monitoring trends over time, applied with a simple regression. For the cross-sectional outlier step, see z-scoring. Full interrupted time series modelling (autocorrelation, seasonality, controls) is Guild territory.

26.2 Scenario

CQC is monitoring Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS) application trends across 150 local authorities over 3 years (12 quarters). A policy change in Q7 (2023 Q3) may have affected application rates.

Analytical Question: Are DoLS application rates increasing? Did the policy change affect application patterns? Which local authorities show unusual trends?

Context: This involves panel data (multiple authorities over time) with a known policy intervention point.

26.3 Learning Objectives

By completing this example, you will learn how to:

  1. Analyse panel data (multiple units over time)
  2. Separate a pre-existing trend from a policy step change - and see why a naive before/after comparison conflates them
  3. Identify outlier authorities with unusual patterns
  4. Account for population size in rate calculations
  5. Visualise trends across multiple units
  6. Interpret policy effects in regulatory context
  7. Communicate findings about system-wide changes

26.7 Step 3: Analyse Policy Impact

Purpose: quantify the policy effect without crediting it with the underlying trend.

The tempting analysis is a before/after comparison of means – and on trending data it is wrong in a predictable direction: the rates were rising anyway, so part of any before/after difference is trend dressed up as policy effect. Compute both, and watch the gap.

  • R
  • Python
# Naive before/after comparison (shown to expose its flaw)
pre_policy <- national_trend$rate_per_10k[national_trend$quarter < 7]
post_policy <- national_trend$rate_per_10k[national_trend$quarter >= 7]

# Segmented regression: underlying trend + level shift at the policy change.
# (A slope-change term was also checked and is indistinguishable from zero.)
import numpy as np

X = np.column_stack([
    np.ones(len(national_trend)),
    national_trend['quarter'],
    (national_trend['quarter'] >= 7).astype(int),
])
coef, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(X, national_trend['rate_per_10k'], rcond=None)

print(f"Underlying trend: {coef[1]:.2f} per 10,000 per quarter")
Underlying trend: 1.84 per 10,000 per quarter
print(f"Level shift at policy change: {coef[2]:.1f} per 10,000")
Level shift at policy change: 30.8 per 10,000

Finding: the naive before/after difference is about 42 per 10,000 – but roughly a quarter of that is the underlying trend, which was lifting rates by about 1.8 per 10,000 each quarter regardless of any policy. The segmented regression separates the two: an underlying climb of ~1.8 per quarter, plus a level shift of ~31 per 10,000 at the policy change, with no detectable change in the slope afterwards. The 31, not the 42, is the honest estimate of the policy’s impact. (For a published analysis, a full interrupted time series model – handling the autocorrelation between quarters, seasonality, and possible controls – is Guild territory; the segmented fit here is the right shape of answer, not the final word.)

26.9 Step 5: Conclusion

Statistical conclusion: DoLS application rates were already rising by about 1.8 per 10,000 each quarter before the policy change; the change added a level shift of roughly 31 per 10,000, sustained through the following six quarters with no detectable change in the underlying slope. Seven authorities (of 150) sit more than two standard deviations from the cross-authority mean and warrant a closer look.

Operational interpretation: the step change is consistent with the policy raising awareness or lowering the application threshold – an association at the right moment, not a proven cause, since anything else that changed in the same quarter would produce the same step. The continuing underlying climb predates the policy and will continue to raise demand regardless of it.

Recommendations:

  1. Monitor capacity: the level shift plus the ongoing trend together describe the demand trajectory assessment capacity must match
  2. Investigate the outlier authorities: high and low – an unusually low rate may signal under-application, which in this context is itself a safeguarding concern
  3. Continue monitoring: confirm the post-change slope stays parallel rather than accelerating
  4. Consider confounders: deprivation, demographics, concurrent local policies

Limitations: - The step’s timing implicates the policy but cannot prove it (no control series) - Quarterly national means are autocorrelated – the simple regression’s standard errors are optimistic; a full interrupted time series model is the robust version - Confounders not adjusted; no individual-level data; three years is a short series

26.10 Summary

This example demonstrates panel data with a policy intervention – and the one move that matters most: separating the pre-existing trend from the step change, because a naive before/after comparison hands the policy credit for growth that was happening anyway. Rates standardise the comparison across differently sized authorities; the segmented regression splits trend from shift; and the outlier scan turns 1,800 observations into a short list worth investigating. The QA record below closes the loop.

## Step 3: Analyse Policy Impact

**Purpose**: quantify the policy effect *without* crediting it with the underlying trend.

The tempting analysis is a before/after comparison of means – and on trending data it is wrong in a predictable direction: the rates were rising anyway, so part of any before/after difference is trend dressed up as policy effect. Compute both, and watch the gap.

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex8-policy-impact-r}
# Naive before/after comparison (shown to expose its flaw)
pre_policy <- national_trend$rate_per_10k[national_trend$quarter < 7]
post_policy <- national_trend$rate_per_10k[national_trend$quarter >= 7]

cat("Naive before/after difference:",
    round(mean(post_policy) - mean(pre_policy), 1), "per 10,000\n\n")

# Segmented regression: underlying trend + level shift at the policy change.
# (A slope-change term was also checked and is indistinguishable from zero.)
national_trend$post <- as.integer(national_trend$quarter >= 7)
its_model <- lm(rate_per_10k ~ quarter + post, data = national_trend)
summary(its_model)

cat("\nUnderlying trend:", round(coef(its_model)["quarter"], 2),
    "per 10,000 per quarter\n")
cat("Level shift at policy change:", round(coef(its_model)["post"], 1),
    "per 10,000\n")
```

### Python

```{python ex8-policy-impact-py}
# Naive before/after comparison (shown to expose its flaw)
pre_policy = national_trend[national_trend['quarter'] < 7]['rate_per_10k']
post_policy = national_trend[national_trend['quarter'] >= 7]['rate_per_10k']

print(f"Naive before/after difference: "
      f"{post_policy.mean() - pre_policy.mean():.1f} per 10,000\n")

# Segmented regression: underlying trend + level shift at the policy change.
# (A slope-change term was also checked and is indistinguishable from zero.)
import numpy as np

X = np.column_stack([
    np.ones(len(national_trend)),
    national_trend['quarter'],
    (national_trend['quarter'] >= 7).astype(int),
])
coef, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(X, national_trend['rate_per_10k'], rcond=None)

print(f"Underlying trend: {coef[1]:.2f} per 10,000 per quarter")
print(f"Level shift at policy change: {coef[2]:.1f} per 10,000")
```

:::

**Finding**: the naive before/after difference is about 42 per 10,000 – but roughly a quarter of that is the underlying trend, which was lifting rates by about 1.8 per 10,000 each quarter regardless of any policy. The segmented regression separates the two: an underlying climb of ~1.8 per quarter, plus a level shift of **~31 per 10,000** at the policy change, with no detectable change in the slope afterwards. The 31, not the 42, is the honest estimate of the policy's impact. (For a published analysis, a full interrupted time series model – handling the autocorrelation between quarters, seasonality, and possible controls – is Guild territory; the segmented fit here is the right shape of answer, not the final word.)

## Step 4: Identify Outlier Authorities

**Purpose**: Find authorities with unusual patterns.

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

```{r ex8-outliers-r}
# Calculate mean rate per LA
la_summary <- aggregate(rate_per_10k ~ la_code + la_name, 
                       data = dols_data,

overall_mean <- mean(la_summary$rate_per_10k)
```

```{python ex8-outliers-py}
la_summary = dols_data.groupby(['la_code', 'la_name'])['rate_per_10k'].mean().reset_index()
```

## Step 5: Conclusion

**Statistical conclusion**: DoLS application rates were already rising by about 1.8 per 10,000 each quarter before the policy change; the change added a level shift of roughly 31 per 10,000, sustained through the following six quarters with no detectable change in the underlying slope. Seven authorities (of 150) sit more than two standard deviations from the cross-authority mean and warrant a closer look.

**Operational interpretation**: the step change is consistent with the policy raising awareness or lowering the application threshold – an *association at the right moment*, not a proven cause, since anything else that changed in the same quarter would produce the same step. The continuing underlying climb predates the policy and will continue to raise demand regardless of it.

**Recommendations**:

1. **Monitor capacity**: the level shift plus the ongoing trend together describe the demand trajectory assessment capacity must match
2. **Investigate the outlier authorities**: high *and* low – an unusually low rate may signal under-application, which in this context is itself a safeguarding concern
3. **Continue monitoring**: confirm the post-change slope stays parallel rather than accelerating
4. **Consider confounders**: deprivation, demographics, concurrent local policies

**Limitations**:
- The step's timing implicates the policy but cannot prove it (no control series)
- Quarterly national means are autocorrelated – the simple regression's standard errors are optimistic; a full interrupted time series model is the robust version
- Confounders not adjusted; no individual-level data; three years is a short series
