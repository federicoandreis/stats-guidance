Z-scoring with Skewed Data

21.1 Scenario Overview

Method: z-scoring with transformation Complexity: Medium-High Time to complete: 2-3 hours QASQA questions: Q3 (Analysis); Q5 (Fitness for Purpose)

NoteMethod Reference

For the z-scoring method itself – including the transformation cascade that CQC’s standard method applies to non-normal data, and the Working z-scores that take over when no transformation works – see the chapter on comparing provider performance. For skewness and transformation in general, see distribution shape.

21.2 Scenario

You are analysing wait times from assessment to treatment across 150 community mental health services using the 2024 Community Mental Health Survey. Most services have wait times under 3 months, but some have waits exceeding 6 months, creating a highly skewed distribution.

Analytical Question: Which mental health services have wait times that are statistically significantly worse than the national average, accounting for the skewed distribution?

Context: Mental health wait times are a key quality indicator. The data shows substantial right skew - most services perform well, but a minority have very long waits. Standard z-scoring assumes normality, which may not hold here.

21.3 Learning Objectives

By working through this example, you will learn to:

  1. Identify non-normal distributions through visual and statistical checks
  2. Understand when z-scoring assumptions fail and why it matters
  3. Apply data transformations (log transformation) to reduce skew
  4. Compare approaches: raw z-scores vs transformed z-scores
  5. Interpret results when assumptions are violated
  6. Conduct sensitivity analysis to assess robustness
  7. Communicate findings about skewed data to stakeholders
  8. Document analytical decisions when methods require adaptation

21.4 Dataset Description

Synthetic Data: data/mental_health_wait_times.csv

150 mental health services with the following variables:

Variable Description Type Notes

service_name Service name String Synthetic names

wait_time_days Median wait time (days) Integer 30-250 days
survey_responses Survey respondents Integer 50-300

Data Characteristics

Realistic issues included: - Strong positive skew (right tail) - Most services 60-90 days, some >180 days - No missing data (complete survey) - Variation in survey response rates - Deprivation correlation (more deprived = longer waits)

Data generation: Synthetic data generated using R (set.seed(42) for reproducibility). The data uses log-normal distribution to create realistic right skew. Both R and Python load the same CSV file, ensuring identical results.

21.10 Step 6: Recommendation

Purpose: Document analytical decision and rationale.

Decision: use log-transformed z-scores for the final analysis.

Rationale: 1. Assumption validity: the log transformation brings the data close to the normality z-scoring assumes 2. Even-handed tails: the raw scale can neither recognise genuinely fast services nor avoid over-flagging slow ones; the log scale treats both directions fairly 3. Consistency with CQC’s method: the standard z-scoring method itself searches a transformation cascade (including log) for non-normal continuous data, so transforming is not a departure from the guidance - it is the guidance 4. Interpretability: results can be back-transformed to days for reporting

Caveats: - The log transformation changes the metric - differences become multiplicative (“50% longer”) rather than additive (“30 days longer”) - Services near the threshold may flip between methods; document both flagged lists - If no transformation had worked, the next step would be Working z-scores, not forcing the raw scale

Communication: report wait times in the original scale (days) but use the log-scale z-scores for the statistical comparison.

## Scenario

You are analysing wait times from assessment to treatment across 150 community mental health services using the 2024 Community Mental Health Survey. Most services have wait times under 3 months, but some have waits exceeding 6 months, creating a highly skewed distribution.

**Analytical Question**: Which mental health services have wait times that are statistically significantly worse than the national average, accounting for the skewed distribution?

**Context**: Mental health wait times are a key quality indicator. The data shows substantial right skew - most services perform well, but a minority have very long waits. Standard z-scoring assumes normality, which may not hold here.

## Step 6: Recommendation

**Purpose**: Document analytical decision and rationale.

---

**Decision**: use log-transformed z-scores for the final analysis.

**Rationale**:
1. **Assumption validity**: the log transformation brings the data close to the normality z-scoring assumes
2. **Even-handed tails**: the raw scale can neither recognise genuinely fast services nor avoid over-flagging slow ones; the log scale treats both directions fairly
3. **Consistency with CQC's method**: the [standard z-scoring method](../chapters/12-z-scoring.qmd) itself searches a transformation cascade (including log) for non-normal continuous data, so transforming is not a departure from the guidance - it is the guidance
4. **Interpretability**: results can be back-transformed to days for reporting

**Caveats**:
- The log transformation changes the metric - differences become multiplicative ("50% longer") rather than additive ("30 days longer")
- Services near the threshold may flip between methods; document both flagged lists
- If no transformation had worked, the next step would be Working z-scores, not forcing the raw scale

**Communication**: report wait times in the original scale (days) but use the log-scale z-scores for the statistical comparison.

```{python ex3-final-py}
print(f"Percentage of total: {100 * len(flagged_final) / len(mh_data):.1f}%\n")

# Top 10
print(flagged_final.head(10))
```
