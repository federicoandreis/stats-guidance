Paired t-test with Small Sample

22.1 Scenario Overview

Method: paired t-test Complexity: Medium Time to complete: 2-3 hours QASQA questions: Q2 (Design); Q3 (Analysis)

22.2 Scenario

A medication safety intervention was piloted in 15 care homes over 6 months. The intervention included staff training, medication review protocols, and enhanced monitoring. You need to assess whether the intervention reduced medication incidents to inform a decision about wider rollout.

Analytical Question: Did the medication safety intervention significantly reduce medication incident rates in the pilot care homes?

Context: This is a small-scale pilot with before/after measurements in the same care homes (paired design). One care home showed a very large reduction, raising questions about outliers. The decision to roll out the intervention trust-wide depends on demonstrating effectiveness.

22.3 Learning Objectives

By working through this example, you will learn to:

  1. Understand paired vs independent designs and when each is appropriate
  2. Handle small samples (N=15) and their implications
  3. Check paired t-test assumptions (normality of differences, no outliers)
  4. Identify and handle outliers in small samples
  5. Calculate and interpret effect sizes (Cohen’s d)
  6. Distinguish statistical vs practical significance
  7. Assess power and risk of Type II error
  8. Communicate findings for decision-making with limited evidence

22.4 Dataset Description

Synthetic Data: data/medication_safety_data.csv

15 care homes with before/after measurements:

Variable Description Type Notes
home_id Care home identifier String CH01-CH15
home_name Care home name String Synthetic names
beds Number of beds Integer 20-80 beds
incidents_before Incidents per month (baseline) Float 3-12 incidents
incidents_after Incidents per month (post-intervention) Float 2-10 incidents
difference Change (after - before) Float Negative = improvement

Data Characteristics

Realistic patterns: - Small sample (N=15 care homes) - Paired measurements (same homes before/after) - One home with a strikingly large proportional reduction (about 65%) - Most homes show modest improvement - Some variation in home size

Data generation: Synthetic data generated using R (set.seed(42)). Most homes show reductions of roughly 20-35%; one home (CH03) shows about 65%. Both R and Python load the same CSV file, ensuring identical results.

Assessment: the IQR rule on the differences finds no formal outlier – the largest absolute reductions (CH05 at −2.9, CH03 and CH14 at −2.6) sit inside the whiskers. What stands out about CH03 is its proportional reduction: from 4.0 to 1.4 incidents per month is a 65% drop, against 20–35% almost everywhere else. The same value can be ordinary on one scale and striking on another – which scale matters is a judgement, not a rule.

Decision: no exclusion is warranted, but CH03’s influence is worth quantifying – run the analysis with and without it as a robustness check.

22.10 Step 6: Conclusion and Recommendation

Statistical conclusion: incidents fell by about 1.4 per month per home (95% CI roughly 0.9 to 1.9; p < 0.001), a large standardised effect (d ≈ 1.7), robust to removing the proportionally extreme home and to swapping in a rank-based test. All 15 homes improved.

Practical significance: at the pilot homes’ baseline of about 4.7 incidents per month, a 1.4-incident reduction is roughly 30% – an improvement worth having. Scaled naively across 100 homes that would be about 140 fewer incidents a month, though scaling is exactly where the caveats below bite.

Limitations – the honest reading from the chapter on comparing two groups applies in full:

  • No control group: incidents may have fallen anyway (seasonal patterns, attention effects, regression to the mean if pilot homes were chosen for high rates)
  • Pilot homes were not selected at random, and pilots enjoy enthusiasm a rollout will not carry
  • Small sample (N=15) and a 6-month window limit generalisability and say nothing about sustainability

Recommendation: the pilot has earned a larger, better-designed evaluation – ideally with comparison homes – alongside any phased rollout. Monitor implementation with control charts per home, investigate what drove CH03’s exceptional proportional improvement, and plan a 12-month follow-up.

## Scenario

A medication safety intervention was piloted in 15 care homes over 6 months. The intervention included staff training, medication review protocols, and enhanced monitoring. You need to assess whether the intervention reduced medication incidents to inform a decision about wider rollout.

**Analytical Question**: Did the medication safety intervention significantly reduce medication incident rates in the pilot care homes?

**Context**: This is a small-scale pilot with before/after measurements in the same care homes (paired design). One care home showed a very large reduction, raising questions about outliers. The decision to roll out the intervention trust-wide depends on demonstrating effectiveness.

---

## Learning Objectives

By working through this example, you will learn to:

1. **Understand paired vs independent designs** and when each is appropriate
2. **Handle small samples** (N=15) and their implications
3. **Check paired t-test assumptions** (normality of differences, no outliers)
4. **Identify and handle outliers** in small samples
5. **Calculate and interpret effect sizes** (Cohen's d)
6. **Distinguish statistical vs practical significance** 
7. **Assess power** and risk of Type II error
8. **Communicate findings** for decision-making with limited evidence

## Dataset Description

### Synthetic Data: `data/medication_safety_data.csv`

**15 care homes** with before/after measurements:

| Variable | Description | Type | Notes |
|----------|-------------|------|-------|
| `home_id` | Care home identifier | String | CH01-CH15 |
| `home_name` | Care home name | String | Synthetic names |
| `beds` | Number of beds | Integer | 20-80 beds |
| `incidents_before` | Incidents per month (baseline) | Float | 3-12 incidents |
| `incidents_after` | Incidents per month (post-intervention) | Float | 2-10 incidents |
| `difference` | Change (after - before) | Float | Negative = improvement |

### Data Characteristics

**Realistic patterns**:
- Small sample (N=15 care homes)
- Paired measurements (same homes before/after)
- One home with a strikingly large *proportional* reduction (about 65%)
- Most homes show modest improvement
- Some variation in home size

**Data generation**: Synthetic data generated using R (`set.seed(42)`). Most homes show reductions of roughly 20-35%; one home (CH03) shows about 65%. Both R and Python load the same CSV file, ensuring identical results.

### Assumption 1: Normality of Differences

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex4-normality-r}
#| label: fig-ex4-qq-r
#| fig-cap: "The fifteen differences track the reference line without dramatic departures - with this few points the plot can only rule out gross non-normality, and it does. The Shapiro-Wilk test agrees."
#| fig-alt: "Q-Q plot of the fifteen within-home differences against theoretical normal quantiles. The points follow the diagonal reference line approximately, with mild wobble at both ends and no extreme departures."
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 6

# Q-Q plot of differences (the quantity the paired test assumes is normal)
ggplot(med_data, aes(sample = difference)) +
  stat_qq(colour = "#375071") +
  stat_qq_line(color = "#A83DA4") +
  labs(
    title = "Q-Q plot: normality of the differences",
    x = "Theoretical quantiles",
    y = "Sample quantiles"
  ) +
  theme_cqc()

# Shapiro-Wilk test
shapiro_result <- shapiro.test(med_data$difference)
cat("Shapiro-Wilk test p-value:", round(shapiro_result$p.value, 3), "\n")
cat("Interpretation:", 
    ifelse(shapiro_result$p.value > 0.05, 
           "No evidence against normality (p > 0.05)",
           "Evidence of non-normality (p < 0.05)"), "\n")
```

### Python

```{python ex4-normality-py}

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 6))

ax.get_lines()[0].set_color('#375071')
ax.get_lines()[1].set_color('#A83DA4')
ax.set_title("Q-Q plot: normality of the differences")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# Shapiro-Wilk test
stat, p_value = stats.shapiro(med_data['difference'])
print(f"Shapiro-Wilk test p-value: {p_value:.3f}")
if p_value > 0.05:
    print("Interpretation: No evidence against normality (p > 0.05)")
else:
```

:::

**Assessment**: the IQR rule on the differences finds no formal outlier – the largest absolute reductions (CH05 at −2.9, CH03 and CH14 at −2.6) sit inside the whiskers. What stands out about CH03 is its *proportional* reduction: from 4.0 to 1.4 incidents per month is a 65% drop, against 20–35% almost everywhere else. The same value can be ordinary on one scale and striking on another – [which scale matters is a judgement, not a rule](../chapters/06-unusual-observations.qmd).

**Decision**: no exclusion is warranted, but CH03's influence is worth quantifying – run the analysis with and without it as a robustness check.

## Step 4: Paired t-test (All Homes)

**Purpose**: Test whether the intervention reduced incidents, and estimate by how much.

---

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex4-ttest-with-r}
# Paired t-test (two-sided, as pre-specified; the CI is the headline)
t_result_with <- t.test(med_data$incidents_after,
                        med_data$incidents_before,
                        paired = TRUE)

print(t_result_with)

# Effect size (Cohen's d for paired data)
mean_diff <- mean(med_data$difference)
sd_diff <- sd(med_data$difference)
cohens_d <- mean_diff / sd_diff

cat("\nEffect size (Cohen's d):", round(cohens_d, 2), "\n")
cat("Interpretation:", 
    ifelse(abs(cohens_d) < 0.2, "Negligible",
    ifelse(abs(cohens_d) < 0.5, "Small",
    ifelse(abs(cohens_d) < 0.8, "Medium", "Large"))), "\n")

# 95% CI for mean difference
ci <- t_result_with$conf.int
cat("\n95% CI for mean reduction: [", round(ci[1], 2), ",", round(ci[2], 2), "] incidents\n")
```

### Python

```{python ex4-ttest-with-py}
# Paired t-test (two-sided, as pre-specified; the CI is the headline)
t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(med_data['incidents_after'],
                                  med_data['incidents_before'])

print(f"Paired t-test results:")
print(f"t-statistic: {t_stat:.3f}")
print(f"p-value: {p_value:.6f}")

# Effect size (Cohen's d for paired data)
mean_diff = med_data['difference'].mean()
sd_diff = med_data['difference'].std()
cohens_d = mean_diff / sd_diff

print(f"\nEffect size (Cohen's d): {cohens_d:.2f}")
if abs(cohens_d) < 0.2:
    interpretation = "Negligible"
elif abs(cohens_d) < 0.5:
    interpretation = "Small"
elif abs(cohens_d) < 0.8:
    interpretation = "Medium"
else:
    interpretation = "Large"
print(f"Interpretation: {interpretation}")

# 95% CI for mean difference
ci = stats.t.interval(0.95, len(med_data)-1, 
                      loc=mean_diff, 
                      scale=stats.sem(med_data['difference']))
print(f"\n95% CI for mean reduction: [{ci[0]:.2f}, {ci[1]:.2f}] incidents")
```

:::

**Results (all homes)**: the mean reduction is about 1.4 incidents per month per home (95% CI roughly 0.9 to 1.9), with a very small p-value and a large standardised effect (d ≈ 1.7). Lead with the interval: the data are consistent with anything from a modest one-incident reduction to nearly two.

## Step 5: Sensitivity Analysis

**Purpose**: quantify how much the conclusion leans on CH03, and cross-check the parametric result with a rank-based test.

---

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex4-ttest-without-r}
# Re-run without CH03 (the proportionally extreme home)
med_data_no_ch03 <- med_data[med_data$home_id != "CH03", ]

t_result_without <- t.test(med_data_no_ch03$incidents_after,
                           med_data_no_ch03$incidents_before,
                           paired = TRUE)

# Effect size without CH03
cohens_d_no <- mean(med_data_no_ch03$difference) / sd(med_data_no_ch03$difference)

cat("=== Comparison ===\n")
cat("All homes:    p =", format.pval(t_result_with$p.value),
    "| d =", round(cohens_d, 2), "\n")
cat("Without CH03: p =", format.pval(t_result_without$p.value),
    "| d =", round(cohens_d_no, 2), "\n")

# Rank-based cross-check (no normality assumption)
w_result <- wilcox.test(med_data$incidents_after, med_data$incidents_before,
                        paired = TRUE, exact = FALSE)
cat("\nWilcoxon signed-rank (all homes): p =",
    format.pval(w_result$p.value), "\n")
```

### Python

```{python ex4-ttest-without-py}
# Re-run without CH03 (the proportionally extreme home)
med_data_no_ch03 = med_data[med_data['home_id'] != "CH03"]

t_stat_no, p_value_no = stats.ttest_rel(med_data_no_ch03['incidents_after'],
                                        med_data_no_ch03['incidents_before'])

# Effect size without CH03
cohens_d_no = (med_data_no_ch03['difference'].mean()
               / med_data_no_ch03['difference'].std())

print("=== Comparison ===")
print(f"All homes:    p = {p_value:.6f} | d = {cohens_d:.2f}")
print(f"Without CH03: p = {p_value_no:.6f} | d = {cohens_d_no:.2f}")

# Rank-based cross-check (no normality assumption)
w_stat, w_p = stats.wilcoxon(med_data['incidents_after'],
                             med_data['incidents_before'])
print(f"\nWilcoxon signed-rank (all homes): p = {w_p:.4f}")
```

:::

**Sensitivity analysis**: the conclusion barely moves. Without CH03 the p-value stays far below any conventional threshold and the effect size is essentially unchanged; the rank-based Wilcoxon test, which makes no normality assumption, agrees. The finding does not lean on one home or on one set of assumptions.

## Step 6: Conclusion and Recommendation

**Statistical conclusion**: incidents fell by about 1.4 per month per home (95% CI roughly 0.9 to 1.9; p < 0.001), a large standardised effect (d ≈ 1.7), robust to removing the proportionally extreme home and to swapping in a rank-based test. All 15 homes improved.

**Practical significance**: at the pilot homes' baseline of about 4.7 incidents per month, a 1.4-incident reduction is roughly 30% – an improvement worth having. Scaled naively across 100 homes that would be about 140 fewer incidents a month, though scaling is exactly where the caveats below bite.

**Limitations** – the honest reading from [the chapter on comparing two groups](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd) applies in full:

- No control group: incidents may have fallen anyway (seasonal patterns, attention effects, regression to the mean if pilot homes were chosen for high rates)
- Pilot homes were not selected at random, and pilots enjoy enthusiasm a rollout will not carry
- Small sample (N=15) and a 6-month window limit generalisability and say nothing about sustainability

**Recommendation**: the pilot has earned a larger, better-designed evaluation – ideally with comparison homes – alongside any phased rollout. Monitor implementation with [control charts](../chapters/13-spc-basics.qmd) per home, investigate what drove CH03's exceptional proportional improvement, and plan a 12-month follow-up.
