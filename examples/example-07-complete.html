25  Example 7: Infection Rates in Care Homes

Comparing Rates with Varying Denominators

25.1 Scenario Overview

Method: Multi-group rate comparison (ANOVA – beyond the core methods; shown with Guild-escalation caveats) Complexity: High Time to complete: 3-4 hours QASQA questions: Q2 (Design); Q3 (Analysis)

NoteMethod Reference

This example compares rates across multiple groups. The underlying data is count data with varying denominators – see data types for why that needs rate-based rather than raw-count comparison. For standardising and benchmarking rates, see z-scoring; for comparing a measure between two groups, see t-tests. Comparisons across three or more groups (ANOVA, or chi-squared for counts) go beyond this guidance – the Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) can advise through the Quant Clinic.

25.2 Scenario

CQC is analysing infection rates across 80 care homes using Vivaldi study data. Care homes vary in size (20-100 residents) and quality ratings.

Analytical Question: Do infection rates differ by CQC rating? How should we account for varying home sizes?

Context: This involves count data (infections) with varying denominators (residents). Simple comparisons may be misleading if not properly adjusted for exposure.

25.3 Learning Objectives

By completing this example, you will learn how to:

  1. Work with count data and rates
  2. Account for varying denominators (exposure adjustment)
  3. Compare rates across groups (CQC ratings)
  4. Respect small groups: what a category of two homes can and cannot tell you
  5. Apply a multi-group test with multiplicity-corrected follow-ups
  6. Resist the tidy story: report the comparison the data actually supports
  7. Communicate findings about quality and safety honestly

Finding: the overall test says the four groups do not share one mean rate (p ≈ 0.006) – but the Bonferroni-corrected pairwise comparisons show where that difference actually lives: only Inadequate versus Good is individually significant (p ≈ 0.004), with Inadequate versus Requires Improvement suggestive but uncorroborated (p ≈ 0.09) and every other pair indistinguishable. 25.8 Step 4: Conclusion

Statistical conclusion: Inadequate-rated homes have substantially higher infection rates (mean ≈ 6.0 per 100 residents) than Good-rated homes (≈ 2.7), and that difference survives multiplicity correction. No other pairwise difference does. The data do not show a tidy gradient down the rating scale – Good and Requires Improvement are statistically indistinguishable here, and the Outstanding category (two homes) supports no conclusion in either direction.

Practical significance: roughly 3 extra infections per 100 residents separates Inadequate homes from Good ones – in a 50-resident home, about 1.5 additional infections per period. As a safety signal concentrated in the homes already judged Inadequate, this corroborates the rating rather than revealing something new; its operational use is prioritising infection-control support.

Recommendations: 1. Target infection-control support at Inadequate-rated homes, where the elevated rates are 2. Do not over-read the rest of the scale – this data does not distinguish Good from Requires Improvement on infection rates, and says nothing about Outstanding 3. Monitor rates as a quality indicator, sized fairly by denominators 4. Consider confounders (resident case-mix, staffing levels) before any causal reading

Limitations: - Cross-sectional data (no causality) - Resident case-mix not adjusted - Severely unbalanced groups (2 / 54 / 17 / 7) – the comparison rests almost entirely on Good versus Inadequate - Reporting bias possible (better-run homes may detect and report more infections)

25.9 Summary

This example demonstrates analysis of count data with varying denominators – and the discipline of reporting the comparison the data actually supports. Rates, not raw counts, make different-sized homes comparable; a multi-group test plus corrected pairwise comparisons locates the real difference; and the honest summary names what cannot be concluded (a rating gradient, anything about a two-home category) as plainly as what can. The QA record below closes the loop.

## Step 3: Statistical Comparison

**Purpose**: Test if infection rates differ significantly by CQC rating.

::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}

### R

```{r ex7-test-r}
# ANOVA on rates
anova_result <- aov(infection_rate ~ cqc_rating, data = infection_data)
summary(anova_result)

# Pairwise comparisons
pairwise_result <- pairwise.t.test(infection_data$infection_rate, 
                                   infection_data$cqc_rating,
                                   p.adjust.method = "bonferroni")
print(pairwise_result)

# Mean rates by rating
cat("\nMean infection rates by CQC rating:\n")
tapply(infection_data$infection_rate, infection_data$cqc_rating, mean)
```

### Python

```{python ex7-test-py}
# ANOVA on rates
from scipy.stats import f_oneway

groups = [infection_data[infection_data['cqc_rating'] == rating]['infection_rate'].values 
          for rating in rating_order]

f_stat, p_value = f_oneway(*groups)
print(f"ANOVA F-statistic: {f_stat:.2f}")
print(f"p-value: {p_value:.4f}")

# Mean rates by rating
print("\nMean infection rates by CQC rating:")
print(infection_data.groupby('cqc_rating')['infection_rate'].mean())
```

:::

The corrected pairwise step is the [multiplicity discipline from the t-tests chapter](../chapters/14-t-tests.qmd) in action: six comparisons invite false discoveries, and the correction is what keeps the one real finding from being buried among them.

**Statistical conclusion**: Inadequate-rated homes have substantially higher infection rates (mean ≈ 6.0 per 100 residents) than Good-rated homes (≈ 2.7), and that difference survives multiplicity correction. No other pairwise difference does. The data do *not* show a tidy gradient down the rating scale – Good and Requires Improvement are statistically indistinguishable here, and the Outstanding category (two homes) supports no conclusion in either direction.

**Practical significance**: roughly 3 extra infections per 100 residents separates Inadequate homes from Good ones – in a 50-resident home, about 1.5 additional infections per period. As a safety signal concentrated in the homes already judged Inadequate, this corroborates the rating rather than revealing something new; its operational use is prioritising infection-control support.

**Recommendations**:
1. **Target infection-control support** at Inadequate-rated homes, where the elevated rates are
2. **Do not over-read the rest of the scale** – this data does not distinguish Good from Requires Improvement on infection rates, and says nothing about Outstanding
3. **Monitor rates** as a quality indicator, sized fairly by denominators
4. **Consider confounders** (resident case-mix, staffing levels) before any causal reading

**Limitations**:
- Cross-sectional data (no causality)
- Resident case-mix not adjusted
- Severely unbalanced groups (2 / 54 / 17 / 7) – the comparison rests almost entirely on Good versus Inadequate
- Reporting bias possible (better-run homes may detect and report more infections)

---

## Summary

This example demonstrates analysis of count data with varying denominators – and the discipline of reporting the comparison the data actually supports. Rates, not raw counts, make different-sized homes comparable; a multi-group test plus corrected pairwise comparisons locates the real difference; and the honest summary names what cannot be concluded (a rating gradient, anything about a two-home category) as plainly as what can.
