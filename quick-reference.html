…then let your data type guide the specifics

The same goal might call for different tools depending on what you are measuring. Ensure you have a clear understanding of the nature of your data before jumping into analysis.

Category Data type What it looks like CQC examples Typical methods
Quantitative Continuous Any value within a range, including decimals Waiting times, length of stay, rates per 1,000 bed days, staff-to-patient ratios Mean & SD; z-scoring; t-tests; Pearson correlation; I-charts
Discrete (counts) Whole-number counts of events Number of incidents, complaints, staff, or beds z-scoring with caution (Poisson); C- and U-charts
Discrete (proportions) A share of a total, derived from counts (%) % of patients seen within target, share of cases rated Good z-scoring; P-charts; tests of proportions
Qualitative Nominal Unordered categories Provider type, region, service type, incident category Counts & cross-tabulations; chi-squared tests of association
Ordinal Ordered categories with unequal gaps CQC ratings, severity levels, Likert agreement scales Median & IQR; Spearman correlation; rank-based tests

Some methods named here that this guidance doesn’t yet cover in depth – tests of proportions, rank-based tests, chi-squared, and ANOVA for three or more groups – are well-established and on the roadmap. The Guild can point you to an approach in the meantime.

The Shape of an Analysis

It’s tempting to read analysis as a pipeline – pick a method, run it, report it – but in practice it’s a loop. A typical path runs question → check the data → choose and run a method → interpret in context → document, but the honest version has arrows pointing backwards too. The data often sends you back to the question (what you wanted to measure isn’t quite what was collected); interpretation sends you back to the method (an assumption doesn’t hold); and the whole process shapes your documentation.

So treat these as a rhythm, not a checklist:

  • Question – sharpen what you’re actually trying to find out before reaching for a method.
  • Data – know your data type, check data quality, look at the distribution and summary statistics, and check for unusual values and missingness.
  • Method – follow the relevant chapter, and check its assumptions before trusting the output.
  • Interpret – read results in context: distance, statistical significance, and practical importance are different things, and significance is never the whole story.
  • Document – record methods, assumptions, limitations, and interpretation as you go, not afterwards (see QA Principles).

Expect to revisit earlier steps. Going back is not a sign you did it wrong; it’s how analysis actually works.
