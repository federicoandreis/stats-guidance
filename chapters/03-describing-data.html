3  Central Tendency and Position

TipLearning outcomes

After reading this chapter you will be able to:

  • read the level of measurement a variable sits at, and tell which summaries are valid for it;
  • compute and interpret the mode, the median and quantiles, and the mean;
  • choose the right kind of mean – arithmetic, geometric, harmonic, weighted – for additive data, rates, and growth;
  • choose a measure by the question rather than by habit, judge when a robust summary serves better, and remember that location always needs pairing with spread.

3.1 Where Is the Middle?

Before you can compare providers or flag an outlier, you have to boil a variable down to a number or two: where does it sit, what is typical? Is the typical waiting time 2 hours or 20? Is the average incident rate 5 a month or 50? That looks like one question with one answer. It is not.

How much you can say depends on how high your data sits on the ladder of measurement. On the bottom rung you can only say what is most common; add ordering and you can speak of a middle; add arithmetic and you can take a mean. So this chapter climbs the ladder rung by rung – the mode, then the median and its quantile family, then the mean – defining each measure and working it through on a handful of real numbers along the way. The arithmetic stays light; the code and the heavier methods come in Part II.

One word needs pinning down first, because everyday speech blurs it. An average is any single value standing in for the typical one – and there are several, so the mode, the median, and the mean are all averages, one for each rung. A mean is one particular kind (and even “the mean” is a small family: arithmetic, geometric, harmonic, …). In conversation “the average” almost always means the arithmetic mean, but this book keeps the words apart: mean for that calculation, median and mode for the other two, and average only as the umbrella over all three.

A note on symbols, while we are pinning down words. When the numbers in front of you are the whole story – every provider in a region this month – you are simply describing them, and this book writes such descriptions with Greek letters: \(\mu\) (mu) for a mean, and \(\sigma\) (sigma, in the next chapter) for a standard deviation. When instead the data is only a sample standing in for a larger population you cannot see in full, the convention switches to Roman letters – \(\bar{x}\) for the sample mean, \(s\) for its standard deviation – and a small correction enters the arithmetic. Throughout the foundations we take the first view, describing the data in hand; the switch to samples, and why it changes the sums, is the business of Statistical Inference. (The count stays \(n\) throughout.)

3.2 Counting: The Mode

The one summary you can always compute is the plainest: which value turns up most often. The mode asks what is most common?, and it asks nothing of the data but the ability to tell categories apart – so it is the only location measure that works on nominal data, where there is no order to exploit and no arithmetic to do. There is nothing to calculate: you count how often each value occurs and report the most frequent one. Take last month’s incident counts at five care homes – 2, 3, 3, 4, 18 – and the mode is 3, the only value that occurs more than once. The most common CQC rating (“Good”) is a mode too.

Its limits are real: the mode need not be unique (a dataset can be bimodal or multimodal, with two or more values tied), it uses little of the data, and for continuous measurements it depends on how you bin (group) the values. For quantitative metrics it rarely leads. But for qualitative outcomes – ratings, compliance status, risk category – it is often the only honest summary of “typical”, and it has one trick the grander measures lack.

Note🤔 Rethinking: When the average describes no one

Ask for the centre of a two-humped distribution and the mean will dutifully hand back a single number sitting in the valley between the humps – an “average” that matches no actual case. Picture provider staffing ratios that fall into two clusters, small rural homes and large urban ones: the mean ratio describes a provider that does not exist, and the median is little better. The mode – modes, plural – is the one summary that says there are two populations here, stop averaging them.

That is why the mode earns a place of its own rather than trailing along behind the mean and median: it is the measure that can tell you the centre is the wrong question. When you find two modes, the usual next move is to split the data and describe each group on its own terms.

3.3 Ordering: The Median and Quantiles

Climb one rung – to data you can put in order – and a richer summary unlocks. A quantile names a position in the sorted data: the value with a given fraction of the data below it. Because that needs only ordering and no arithmetic, quantiles reach onto the ordinal rung, where means cannot follow. You can read the median CQC rating (Inadequate < Requires Improvement < Good < Outstanding) even though a “mean rating” is meaningless.

The median is the most-used quantile of all – the value with half the data below and half above. To find it, sort the values and take the middle one. With an odd number of values that is a single observation; with an even number it is the arithmetic mean of the two middle ones. Return to the five homes – 2, 3, 3, 4, 18, already in order – and with five values the middle one is the third: the median is 3.

The wider family is just the same idea at different resolutions: divide the sorted data into equal-sized groups. Percentiles make 100 groups, vigintiles 20, deciles 10, quintiles 5, quartiles 4 (Q1, Q2, Q3 at the 25th, 50th, and 75th). The median is simply the 50th percentile, the second quartile, the fifth decile – the same point named at different resolutions.

The median earns its keep because it is robust: one monstrous value barely moves it, so when a few values lie far from the rest it often describes the typical experience better than the mean. “Half of providers have shorter waits, half longer” is also easy to say and hard to misread.

Beyond the median, the wider family is what lets you rank and set thresholds. “This provider is in the top 10% for incident rates” lands with a non-technical audience far better than “1.5 standard deviations above the mean”, and “inspect everyone above the 95th percentile” is a clear, defensible rule. Because quantiles assume nothing about the shape of the distribution, they stay meaningful whether the data is normal, lopsided, or lumpy – and, again, they work on ordinal data where means cannot go.

Note🤔 Rethinking: Percentiles aren’t percentages

“This provider is at the 90th percentile” does not mean 90% of their patients experience some outcome. It means the provider ranks above 90% of other providers. The percentile is a position in the distribution of providers, not a rate within any one of them.

A provider at the 90th percentile for waiting times might have a mean wait of 4 hours – the percentile says 90% of providers have shorter mean waits, and tells you nothing about the spread of waits inside that provider, where some patients wait 2 hours and some 6. For regulatory communication, spell it out: “this provider’s incident rate is higher than 90% of similar providers,” not “this provider is at the 90th percentile.”

3.4 Arithmetic: The Mean

At the top rung, where the gaps between values are real numbers, arithmetic unlocks the most powerful summaries in the book – and the most demanding. The mean uses every value, which is what makes it informative and also what makes it fragile: a single extreme value drags it, and it needs genuinely quantitative data beneath it.

And the mean itself is not one thing – several calculations share the name, each combining numbers differently to suit a different kind of data. Write the values as \(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\), where \(n\) is how many there are, and give each value a weight \(w_i\) when some should count for more than others. The symbol \(\sum\) means “add up over all the values” and \(\prod\) “multiply them all together”. Then:

  • Arithmetic mean \(\displaystyle \mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\) – add the values up and divide by the count.
  • Geometric mean \(\displaystyle \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} x_i\right)^{1/n}\) – multiply the values together and take the \(n\)th root (it needs positive values).
  • Harmonic mean \(\displaystyle \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} 1/x_i}\) – divide the count by the sum of the reciprocals.
  • Weighted (arithmetic) mean \(\displaystyle \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}\) – multiply each value by its weight, add those up, and divide by the total weight.

On the five homes – 2, 3, 3, 4, 18 – the arithmetic mean is \((2+3+3+4+18)/5 = 6\). Notice that the mean (6) sits well above the mode and median (both 3): the single home with 18 incidents has pulled it up, while the median has not budged. That one comparison is the whole case for robustness, and it takes no special vocabulary to see.

The other means are for data the arithmetic mean handles badly. Three annual growth factors of 1.05, 1.10 and 1.03 multiply to 1.189, whose cube root, 1.059, is the growth rate that compounds correctly across the three years – the arithmetic mean would overstate it. And for rates built on different denominators – incidents per bed-day across a 100-bed and a 5-bed home – the harmonic mean respects those denominators, where a plain mean of the two rates would not. The table says which to reach for:

Which mean to reach for
Mean Best for CQC example
Arithmetic Additive quantities, roughly symmetric data Mean waiting time, incident counts
Geometric Multiplicative processes, growth rates, ratios Year-on-year percentage changes
Harmonic Rates whose denominators vary Incidents per 1,000 bed-days across providers of different sizes
Weighted arithmetic Aggregating across different sizes or precisions National benchmark weighted by provider size

Whose mean? Weighting and why it matters

Any mean can be weighted so some observations count for more than others, and the choice is rarely neutral. An unweighted national mean treats every provider the same and answers “what is typical for a provider?” – the right lens for fairness across providers. Weighting by size lets a 100-bed home count for ten times a 10-bed home and answers a different question, “what is typical for a patient?” – the right lens for system-level and resource questions. (Weights can also reflect precision, so more reliable estimates count for more, or recency, so newer data counts for more.) Neither is more correct; they answer different questions, so pick the one that matches your purpose and say which you used.

This matters most where providers differ wildly in size. A hospital with 10,000 admissions and 500 readmissions (5%) carries far more information than a unit with 20 admissions and 2 readmissions (10%); the plain arithmetic mean of the two rates is 7.5%, but that treats them as equals, where a weighted or harmonic mean respects how much each one actually tells you. A good habit is to compute more than one: if the arithmetic and harmonic means diverge sharply, that is itself a finding – your denominators are heterogeneous, and you must decide, deliberately, which mean answers your question.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: The generalised mean

The arithmetic, geometric, and harmonic means are not three unrelated tricks; they are one formula seen at three settings. The generalised mean (or power mean) is

\[M_p(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^{\,p} \right)^{1/p}\]

that is: raise each value to a power p, take the ordinary mean, then take the pth root. The familiar means are particular values of p: \(p = 1\) gives the arithmetic mean, \(p \to 0\) the geometric, \(p = -1\) the harmonic, and \(p = 2\) the quadratic mean (root mean square). Larger p leans towards the big values, smaller p towards the small ones.

Weighting is a separate dial layered on top: replace the simple mean with a weighted one (\(w_i\) on each \(x_i\)) and you can have a weighted arithmetic, geometric, or harmonic mean. Every mean you have ever used is some choice of these two dials – a combination rule (p) and an importance rule (w). For CQC work you almost never touch p directly: additive data wants arithmetic, multiplicative data geometric, rates with varying denominators harmonic. A power mean with p outside {−1, 0, 1, 2}, or a weighted geometric or harmonic mean, is worth raising with the Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) – valid, but in need of justifying.

When the mean is fragile: robust alternatives

The mean’s habit of using every value is also its weakness: a single extreme case drags it, as the 18 just did. When that worries you, three robust options keep the description honest:

  • the median you have already met – it cares only how many values lie on each side, not how far the extremes reach;
  • a trimmed mean drops a fixed fraction from each end before averaging – a 10% trimmed mean discards the lowest and highest 10% of values, then takes the arithmetic mean of what remains;
  • a winsorised mean keeps every observation but caps the extremes, replacing the lowest and highest few with the nearest surviving value before averaging.

For most CQC work, reporting the mean and the median together and explaining any gap between them is clearer than reaching for these – and a large gap is a finding worth stating plainly (the formal language for that lopsidedness, skew, comes in Distribution Shape). For a high-stakes output, or when extreme values need investigating rather than taming, take it to the Guild and to Outlier Methods.

3.5 Choosing the Right Measure

It is tempting to read the ladder as “always climb as high as you can”. That is the wrong lesson. The ladder is a gate, not a ranking: it tells you which measures are valid – you cannot take a mean of nominal categories – not which is best. Once a measure is permitted, the question decides, and a lower-rung measure can be the better choice on higher-rung data.

What each rung makes available
If your data is… the valid summaries are…
Nominal (categories) the mode
Ordinal (ordered) the mode, plus the median and quantiles
Quantitative (numbers) the mode, the median and quantiles, plus the mean

For quantitative data – the top rung – all three are on the table, and which serves you best depends on the question. Reach for the mean when every value should count and when the result will feed further arithmetic (standard deviations, z-scores, tests). Reach for the median when extremes would distort the mean, or when “half are above, half below” communicates more honestly. Reach for the mode when the single most common value is the point – the typical rating, the usual length of stay. Often the right answer is more than one: reporting the mean alongside the median says more than either alone, precisely because the gap between them is informative.

Audience nudges you along the same lines: technical readers are comfortable with means, standard deviations, and confidence intervals; non-technical readers follow medians and percentiles more easily; and regulatory communication often shows both – the median for the typical experience, the mean for the system-level picture.

3.6 Position Isn’t Everything

Everything so far describes where the data sits. But location is only half a description. Two providers can share a mean incident rate of 10 a month while one runs steadily at 9–11 and the other lurches between 0 and 20 – same centre, completely different stories. The missing half is spread, and it is the essential complement to location: turn to Variation and Uncertainty next, and to Distribution Shape to see how the shape of a distribution decides whether the mean or the median tells the truth.

## Choosing the Right Measure

It is tempting to read the ladder as "always climb as high as you can". That is the wrong lesson. The ladder is a **gate, not a ranking**: it tells you which measures are *valid* – you cannot take a mean of nominal categories – not which is *best*. Once a measure is permitted, the question decides, and a lower-rung measure can be the better choice on higher-rung data.

| If your data is… | the valid summaries are… |
|---|---|
| **Nominal** (categories) | the mode |
| **Ordinal** (ordered) | the mode, plus the median and quantiles |
| **Quantitative** (numbers) | the mode, the median and quantiles, plus the mean |

: What each rung makes available {tbl-colwidths="[42,58]"}

For quantitative data – the top rung – all three are on the table, and which serves you best depends on the question. Reach for the **mean** when every value should count and when the result will feed further arithmetic (standard deviations, z-scores, tests). Reach for the **median** when extremes would distort the mean, or when "half are above, half below" communicates more honestly. Reach for the **mode** when the single most common value is the point – the typical rating, the usual length of stay. Often the right answer is *more than one*: reporting the mean alongside the median says more than either alone, precisely because the gap between them is informative.

Audience nudges you along the same lines: technical readers are comfortable with means, standard deviations, and confidence intervals; non-technical readers follow medians and percentiles more easily; and regulatory communication often shows both – the median for the typical experience, the mean for the system-level picture.

## Position Isn't Everything

Everything so far describes *where* the data sits. But location is only half a description. Two providers can share a mean incident rate of 10 a month while one runs steadily at 9–11 and the other lurches between 0 and 20 – same centre, completely different stories. The missing half is **spread**, and it is the essential complement to location: turn to [Variation and Uncertainty](04-variation-uncertainty.qmd) next, and to [Distribution Shape](05-distribution-shape.qmd) to see how the shape of a distribution decides whether the mean or the median tells the truth.
