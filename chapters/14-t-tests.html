14  Comparing Two Groups: t-tests

TipWhat this is for

You need to decide whether two means genuinely differ – or whether one mean differs from a target. For example:

  • Did patient experience scores improve in the practices that ran a care coordination pilot, compared with those that did not?
  • Has mean length of stay changed after a discharge intervention, comparing the same wards before and after?
  • Is a trust’s mean handover time different from the 15-minute standard?

A t-test weighs the difference you observed against the difference random variation could plausibly produce, and returns the verdict together with an interval for how large the true difference might be.

14.1 One Idea, Three Flavours

Strip away the variants and every t-test computes the same quantity:

\[t = \frac{\text{estimate} - \text{null value}}{\text{standard error of the estimate}}\]

The numerator is the signal – how far what you observed sits from business as usual. The denominator is the noise – the standard error, how much that estimate would wobble from sample to sample. Their ratio says how many standard errors of noise the signal spans: a t near zero is unremarkable, a t of 3 means the observed difference is three times the wobble that chance alone produces, which chance rarely manages. Because the noise is estimated from the sample rather than known, the ratio is compared against Student’s t distribution – a bell curve with heavier tails than the normal, the extra tail-weight being the honest price of estimating the spread from limited data. As samples grow the penalty fades and t becomes the normal distribution; with ten observations it matters.

What changes between the flavours is only which estimate goes in the numerator:

The three t-test flavours share one logic
Flavour Question Estimate Standard error Degrees of freedom
One-sample Does this group’s mean differ from a target \(\mu_0\)? \(\bar{x} - \mu_0\) \(s/\sqrt{n}\) \(n - 1\)
Two-sample (independent) Do two separate groups differ? \(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2\) combined from both groups’ spreads from both group sizes
Paired Did the same units change between two measurements? \(\bar{d}\), the mean within-pair difference \(s_d/\sqrt{n}\) \(n - 1\) pairs

Notation here is the sample notation of the chapter on statistical inference – \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\), divisor \(n-1\) – because a t-test is inference through and through: the groups in hand stand for the larger reality you are reasoning about.

The choice between the last two flavours is about design, not preference. Pairing applies when the same units appear in both measurements – the same wards before and after, the same practices in two winters. Differencing within each pair cancels everything stable about the unit (size, case-mix, culture), so the comparison rides only on what changed, which usually buys substantial precision. Treating genuinely paired data as independent throws that precision away; treating independent groups as paired is simply invalid. Match the test to how the data arose.

Is a t-test the right tool at all?

At CQC the honest answer is often no – not because t-tests are weak, but because the routine regulatory questions have different shapes. Comparing many providers against a national picture is z-scoring and funnel plot territory: t-tests handle exactly two groups, and running one per provider both asks the wrong question and multiplies false alarms. Watching one provider or system over time belongs to statistical process control. The t-test’s natural CQC habitat is the deliberate two-group question: a pilot evaluated against controls, a before/after comparison with no ongoing stream, a thematic analysis contrasting two defined groups of services. If that is your question, read on.

14.2 Before You Start

Data requirements. A continuous outcome (quantitative data, at the top of the measurement ladder); two defined groups, or one group plus a meaningful target; and enough observations to estimate a spread – below about five per group the test technically runs but tells you little. Complete the usual data-quality checks first.

Independence is the assumption that kills. Each observation must be one independent unit. The CQC trap is clustering: if you compare patient-level scores between two groups of practices, the patients within a practice resemble each other, the effective sample is far smaller than the patient count, and the test’s certainty is fictitious. The simple, safe unit for a t-test is the provider: analyse practice-level means and let \(n\) be the number of practices. Patient-level comparisons across clustered groups need multilevel methods – Guild territory.

When a t-test is not appropriate. More than two groups (ANOVA – raise with the Guild); heavily skewed outcomes with small samples (transform first, or use the rank-based alternatives in the Overthinking box); categorical outcomes (different methods entirely); paired and independent structure mixed together (Guild).

QA framework alignment. t-tests most directly support QA question 3 (Analysis – is the method appropriate and correctly applied?) and, in pilot evaluations, question 5 (Fitness for Purpose – does the comparison answer the right question?). Document the flavour chosen and why, the assumption checks, and every comparison you ran – not only the significant ones.

Worked examples for this chapter. Example 4: Medication Safety Pilot is a paired before/after evaluation; Example 5: Regional A&E Performance is a two-sample comparison with assumption checking.

14.3 Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: State the hypotheses before touching the data

The null hypothesis is business as usual, exactly as the chapter on statistical inference frames it: the pilot made no difference, the two regions share one true mean, the trust meets the standard. The alternative is the possibility you are entertaining. State them – and the direction, if you genuinely have one – before looking at the outcomes, because a hypothesis written after peeking inherits the peek’s bias.

Step 2: Check the assumptions, in order of importance

Independence first, because no later step can repair it: one unit, one observation, no clustering, no repeated measures smuggled into an “independent” comparison.

Normality second, with proportion: the test assumes the relevant quantity – the observations, or for a paired test the within-pair differences – is roughly normal. Look at a histogram or Q-Q plot rather than leaning on a formal test (Shapiro–Wilk flags trivial departures in large samples and misses real ones in small samples, which is exactly backwards for your needs). With more than 30 or so observations per group, the Central Limit Theorem makes the test robust to moderate non-normality; with ten, heavy skew or wild outliers genuinely distort it.

Equal variances you can largely retire as a worry: use Welch’s t-test, which estimates each group’s spread separately and adjusts the degrees of freedom. It is R’s default for good reason – it costs almost nothing when the variances happen to be equal and protects you when they do not. Do not run a preliminary variance test to decide which t-test to use; the two-stage routine distorts the very error rates it is meant to guard.

Step 3: Run the test

One line in either language:

  • R: t.test(group1, group2) – Welch by default; paired = TRUE for paired; mu = target with a single vector for one-sample.
  • Python: scipy.stats.ttest_ind(group1, group2, equal_var=False); ttest_rel for paired; ttest_1samp for one-sample.

Step 4: Report the estimate first, the verdict second

Lead with the difference in means and its confidence interval, in the indicator’s own units – that is the finding. The t-statistic, degrees of freedom and p-value follow as supporting evidence, with the p-value read as graded surprise, not proof. The interval in the software output is 95 per cent by convention and by default; remember the level is a dial someone chose, not a law of nature. And translate: “scores were 6.8 points higher (95 per cent CI 1.9 to 11.8)” tells a decision-maker something; “p = 0.011” alone does not.

14.4 Effect Sizes: Answering “How Large?”

A p-value answers “could this be noise?” and nothing else. The question a regulator actually acts on – is this difference big enough to matter? – needs an effect size: a measure of magnitude that does not change just because you collected more data.

The most useful effect size is usually the plainest: the difference in means, in original units, with its confidence interval. “6.8 points on a 100-point experience score” can be weighed against costs, baselines, and what a point of experience score means for people using services; no further statistics needed.

The standardised companion is Cohen’s d: the difference in means divided by the pooled standard deviation,

\[d = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{s_p}\]

which expresses the gap in units of how much practices ordinarily differ from each other. Its value is comparability – d can be compared across indicators with different units, which is why evaluation literatures speak it. Conventional labels exist (0.2 small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large), and they are the most over-trusted numbers in applied statistics: Cohen offered them reluctantly, for use when nothing better was available, and context beats them every time. A d of 0.2 on mortality matters more than a d of 0.8 on a survey item nobody acts on.

Effect sizes also carry the power lesson in reverse. A small pilot can only ever detect large effects – with ten units a group, a real but modest improvement will routinely fail to reach significance. So a non-significant pilot has usually told you “this study could not see effects of plausible size”, not “there is no effect”. If a pilot’s verdict will drive investment, size the study for the smallest effect worth detecting before collecting data – a power calculation, and a conversation for the Guild.

Note🤔 Rethinking: the verdict trap in pilot evaluations

Pilot evaluations invite a binary reading – “did it work, yes or no?” – and the t-test seems to oblige, p-value as gavel. Both halves of that reading mislead.

A significant pilot has shown that something moved the numbers in the pilot sites. Whether it was the intervention is a separate question the test cannot answer: pilot sites are rarely chosen at random, they enjoy attention, enthusiasm, and resources that a rollout will not carry (and improvement driven by being watched has its own name, the Hawthorne effect), and a site selected because it was struggling will drift back towards ordinary regardless – the same regression to the mean that haunts provider flags. A significant t-test is evidence the pilot sites improved; corroboration is what turns it into evidence the programme works.

A non-significant pilot has shown even less. With small samples the test is weak, so “no significant difference” is usually a statement about the pilot’s size, not the programme’s value – absence of evidence, not evidence of absence. The honest summary of a small null pilot is the confidence interval: “the data are consistent with effects anywhere from −1 to +9 points” keeps the genuine uncertainty visible, where “the pilot found no effect” quietly buries it. Decisions can still be made under that uncertainty; they should just be made knowing it is there.

14.5 Worked Example: Care Coordination Pilot

Scenario: ten GP practices ran a care coordination pilot for a year; ten comparable practices did not. The outcome is each practice’s mean patient experience score (0–100) from the year-end survey. Did experience improve in the pilot practices?

Note the unit of analysis: the practice, not the patient. Each practice contributes one number – its mean score – so the twenty observations are independent and a t-test is legitimate. Comparing the thousands of underlying patient responses directly would ignore the clustering of patients within practices and overstate the evidence.

Interpret

Lead with the estimate: pilot practices scored about 6.8 points higher on average, with a 95 per cent confidence interval from roughly 1.9 to 11.8 points. The interval excludes zero – equivalently, the test is significant (t ≈ 2.95, p ≈ 0.011) – so the difference is unlikely to be noise. But read the interval honestly: the data are consistent with anything from a modest 2-point gap to a substantial 12-point one. Cohen’s d ≈ 1.3 sounds impressive, yet with ten practices a side it is estimated as imprecisely as everything else here.

Then temper the causal reading, as the Rethinking box above argues: this establishes that pilot practices ended the year scoring higher, not that the programme caused it. Were the pilot sites selected at random, or did enthusiastic practices volunteer? Did they differ at baseline? A 6.8-point difference with this much honest context is a good case for a larger, better-designed evaluation – which is usually exactly what a pilot should earn.

14.6 Many Comparisons: the Multiplicity Problem

Run one test at the conventional threshold and a true null has a 5 per cent chance of a false alarm. Run twenty – twenty indicators, twenty subgroups, twenty regions – and the chance that at least one comes up “significant” by luck alone is about 64 per cent. Multiplicity is how diligent, well-intentioned analysis manufactures findings out of noise, and two-group comparisons are especially prone to it because each one is so easy to run.

The defences, in the order to reach for them:

  • Pre-specify. Name the primary comparison before touching the data; everything else is exploratory and labelled as such. This is the cheapest and strongest defence.
  • Report everything. All comparisons run, not just the survivors – a “significant” result means little without the denominator of attempts behind it.
  • Correct formally where verdicts matter. The Bonferroni correction divides the significance threshold by the number of tests (20 tests → 0.05/20 = 0.0025 each): simple, defensible, and conservative – with many tests it can drown real effects. The Benjamini–Hochberg procedure controls the false discovery rate instead – the expected share of flagged results that are false – which suits screening contexts where some false alarms are tolerable but a flood is not. Choosing between them, and counting what the “family” of tests even is, is worth a Guild conversation when the stakes are real.

In routine profiling across many indicators, recall the corroboration posture from z-scoring: require signals to agree across sources before acting. Formal corrections are for the designed, verdict-bearing analyses – evaluations, published comparisons – where a stated error rate is part of the claim.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: when the assumptions fail

Non-normal data. With small samples and visible skew, two routes exist. A transformation (log is the workhorse for right-skewed times and rates) often restores enough symmetry, at the price of interpreting results on the transformed scale – “a 15 per cent multiplicative difference” rather than “3 days”. Or switch to the rank-based alternatives: Mann–Whitney U for two independent groups, Wilcoxon signed-rank for paired data. They trade a little power for robustness, and note they test a subtly different hypothesis – whether one group’s values tend to exceed the other’s, not whether means differ – which is often closer to the regulatory question anyway.

Outliers. A single wild value can swamp a ten-observation t-test. Do not delete it to rescue significance: investigate it, report results with and without it, and if the two disagree, say so – that disagreement is the finding.

Clustered data. No alternative test fixes broken independence. Patients within practices, residents within homes, repeated months within a provider – these need the right unit of analysis (aggregate to one value per cluster, as the worked example does) or multilevel models (Guild). A t-test on clustered observations is not conservative or approximate; it is wrong, and wrong in the flattering direction.

Equivalence is not a failed difference test. “No significant difference” never demonstrates that two groups are the same – the absence-of-evidence trap again. If sameness is the actual claim (a cheaper service performs no worse, say), that is equivalence or non-inferiority testing, which reverses the burden of proof and needs its own design. Raise it with the Guild before promising anyone a “no difference” conclusion.

14.7 Documenting Your Analysis

For QA purposes, record: the question and the two groups (or target), with the unit of analysis stated; the flavour of test and why; the assumption checks and what they showed; every comparison run, including the non-significant ones; the estimate with its confidence interval, the test statistics, and the effect size; and the practical interpretation, including the limits on any causal reading. The Code of Practice’s sound-methods obligations are satisfied by exactly this trail – and a pilot evaluation that may drive investment decisions deserves nothing less.

14.8 Related Approaches

  • Comparing many providers at once → z-scoring and funnel plots; running a t-test per provider is the multiplicity problem in costume.
  • Following one provider or system over time → statistical process control; a before/after question with an ongoing data stream is usually better answered by a chart with frozen baseline limits.
  • Relationships between two continuous variables → correlation.
  • Foundations → statistical inference for p-values, intervals, and the error trade-off; distribution shape for skewness and transformation.
  • Complete workflows → Example 4: Medication Safety Pilot (paired) and Example 5: Regional A&E Performance (two-sample).

14.9 When to Seek Support from the Guild

Raise your analysis with the Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) at a Quant Clinic session when: you need more than two groups (ANOVA), adjustment for confounders (regression), or repeated measures (multilevel models); the data are clustered and aggregation to one value per cluster loses the question; you need a power calculation before a pilot; the claim is equivalence rather than difference; the comparisons multiply and you need a correction strategy; or assumptions fail and you are unsure which alternative answers the question actually asked.

The simple, safe unit for a t-test is the provider: analyse practice-level means and let $n$ be the number of practices. Patient-level comparisons across clustered groups need multilevel methods – Guild territory.

**When a t-test is not appropriate.** More than two groups (ANOVA – raise with the Guild); heavily skewed outcomes with small samples (transform first, or use the rank-based alternatives in the Overthinking box); categorical outcomes (different methods entirely); paired and independent structure mixed together (Guild).

**QA framework alignment.** t-tests most directly support QA question 3 (Analysis – is the method appropriate and correctly applied?) and, in pilot evaluations, question 5 (Fitness for Purpose – does the comparison answer the right question?). Document the flavour chosen and why, the assumption checks, and every comparison you ran – not only the significant ones.

**Worked examples for this chapter.** [Example 4: Medication Safety Pilot](../examples/example-04-complete.qmd) is a paired before/after evaluation; [Example 5: Regional A&E Performance](../examples/example-05-complete.qmd) is a two-sample comparison with assumption checking.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: State the hypotheses before touching the data

The null hypothesis is business as usual, exactly as [the chapter on statistical inference](07-statistical-inference.qmd) frames it: the pilot made no difference, the two regions share one true mean, the trust meets the standard. The alternative is the possibility you are entertaining. State them – and the direction, if you genuinely have one – before looking at the outcomes, because a hypothesis written after peeking inherits the peek's bias.

### Step 2: Check the assumptions, in order of importance

*Independence* first, because no later step can repair it: one unit, one observation, no clustering, no repeated measures smuggled into an "independent" comparison.

*Normality* second, with proportion: the test assumes the relevant quantity – the observations, or for a paired test the within-pair *differences* – is roughly normal. Look at a histogram or Q-Q plot rather than leaning on a formal test (Shapiro–Wilk flags trivial departures in large samples and misses real ones in small samples, which is exactly backwards for your needs). With more than 30 or so observations per group, the Central Limit Theorem makes the test robust to moderate non-normality; with ten, heavy skew or wild outliers genuinely distort it.

*Equal variances* you can largely retire as a worry: use **Welch's t-test**, which estimates each group's spread separately and adjusts the degrees of freedom. It is R's default for good reason – it costs almost nothing when the variances happen to be equal and protects you when they do not. Do not run a preliminary variance test to decide which t-test to use; the two-stage routine distorts the very error rates it is meant to guard.

### Step 3: Run the test

One line in either language:

- R: `t.test(group1, group2)` – Welch by default; `paired = TRUE` for paired; `mu = target` with a single vector for one-sample.
- Python: `scipy.stats.ttest_ind(group1, group2, equal_var=False)`; `ttest_rel` for paired; `ttest_1samp` for one-sample.

### Step 4: Report the estimate first, the verdict second

Lead with the difference in means and its confidence interval, in the indicator's own units – that is the finding. The t-statistic, degrees of freedom and p-value follow as supporting evidence, with the p-value read as [graded surprise, not proof](07-statistical-inference.qmd). The interval in the software output is 95 per cent by convention and by default; remember the level is a dial someone chose, not a law of nature. And translate: "scores were 6.8 points higher (95 per cent CI 1.9 to 11.8)" tells a decision-maker something; "p = 0.011" alone does not.

## Effect Sizes: Answering "How Large?"

A p-value answers "could this be noise?" and nothing else. The question a regulator actually acts on – is this difference big enough to matter? – needs an **effect size**: a measure of magnitude that does not change just because you collected more data.

The most useful effect size is usually the plainest: the difference in means, in original units, with its confidence interval. "6.8 points on a 100-point experience score" can be weighed against costs, baselines, and what a point of experience score means for people using services; no further statistics needed.

The standardised companion is *Cohen's d*: the difference in means divided by the pooled standard deviation,

$$d = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{s_p}$$

which expresses the gap in units of how much practices ordinarily differ from each other. Its value is comparability – d can be compared across indicators with different units, which is why evaluation literatures speak it. Conventional labels exist (0.2 small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large), and they are the most over-trusted numbers in applied statistics: Cohen offered them reluctantly, for use when nothing better was available, and context beats them every time. A d of 0.2 on mortality matters more than a d of 0.8 on a survey item nobody acts on.

Effect sizes also carry the power lesson in reverse. A small pilot can only ever *detect* large effects – with ten units a group, a real but modest improvement will routinely fail to reach significance. So a non-significant pilot has usually told you "this study could not see effects of plausible size", not "there is no effect". If a pilot's verdict will drive investment, size the study for the smallest effect worth detecting *before* collecting data – a power calculation, and a conversation for the Guild.

::: {.callout-note icon=false}
## 🤔 Rethinking: the verdict trap in pilot evaluations

Pilot evaluations invite a binary reading – "did it work, yes or no?" – and the t-test seems to oblige, p-value as gavel. Both halves of that reading mislead.

A significant pilot has shown that *something* moved the numbers in the pilot sites. Whether it was the intervention is a separate question the test cannot answer: pilot sites are rarely chosen at random, they enjoy attention, enthusiasm, and resources that a rollout will not carry (and improvement driven by being watched has its own name, the Hawthorne effect), and a site selected *because* it was struggling will drift back towards ordinary regardless – the same [regression to the mean](12-z-scoring.qmd) that haunts provider flags. A significant t-test is evidence the pilot sites improved; corroboration is what turns it into evidence the *programme* works.

A non-significant pilot has shown even less. With small samples the test is weak, so "no significant difference" is usually a statement about the pilot's size, not the programme's value – absence of evidence, not evidence of absence. The honest summary of a small null pilot is the confidence interval: "the data are consistent with effects anywhere from −1 to +9 points" keeps the genuine uncertainty visible, where "the pilot found no effect" quietly buries it. Decisions can still be made under that uncertainty; they should just be made knowing it is there.
:::

## Worked Example: Care Coordination Pilot

*Scenario*: ten GP practices ran a care coordination pilot for a year; ten comparable practices did not. The outcome is each practice's mean patient experience score (0–100) from the year-end survey. Did experience improve in the pilot practices?

Note the unit of analysis: the practice, not the patient. Each practice contributes one number – its mean score – so the twenty observations are independent and a t-test is legitimate. Comparing the thousands of underlying patient responses directly would ignore the clustering of patients within practices and overstate the evidence.

### Set up and look at the data

### Run the test and size the effect

:::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}
## R
```{r}
#| label: ttest-run
# Welch two-sample t-test (R's default)
result <- t.test(pilot, control)
result

# Effect size: Cohen's d (pooled standard deviation)
s_pooled <- sqrt((var(pilot) * 9 + var(control) * 9) / 18)
d <- (mean(pilot) - mean(control)) / s_pooled
cat("Difference in means:", round(mean(pilot) - mean(control), 1),
    "points; Cohen's d:", round(d, 2), "\n")
```

## Python
```{python}
#| label: ttest-run-py
from scipy import stats

# Welch two-sample t-test (equal_var=False)
result = stats.ttest_ind(pilot, control, equal_var=False)
ci = result.confidence_interval()
print(f"t = {result.statistic:.2f}, df = {result.df:.1f}, "
      f"p = {result.pvalue:.4f}")
print(f"95% CI for the difference: {ci.low:.2f} to {ci.high:.2f}")

# Effect size: Cohen's d (pooled standard deviation)
s_pooled = np.sqrt((pilot.var(ddof=1) * 9 + control.var(ddof=1) * 9) / 18)
d = (pilot.mean() - control.mean()) / s_pooled
print(f"Difference in means: {pilot.mean() - control.mean():.1f} points; "
      f"Cohen's d: {d:.2f}")
```
::::

### Interpret

Lead with the estimate: pilot practices scored about 6.8 points higher on average, with a 95 per cent confidence interval from roughly 1.9 to 11.8 points. The interval excludes zero – equivalently, the test is significant (t ≈ 2.95, p ≈ 0.011) – so the difference is unlikely to be noise. But read the interval honestly: the data are consistent with anything from a modest 2-point gap to a substantial 12-point one. Cohen's d ≈ 1.3 sounds impressive, yet with ten practices a side it is estimated as imprecisely as everything else here.

Then temper the causal reading, as the Rethinking box above argues: this establishes that pilot practices ended the year scoring higher, not that the programme caused it. Were the pilot sites selected at random, or did enthusiastic practices volunteer? Did they differ at baseline? A 6.8-point difference with this much honest context is a good case for a larger, better-designed evaluation – which is usually exactly what a pilot should earn.

## Many Comparisons: the Multiplicity Problem

Run one test at the conventional threshold and a true null has a 5 per cent chance of a false alarm. Run twenty – twenty indicators, twenty subgroups, twenty regions – and the chance that *at least one* comes up "significant" by luck alone is about 64 per cent. Multiplicity is how diligent, well-intentioned analysis manufactures findings out of noise, and two-group comparisons are especially prone to it because each one is so easy to run.

The defences, in the order to reach for them:

- **Pre-specify.** Name the primary comparison before touching the data; everything else is exploratory and labelled as such. This is the cheapest and strongest defence.
- **Report everything.** All comparisons run, not just the survivors – a "significant" result means little without the denominator of attempts behind it.
- **Correct formally where verdicts matter.** The *Bonferroni correction* divides the significance threshold by the number of tests (20 tests → 0.05/20 = 0.0025 each): simple, defensible, and conservative – with many tests it can drown real effects. The *Benjamini–Hochberg* procedure controls the false discovery rate instead – the expected share of flagged results that are false – which suits screening contexts where some false alarms are tolerable but a flood is not. Choosing between them, and counting what the "family" of tests even is, is worth a Guild conversation when the stakes are real.

In routine profiling across many indicators, recall [the corroboration posture from z-scoring](12-z-scoring.qmd): require signals to agree across sources before acting. Formal corrections are for the designed, verdict-bearing analyses – evaluations, published comparisons – where a stated error rate is part of the claim.

::: {.callout-warning icon=false}
## ⚙️ Overthinking: when the assumptions fail

**Non-normal data.** With small samples and visible skew, two routes exist. A transformation (log is the workhorse for right-skewed times and rates) often restores enough symmetry, at the price of interpreting results on the transformed scale – "a 15 per cent multiplicative difference" rather than "3 days". Or switch to the rank-based alternatives: *Mann–Whitney U* for two independent groups, *Wilcoxon signed-rank* for paired data. They trade a little power for robustness, and note they test a subtly different hypothesis – whether one group's values tend to exceed the other's, not whether means differ – which is often closer to the regulatory question anyway.

**Outliers.** A single wild value can swamp a ten-observation t-test. Do not delete it to rescue significance: [investigate it](06-unusual-observations.qmd), report results with and without it, and if the two disagree, say so – that disagreement *is* the finding.

**Clustered data.** No alternative test fixes broken independence. Patients within practices, residents within homes, repeated months within a provider – these need the right unit of analysis (aggregate to one value per cluster, as the worked example does) or multilevel models (Guild). A t-test on clustered observations is not conservative or approximate; it is wrong, and wrong in the flattering direction.

**Equivalence is not a failed difference test.** "No significant difference" never demonstrates that two groups are the same – the absence-of-evidence trap again. If sameness is the actual claim (a cheaper service performs *no worse*, say), that is equivalence or non-inferiority testing, which reverses the burden of proof and needs its own design. Raise it with the Guild before promising anyone a "no difference" conclusion.
:::

## Documenting Your Analysis

For QA purposes, record: the question and the two groups (or target), with the unit of analysis stated; the flavour of test and why; the assumption checks and what they showed; *every* comparison run, including the non-significant ones; the estimate with its confidence interval, the test statistics, and the effect size; and the practical interpretation, including the limits on any causal reading. The Code of Practice's sound-methods obligations are satisfied by exactly this trail – and a pilot evaluation that may drive investment decisions deserves nothing less.

Raise your analysis with the Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) at a Quant Clinic session when: you need more than two groups (ANOVA), adjustment for confounders (regression), or repeated measures (multilevel models); the data are clustered and aggregation to one value per cluster loses the question; you need a power calculation before a pilot; the claim is equivalence rather than difference; the comparisons multiply and you need a correction strategy; or assumptions fail and you are unsure which alternative answers the question actually asked.
