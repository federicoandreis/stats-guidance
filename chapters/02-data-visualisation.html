2  Visualising Data

TipLearning outcomes

After reading this chapter you will be able to:

  • explain why you should look at a distribution before you trust a number that summarises it;
  • name the one workhorse plot that fits each rung of the ladder of measurement – a bar chart for nominal data, an ordered bar chart for ordinal, a histogram for quantitative – and read what each one shows;
  • match the mark to the data within the top rung – touching bars for a continuous measurement, sticks or lollipops for whole-number counts;
  • recognise that a histogram’s bin width is a choice, not a fact, and that the choice can reveal or hide the story;
  • see that the right plot depends on the data type, the analytical job, and what you need to communicate, so the workhorse is a starting point, not the only answer;
  • apply a few principles – truthful scales, an honest bin width, an assertive title, and accessibility – that keep a chart from misleading its reader.

2.1 Why Look Before You Summarise?

Two hospital wards each reported exactly 60 incidents over the past year. That single total is the kind of figure that lands in a report, and on the strength of it the two wards look identical: same count, same year, nothing to choose between them.

But a yearly total only tells you how many, not when. To see that, you have to look at the fuller pattern – how those 60 incidents fall across the twelve months. That pattern, the complete picture of which values occur and how often (here, in which months the incidents landed), is what we will call a distribution. A total is a single figure squeezed out of it; the distribution is everything the figure left behind.

So plot the distribution – the same 60 incidents, month by month.

Now the two wards could hardly look less alike. Ward A is steady at about five a month; Ward B was quiet for eleven months and then had one catastrophic one. That spike is exactly what a regulator would want to see, and the total – a perfectly honest number – buried it completely. Boiling a distribution down to a single figure is something we do constantly, because one number is compact and easy to compare; the catch is that every such figure throws away the shape it came from, and sometimes the shape was the story. No number here was wrong; the number was simply not enough.

This is the habit the chapter is built around: look at the distribution first, then reduce it to numbers. Drawing the picture is cheap, and it is the surest guard against being misled by your own figures.

This chapter sits early, right after Data Types, for a simple reason: the picture you should draw is decided by the level of measurement. So that is where we start.

2.2 A Plot for Each Rung of the Ladder

Visualisation climbs the same ladder of measurement the data types chapter set out. Each rung adds structure, and that structure decides which plot does the most honest work with the least effort. There are many charts to choose from, but for everyday work one workhorse fits each rung. Learn these three first; reach for the rest when the job asks for them.

Nominal: the bar chart

At the ground floor all you can do is sort cases into categories and count them, so the natural plot draws one bar per category, its height the count. Nothing about the categories is ordered, which leaves you free to sort the bars by size – tallest first – and that makes the chart far easier to read: the eye lands on the biggest group at once.

It is tempting to reach for a pie chart here, but the eye compares lengths far more accurately than angles – a pie with five near-equal slices defeats the reader, where a bar chart settles the ranking at a glance. When in doubt, a bar chart is the safe default for counting categories.

Ordinal: the same bar chart, but the order is sacred

Add an ordering and the bar chart still fits – but one freedom is now forbidden. Because the categories run in a genuine sequence (Inadequate, Requires Improvement, Good, Outstanding), you must keep them in that order along the axis, even when it puts a short bar between two tall ones. Sorting by size, the right move for nominal data, would destroy the very thing that makes the data ordinal.

Keep one thing honest: the bars sit at equal distances along the axis, but that even spacing is just a layout convenience, not a claim that the step from Inadequate to Requires Improvement is the same size as the step from Good to Outstanding. The order is real; the spacing is not – exactly the caution the data types chapter raised about treating ratings as numbers.

Quantitative: the histogram

At the top rung the categories dissolve into a true number line, and the plot changes character. A histogram slices that line into equal-width intervals – bins – counts how many values fall in each, and draws the counts as bars that touch. The touching is the point: it signals that the axis is a continuous quantity rather than a set of separate labels. For the first time the plot shows the shape of the data – where the bulk of the values sit, whether they trail off further on one side, whether there is one peak or two.

A histogram is, in effect, what a bar chart becomes once the axis is a real number line: pushing the bars together turns a count of labels into a picture of a distribution. But it hides a decision the bar charts above did not have to make – how wide should the bins be? – and that decision deserves its own look.

The bin width is a dial, not a given

The bars of a histogram do not exist in the data; you create them by choosing how finely to slice the axis. Slice it coarsely and you get a few wide bins; slice it finely and you get many narrow ones. That choice is not cosmetic – it can change the story the chart appears to tell. The two panels below show the same measurements binned two ways.

Left, the data looks like one group with a single typical value. Right, it splits cleanly into two – a short-stay cluster and a long-stay cluster – which almost certainly means two different kinds of patient have been pooled into one chart. Same numbers, opposite conclusion, decided entirely by the width of a bin.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: Don’t trust the default number of bins

Every plotting tool picks a bin width for you if you do not, and it is easy to accept that default as if it were the answer. It is not. The common rules – names you may meet, such as Sturges’, Scott’s, or the Freedman–Diaconis rule – are heuristics: sensible opening guesses derived from the number of values and their spread, nothing more. Each can mislead. Sturges’ rule tends to use too few bins for large datasets and can smooth a real second peak into a single hump, exactly as the left panel above does.

So treat the first histogram your software draws as a question, not an answer. Redraw it a few times – try half the bin width and twice it – and watch what survives. If a second peak appears and disappears as you nudge the width, you have learnt something either way: a peak that holds up across several widths is probably real, and one that vanishes the moment you change the bin is not yet to be trusted. Playing with the dial is where the insight is.

There is a sharper edge to this. Because the bin width is a free choice that changes the apparent message, it is also a lever for deception – a histogram can be tuned to make a problem vanish or a difference loom, whether through carelessness or intent. A visualisation is one of the most persuasive things you can put in front of a decision-maker, which is precisely why it is one of the easiest ways to mislead one. Choosing the bin width honestly – and being ready to show the chart at other widths – is part of doing the analysis in good faith.

Continuous or discrete: match the mark to the data

The top rung holds two kinds of data, and the data types chapter flagged that the split decides which chart to reach for. Continuous data – waiting times, rates, lengths of stay – can fall anywhere on the line, so the histogram’s touching bars are exactly right: each bar owns a stretch of the axis, and the unbroken row says the axis is a continuum. Discrete data – counts that can only land on whole numbers, like serious incidents per provider or beds per home – does not live in stretches. A provider has 2 incidents or 3, never 2.6, so the value sits at a single point on the line with nothing on either side of it.

Drawing counts as touching, filled bars quietly contradicts that: a bar spanning the width from 1.5 to 2.5 implies the value could have been anything in between, which it could not. A stick (or lollipop) plot says it straight – a thin stem rising to a dot at each whole-number count, and bare axis between – so the chart matches the data: discrete values at discrete points.

None of this makes the bar version wrong – with many possible count values a histogram is often the practical choice, and separated bars are a perfectly honest middle ground. The point is to choose the mark on purpose: reserve touching bars for data that really is continuous, and let sticks or lollipops carry counts, especially when the counts are few or when a lighter, less ink-heavy chart would read more clearly. It is the same instinct as the rest of the chapter – let the form of the plot match the form of the data.

2.3 The Workhorse Is Just the Start

One plot per rung is the safe default, but it is not the whole story. The right chart depends on three questions answered together, and the data type is only the first:

  • What is the data type? This rules plots in and out. You cannot put nominal data on a number line, and you should not sort ordinal data by height. The rung sets the boundaries.
  • What is the analytical job? Showing one variable’s shape, comparing several groups, tracking a value over time, or revealing how two quantities move together are different jobs, and each favours a different plot.
  • What do you need to communicate, and to whom? A chart you use to explore your own data is not the same as one a board sees on a slide. Exploration rewards detail; communication rewards a single clear message.

Quantitative data shows this most clearly. The histogram is the default for one variable’s shape, but if the job is to compare several groups a box plot is often better: it draws each group as a compact summary – a box covering the central bulk of the values, a line for the middle, and whiskers reaching toward the extremes – so a dozen groups line up side by side in the space one histogram would take. (The exact recipe for that box is the business of later chapters; here it is enough that it summarises a distribution at a glance.) You give up the fine detail of the shape to gain the comparison. Which trade is right is not a property of the data alone – it is a property of the question you are asking and the person you are answering.

Note🤔 Rethinking: The same data, more than one honest plot

A plot is not just a faithful record of the data; it is an argument about what matters in it. The same column of waiting times can honestly become several different charts depending on the job. An analyst hunting for problems wants the histogram – the full shape, every bump and gap on show. A report comparing provider types wants box plots stacked side by side – less detail per group, but the comparison made instantly. A board paper might want a single headline figure with a small strip of dots behind it – almost no detail, one message that lands in a second.

None of these is the “correct” plot and the others wrong. They are tuned to different readers and different decisions, and all three can tell the truth. The skill is not memorising one plot per data type; it is matching the chart to the question being asked and the person asking it. That judgement – and the much wider catalogue of plots it draws on – is the craft the Data Visualisation Hub and Team (the Hub) exists to support.

Beyond one variable: pairs, matrices, and maps

Everything so far has shown a single variable at a time. Step up to two and a new family of plots opens, far too large to survey here – this is where the “so many options” really begins, and where the Hub’s deeper guidance earns its place. Three signposts are enough for now.

When you have two quantitative variables and want to see whether they move together – do longer waits go with more complaints? – the natural plot is the scatter plot: one variable on each axis, a dot for each case, and the pattern of the cloud tells the story. When you have a grid of values – every region crossed with every provider type, say – a heatmap colours each cell by its value, turning a dense table into something the eye can scan for hot spots. And when the unit is a place – providers by local authority, say – a map puts the data where it belongs geographically, revealing clusters a bar chart never could.

The scatter plot also carries a warning worth meeting now, because it shows how far a plot can outrun a summary. The four clouds below come from four datasets that share the same standard summary measures – the same centre, the same spread, and the same strength of straight-line relationship between the two variables (all quantities made precise in later chapters; take them on trust for the moment). By those numbers the four are interchangeable.

Only the first is the tidy relationship those numbers imply. One bends, one is a clean line knocked askew by a single outlier, and one conjures a trend out of a lone far-off point. A regulator who reported only the summary for the fourth would be claiming a relationship that rests entirely on one case. It is the same lesson the two wards taught at the start of the chapter, now at the top of the ladder: the summary can agree while the shape disagrees, so look first.

2.4 Principles for an Honest, Readable Chart

Whatever plot you choose, a handful of principles keep it honest. The bin-width trap was one of them; here are four more. Each is simple to state and easy to breach by accident – and, as the binning example warned, easy to breach on purpose too.

Scales tell the truth. The fastest way to mislead with a truthful dataset is to clip the axis. A bar encodes its value as length from a baseline, so a bar chart’s axis must start at zero. Truncate it and you turn a small difference into a dramatic one.

Show the data, not just a summary. Where you reasonably can, prefer plots that reveal the distribution over those that hide it behind a single mark. A bar showing a group’s average, with a small error bar on top, collapses a whole distribution to two numbers and can conceal a long tail, a stray extreme value, or even two distinct clusters hiding inside one average – the very thing the binning example exposed. When the data is few enough to show, show it.

Minimise chartjunk. Every drop of ink should carry information. Strip out the decoration – heavy gridlines, three-dimensional effects, redundant colour – that adds nothing. In the rung charts above, colour does no work because the category is already named on the axis, so a single colour is the honest choice; bring colour in only when it encodes something the reader needs.

Note🤔 Rethinking: The title is part of the chart

Most charts are labelled with their subject – “Waiting times by provider type” – which tells the reader what they are looking at but not what to see. For a plot whose job is to communicate a finding, the title can do far more work if it states the finding itself: “Mental health trusts wait twice as long as care homes.” An assertive title like that is sometimes the only line a busy reader takes in, so it should carry the message, with the chart standing as the evidence beneath it.

This applies to plots meant for an audience. A quick chart you draw to explore your own data, mid-way through a larger piece of work, needs no such polish – a plain label is fine, and over-styling exploratory plots is wasted effort. The distinction is the same one the three questions drew: a plot for thinking and a plot for telling are different objects, and the title is where the difference shows most.

Finally, and least negotiable of all: accessibility is a requirement, not a finishing touch.

A chart that some of your readers cannot read has failed, however elegant it is. Designing for accessibility from the start – not bolting it on at the end – means: choosing colourblind-safe palettes; never relying on colour alone to carry meaning, but doubling it up with labels, shapes, or position; ensuring text and lines have enough contrast against the background; and writing a short text description (a fig-alt, as every figure in this book carries) so that a reader using a screen reader gets the same information as everyone else, or should the image break for some reason, the user would still get the description in its stead.

This is a craft in its own right, and one with real consequences for who can act on your analysis. The Data Visualisation Team maintain dedicated accessibility guidance that goes well beyond this paragraph – consult it whenever you are producing a chart for others to read and reach out to them when in doubt. (Signpost to the Hub’s accessibility guidance to follow.)

2.5 Where Visualisation Leads

The three workhorses – bar, ordered bar, histogram – will carry most of your everyday work, and the principles above will keep them honest. But they are only a doorway. Even within this chapter you have stepped past them: a stick plot to keep whole-number counts honest, and a scatter plot to see whether two quantities move together. And the chapter has named more plots than it had room to draw – the box plot for comparing many groups at once, the heatmap for a grid of values, the map for data tied to a place – each a craft of its own.

The lesson is that choosing a plot rewards as much thought as the analysis it illustrates. Whenever the data, the analytical question, or the audience asks for more than a workhorse can give, the wider catalogue – and the people who tend it – are a conversation with the Hub away. Whatever you reach for, the habit that opened this chapter is the one to keep: look at the distribution first, then reduce it to numbers.

## A Plot for Each Rung of the Ladder

Visualisation climbs the same [ladder of measurement](01-data-types.qmd) the data types chapter set out. Each rung adds structure, and that structure decides which plot does the most honest work with the least effort. There are many charts to choose from, but for everyday work one workhorse fits each rung. Learn these three first; reach for the rest when the job asks for them.

### Nominal: the bar chart

At the ground floor all you can do is sort cases into categories and count them, so the natural plot draws one bar per category, its height the count. Nothing about the categories is ordered, which leaves you free to **sort the bars by size** – tallest first – and that makes the chart far easier to read: the eye lands on the biggest group at once.

```{r}
#| label: fig-bar-nominal
#| fig-cap: "A bar chart of provider counts by type in one region (illustrative figures). Because the categories have no natural order, the bars are sorted from most to least frequent, so the largest group reads first. Heights are counts; the gaps between bars signal that the categories are separate, unconnected labels."
#| fig-alt: "Vertical bar chart of five provider types sorted from tallest to shortest: Care home about 142, GP practice about 98, Dental about 61, Hospital about 24, Mental health about 15. Bars are evenly spaced with clear gaps between them, all drawn in a single teal colour."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 3.4

library(ggplot2)

prov <- data.frame(
  type  = c("Care home", "GP practice", "Dental", "Hospital", "Mental health"),
  count = c(142, 98, 61, 24, 15)
)

ggplot(prov, aes(x = reorder(type, -count), y = count)) +
  geom_col(width = 0.7, fill = "#006560", alpha = 0.9) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = "Number of providers") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        panel.grid.major.x = element_blank())
```

Sorting by size, the right move for nominal data, would destroy the very thing that makes the data ordinal.

```{r}
#| label: fig-bar-ordinal
#| fig-cap: "A bar chart of CQC ratings across providers in one region (illustrative figures). A single neutral colour keeps the focus on the shape; the order is carried by position, not by colour."
#| fig-alt: "Vertical bar chart of four CQC ratings kept in fixed order along the axis: Inadequate about 8, Requires Improvement about 34, Good about 121 and by far the tallest, Outstanding about 27. The bars are not sorted by height; they follow the rating scale from worst to best, all drawn in a single blue colour."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 3.4

library(ggplot2)

rating_levels <- c("Inadequate", "Requires\nImprovement", "Good", "Outstanding")
rat <- data.frame(
  rating = factor(rating_levels, levels = rating_levels),
  count  = c(8, 34, 121, 27)
)

ggplot(rat, aes(x = rating, y = count)) +
  geom_col(width = 0.7, fill = "#007EF5", alpha = 0.9) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = "Number of providers") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        panel.grid.major.x = element_blank())

### Quantitative: the histogram

At the top rung the categories dissolve into a true number line, and the plot changes character. A **histogram** slices that line into equal-width intervals – *bins* – counts how many values fall in each, and draws the counts as bars that *touch*. The touching is the point: it signals that the axis is a continuous quantity rather than a set of separate labels. For the first time the plot shows the **shape** of the data – where the bulk of the values sit, whether they trail off further on one side, whether there is one peak or two.

```{r}
#| label: fig-histogram-quantitative
#| fig-cap: "A histogram of provider waiting times in hours (synthetic data, n = 400). The continuous axis is sliced into one-hour bins, and the touching bars show the shape: most providers cluster at short waits, with a long tail of slower ones reaching to the right. A single average could not show that lopsidedness; the picture shows it at once."
#| fig-alt: "Histogram of waiting times in hours with touching bars. The distribution rises quickly to a peak around two to three hours then falls away gradually, with a long thin tail of bars extending right past ten hours. The shape is clearly asymmetric, with a long tail to the right, drawn in gold with white bar edges."

#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 3.4

set.seed(11)
waits <- rgamma(400, shape = 2.2, scale = 1.7)
wait_df <- data.frame(hours = waits)

ggplot(wait_df, aes(x = hours)) +
  geom_histogram(binwidth = 1, boundary = 0,
                 fill = "#A57703", colour = "white", alpha = 0.9) +
  labs(x = "Waiting time (hours)", y = "Number of providers") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +

Had this been a real analysis, the wide-binned version would have hidden the fact that two different kinds of patient are mixed together."
#| fig-alt: "Two histograms of the same length-of-stay data. Left panel, wide bins: three tall bars forming a single central hump, suggesting one group of patients. Right panel, narrow bins: two separate peaks, one around five days and one around ten days, with a deep valley between them, revealing two distinct groups that the wide bins concealed."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 8
#| fig-height: 3.4

library(ggplot2)

set.seed(3)
los <- c(rnorm(160, 5, 1.1), rnorm(160, 10, 1.1))

make_bins <- function(x, bw, label) {
  brks <- seq(0, ceiling(max(x)) + bw, by = bw)
  h <- hist(x, breaks = brks, plot = FALSE)
  data.frame(panel = label,
             xmin = head(h$breaks, -1),
             xmax = tail(h$breaks, -1),
             count = h$counts)
}

bins <- rbind(
  make_bins(los, 5, "Wide bins: one hump"),
  make_bins(los, 1, "Narrow bins: two groups")
)
bins$panel <- factor(bins$panel,
                     levels = c("Wide bins: one hump", "Narrow bins: two groups"))

ggplot(bins, aes(xmin = xmin, xmax = xmax, ymin = 0, ymax = count)) +
  geom_rect(fill = "#A57703", colour = "white", alpha = 0.9) +
  facet_wrap(~panel) +
  labs(x = "Length of stay (days)", y = "Number of patients") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        panel.grid.major.x = element_blank(),
        strip.text = element_text(face = "bold"))
```

Left, the data looks like one group with a single typical value. Right, it splits cleanly into two – a short-stay cluster and a long-stay cluster – which almost certainly means two different kinds of patient have been pooled into one chart. Same numbers, opposite conclusion, decided entirely by the width of a bin.

::: {.callout-warning icon=false}
## ⚙️ Overthinking: Don't trust the default number of bins

Every plotting tool picks a bin width for you if you do not, and it is easy to accept that default as if it were the answer. It is not. The common rules – names you may meet, such as Sturges', Scott's, or the Freedman–Diaconis rule – are *heuristics*: sensible opening guesses derived from the number of values and their spread, nothing more. Each can mislead. Sturges' rule tends to use too few bins for large datasets and can smooth a real second peak into a single hump, exactly as the left panel above does.

So treat the first histogram your software draws as a question, not an answer. Redraw it a few times – try half the bin width and twice it – and watch what survives. If a second peak appears and disappears as you nudge the width, you have learnt something either way: a peak that holds up across several widths is probably real, and one that vanishes the moment you change the bin is not yet to be trusted. Playing with the dial is where the insight is.

There is a sharper edge to this. Because the bin width is a free choice that changes the apparent message, it is also a lever for **deception** – a histogram can be tuned to make a problem vanish or a difference loom, whether through carelessness or intent. A visualisation is one of the most persuasive things you can put in front of a decision-maker, which is precisely why it is one of the easiest ways to mislead one. Choosing the bin width honestly – and being ready to show the chart at other widths – is part of doing the analysis in good faith.
:::

### Continuous or discrete: match the mark to the data

The top rung holds two kinds of data, and the [data types chapter](01-data-types.qmd) flagged that the split decides which chart to reach for. **Continuous** data – waiting times, rates, lengths of stay – can fall anywhere on the line, so the histogram's touching bars are exactly right: each bar owns a stretch of the axis, and the unbroken row says the axis is a continuum. **Discrete** data – counts that can only land on whole numbers, like serious incidents per provider or beds per home – does not live in stretches. A provider has 2 incidents or 3, never 2.6, so the value sits at a single point on the line with nothing on either side of it.

Drawing counts as touching, filled bars quietly contradicts that: a bar spanning the width from 1.5 to 2.5 implies the value could have been anything in between, which it could not. A **stick** (or **lollipop**) plot says it straight – a thin stem rising to a dot at each whole-number count, and bare axis between – so the chart matches the data: discrete values at discrete points.

```{r}
#| label: fig-discrete-mark
#| fig-cap: "The same distribution of a count – serious incidents per provider (synthetic data, n = 300) – drawn two ways. Left, as touching bars: the unbroken row implies a continuum, as if a provider could have had 2.6 incidents. Right, as a lollipop plot: a dot on a thin stem at each whole number, with empty axis between, which is what a count actually is. The lollipop also spends far less ink for the same information – an echo of the chartjunk principle below."
#| fig-alt: "Two panels of the same count data, integers zero to eight. Left panel, touching bars: filled gold bars meeting edge to edge, looking like a histogram and implying a continuous axis. Right panel, lollipops: a thin grey stem rising to a teal dot above each whole number, with clear empty space between the stems, emphasising that the values are separate whole-number counts. The heights match across the two panels."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 8
#| fig-height: 3.4

library(ggplot2)

set.seed(8)
x <- rpois(300, 2.3)
tab <- as.data.frame(table(factor(x, levels = 0:max(x))))
names(tab) <- c("value", "count")
tab$value <- as.integer(as.character(tab$value))

lev <- c("Touching bars: imply a continuum", "Lollipops: one count, one point")
df_bar   <- transform(tab, panel = factor("Touching bars: imply a continuum", levels = lev))
df_lolli <- transform(tab, panel = factor("Lollipops: one count, one point", levels = lev))

ggplot(mapping = aes(x = value, y = count)) +
  geom_col(data = df_bar, width = 1, fill = "#A57703", colour = "white", alpha = 0.9) +
  geom_segment(data = df_lolli, aes(xend = value, yend = 0),
               colour = "#8796AA", linewidth = 0.6) +
  geom_point(data = df_lolli, colour = "#006560", size = 2.6) +
  facet_wrap(~panel) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = 0:8) +
  labs(x = "Serious incidents per provider (a count)", y = "Number of providers") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        panel.grid.major.x = element_blank(),
        strip.text = element_text(face = "bold"))
```

None of this makes the bar version *wrong* – with many possible count values a histogram is often the practical choice, and separated bars are a perfectly honest middle ground. The point is to choose the mark on purpose: reserve touching bars for data that really is continuous, and let sticks or lollipops carry counts, especially when the counts are few or when a lighter, less ink-heavy chart would read more clearly. It is the same instinct as the rest of the chapter – let the form of the plot match the form of the data.

## The Workhorse Is Just the Start

One plot per rung is the safe default, but it is not the whole story. The right chart depends on three questions answered *together*, and the data type is only the first:

- **What is the data type?** This rules plots in and out. You cannot put nominal data on a number line, and you should not sort ordinal data by height. The rung sets the boundaries.
- **What is the analytical job?** Showing one variable's shape, comparing several groups, tracking a value over time, or revealing how two quantities move together are different jobs, and each favours a different plot.
- **What do you need to communicate, and to whom?** A chart you use to explore your own data is not the same as one a board sees on a slide. Exploration rewards detail; communication rewards a single clear message.

Quantitative data shows this most clearly. The histogram is the default for *one variable's shape*, but if the job is to *compare several groups* a **box plot** is often better: it draws each group as a compact summary – a box covering the central bulk of the values, a line for the middle, and whiskers reaching toward the extremes – so a dozen groups line up side by side in the space one histogram would take. (The exact recipe for that box is the business of later chapters; here it is enough that it summarises a distribution at a glance.) You give up the fine detail of the shape to gain the comparison. Which trade is right is not a property of the data alone – it is a property of the question you are asking and the person you are answering.

::: {.callout-note icon=false}
## 🤔 Rethinking: The same data, more than one honest plot

A plot is not just a faithful record of the data; it is an argument about what matters in it. The same column of waiting times can honestly become several different charts depending on the job. An analyst hunting for problems wants the **histogram** – the full shape, every bump and gap on show. A report comparing provider types wants **box plots** stacked side by side – less detail per group, but the comparison made instantly. A board paper might want a single headline figure with a small strip of dots behind it – almost no detail, one message that lands in a second.

None of these is the "correct" plot and the others wrong. They are tuned to different readers and different decisions, and all three can tell the truth. The skill is not memorising one plot per data type; it is matching the chart to the question being asked and the person asking it. That judgement – and the much wider catalogue of plots it draws on – is the craft the Data Visualisation Hub and Team (the Hub) exists to support.
:::

### Beyond one variable: pairs, matrices, and maps

Everything so far has shown a single variable at a time. Step up to two and a new family of plots opens, far too large to survey here – this is where the "so many options" really begins, and where the Hub's deeper guidance earns its place. Three signposts are enough for now.

When you have **two quantitative variables** and want to see whether they move together – do longer waits go with more complaints? – the natural plot is the **scatter plot**: one variable on each axis, a dot for each case, and the pattern of the cloud tells the story. When you have a **grid of values** – every region crossed with every provider type, say – a **heatmap** colours each cell by its value, turning a dense table into something the eye can scan for hot spots. And when the unit is a **place** – providers by local authority, say – a **map** puts the data where it belongs geographically, revealing clusters a bar chart never could.

The scatter plot also carries a warning worth meeting now, because it shows how far a plot can outrun a summary. The four clouds below come from four datasets that share the same standard summary measures – the same centre, the same spread, and the same strength of straight-line relationship between the two variables (all quantities made precise in later chapters; take them on trust for the moment). By those numbers the four are interchangeable.

```{r}
#| label: fig-anscombe
#| fig-cap: "Anscombe's quartet: four datasets that agree on every common summary measure – the average of each variable, how spread out each is, and how tightly the two track together – yet look nothing alike once plotted. Only the first is the simple cloud those numbers suggest. The second bends in a smooth curve, the third is a straight line dragged off course by a single high point, and the fourth invents a relationship out of one lone point far to the right. A summary agreed; the pictures did not."
#| fig-alt: "Four scatter panels, each with the same straight grey trend line. Dataset 1: points scatter loosely around the line, a genuine straight-line relationship. Dataset 2: points trace a smooth curve, clearly not a straight line. Dataset 3: points lie exactly on a steeper line except one high outlier that bends the trend. Dataset 4: all points share one position in a vertical stack except a single far point to the right that alone creates the trend."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 8
#| fig-height: 4.2

library(ggplot2)

ans <- datasets::anscombe
long <- do.call(rbind, lapply(1:4, function(i) {
  data.frame(set = paste0("Dataset ", i),
             x = ans[[paste0("x", i)]],
             y = ans[[paste0("y", i)]])
}))

ggplot(long, aes(x, y)) +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE, colour = "#8796AA", linewidth = 0.5) +
  geom_point(colour = "#007EF5", size = 2, alpha = 0.9) +
  facet_wrap(~set, nrow = 1) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = c(5, 10, 15)) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = NULL) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        strip.text = element_text(face = "bold"))
```

Only the first is the tidy relationship those numbers imply. One bends, one is a clean line knocked askew by a single outlier, and one conjures a trend out of a lone far-off point. A regulator who reported only the summary for the fourth would be claiming a relationship that rests entirely on one case. It is the same lesson the two wards taught at the start of the chapter, now at the top of the ladder: the summary can agree while the shape disagrees, so look first.

## Principles for an Honest, Readable Chart

Whatever plot you choose, a handful of principles keep it honest. The bin-width trap was one of them; here are four more. Each is simple to state and easy to breach by accident – and, as the binning example warned, easy to breach on purpose too.

**Scales tell the truth.** The fastest way to mislead with a truthful dataset is to clip the axis. A bar encodes its value as length from a baseline, so a bar chart's axis must start at zero. Truncate it and you turn a small difference into a dramatic one.

```{r}
#| label: fig-truncated-axis
#| fig-cap: "The misleading version. Four regions' share of providers rated Good or better (illustrative figures: 88%, 90%, 91%, 93%). With the axis clipped to start near 85%, the East's bar towers over the North's and the differences look dramatic – yet they span just five percentage points."
#| fig-alt: "Bar chart of four regions with the vertical axis running only from about 85 to 94 percent. The bars appear very different in height, with North looking far shorter than East, exaggerating what is in fact a five-percentage-point spread. The truncated axis makes small differences look large."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 5.2
#| fig-height: 3.2

library(ggplot2)

reg <- data.frame(
  region = factor(c("North", "Midlands", "South", "East"),
                  levels = c("North", "Midlands", "South", "East")),
  pct = c(88, 90, 91, 93)
)

ggplot(reg, aes(x = region, y = pct)) +
  geom_col(width = 0.7, fill = "#A83DA4", alpha = 0.9) +
  coord_cartesian(ylim = c(85, 94)) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = "% Good or better") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        panel.grid.major.x = element_blank())
```

```{r}
#| label: fig-honest-axis
#| fig-cap: "The honest version of the same four numbers. With the axis starting at zero, the bars are nearly level – the regions are much alike, and the small lead the East holds is shown at its true size. Same data, opposite impression, decided entirely by where the axis begins."
#| fig-alt: "Bar chart of the same four regions with the vertical axis running from zero to 100 percent. All four bars are tall and almost equal in height, correctly showing that the regions differ only slightly. The honest baseline reveals the five-percentage-point spread as the minor difference it is."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 5.2
#| fig-height: 3.2

library(ggplot2)

reg <- data.frame(
  region = factor(c("North", "Midlands", "South", "East"),
                  levels = c("North", "Midlands", "South", "East")),
  pct = c(88, 90, 91, 93)
)

ggplot(reg, aes(x = region, y = pct)) +
  geom_col(width = 0.7, fill = "#007EF5", alpha = 0.9) +
  coord_cartesian(ylim = c(0, 100)) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = "% Good or better") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        panel.grid.major.x = element_blank())
```

**Show the data, not just a summary.** Where you reasonably can, prefer plots that reveal the distribution over those that hide it behind a single mark. A bar showing a group's average, with a small error bar on top, collapses a whole distribution to two numbers and can conceal a long tail, a stray extreme value, or even two distinct clusters hiding inside one average – the very thing the binning example exposed. When the data is few enough to show, show it.

**Minimise chartjunk.** Every drop of ink should carry information. Strip out the decoration – heavy gridlines, three-dimensional effects, redundant colour – that adds nothing. In the rung charts above, colour does no work because the category is already named on the axis, so a single colour is the honest choice; bring colour in only when it encodes something the reader needs.

::: {.callout-note icon=false}
## 🤔 Rethinking: The title is part of the chart

Most charts are labelled with their subject – "Waiting times by provider type" – which tells the reader what they are looking at but not what to see. For a plot whose job is to *communicate a finding*, the title can do far more work if it states the finding itself: "Mental health trusts wait twice as long as care homes." An assertive title like that is sometimes the only line a busy reader takes in, so it should carry the message, with the chart standing as the evidence beneath it.

This applies to plots meant for an audience. A quick chart you draw to explore your own data, mid-way through a larger piece of work, needs no such polish – a plain label is fine, and over-styling exploratory plots is wasted effort. The distinction is the same one the three questions drew: a plot for thinking and a plot for telling are different objects, and the title is where the difference shows most.
:::

Finally, and least negotiable of all: **accessibility is a requirement, not a finishing touch**.

A chart that some of your readers cannot read has failed, however elegant it is. Designing for accessibility from the start – not bolting it on at the end – means: choosing colourblind-safe palettes; never relying on colour *alone* to carry meaning, but doubling it up with labels, shapes, or position; ensuring text and lines have enough contrast against the background; and writing a short text description (a *fig-alt*, as every figure in this book carries) so that a reader using a screen reader gets the same information as everyone else, or should the image break for some reason, the user would still get the description in its stead.

This is a craft in its own right, and one with real consequences for who can act on your analysis. The Data Visualisation Team maintain dedicated accessibility guidance that goes well beyond this paragraph – consult it whenever you are producing a chart for others to read and reach out to them when in doubt. **(Signpost to the Hub's accessibility guidance to follow.)**

## Where Visualisation Leads

The three workhorses – bar, ordered bar, histogram – will carry most of your everyday work, and the principles above will keep them honest. But they are only a doorway. Even within this chapter you have stepped past them: a stick plot to keep whole-number counts honest, and a scatter plot to see whether two quantities move together. And the chapter has named more plots than it had room to draw – the box plot for comparing many groups at once, the heatmap for a grid of values, the map for data tied to a place – each a craft of its own.

The lesson is that choosing a plot rewards as much thought as the analysis it illustrates. Whenever the data, the analytical question, or the audience asks for more than a workhorse can give, the wider catalogue – and the people who tend it – are a conversation with the Hub away. Whatever you reach for, the habit that opened this chapter is the one to keep: look at the distribution first, then reduce it to numbers.
