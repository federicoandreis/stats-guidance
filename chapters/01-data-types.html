1  Data Types & Implications

TipLearning outcomes

After reading this chapter you will be able to:

  • place any CQC variable on the ladder of measurement – nominal, ordinal, or quantitative (continuous or discrete) – using two simple questions;
  • read each rung for what it unlocks, choosing a method on the basis of the data’s type, not its subject;
  • give qualitative data its own proper methods rather than forcing it into quantitative tools;
  • recognise the boundary cases where the type is a judgement call, and know to document the choice and consult.

1.1 Why Data Type Matters

Suppose someone hands you a column of CQC ratings – Inadequate, Requires Improvement, Good, Outstanding – and asks for the average. It is tempting to code them 1 to 4, take the mean, and report, say, 2.7. But what is 2.7? It lands between Requires Improvement and Good, yet nothing in the data says a provider is “seven-tenths of the way to Good”, and nothing says the gap from Inadequate to Requires Improvement is the same size as the gap from Good to Outstanding. The figure is arithmetically neat and analytically empty. The mistake was not in the calculation – it was in choosing a method the data could not bear.

That is what this chapter is about. Before you can pick a sound method, you have to know what kind of data you are holding, because the same regulatory question – is this provider unusual? – calls for different tools depending on whether you are looking at waiting times, incident counts, or ratings. Get the type right and the method usually follows. Get it wrong and the analysis can be invalid before you have computed a single number.

Data type decides:

  • which statistical methods are valid,
  • which distributions might plausibly describe the data,
  • how to summarise it honestly,
  • which charts make sense,
  • and how to read the result.

It is the first thing to settle and the cheapest mistake to avoid.

1.2 Two Questions That Sort Any Variable

A variable is any characteristic that can take different values from one case to the next – a provider’s rating, its waiting time, the number of incidents it reported last quarter. Almost anything you measure at CQC is a variable, and it can be sorted into a type with two questions.

The first is the big one: is this a quantity, or a label? A quantity is a number you can do arithmetic on – waiting times, counts, rates – where sums and means are meaningful. A label sorts cases into groups – region, rating, incident category – where arithmetic is nonsense, however numeric the codes look. This split, quantitative versus qualitative, governs everything downstream.

The second question depends on the answer to the first, and it is what separates four data sub-types.

Quantitative: numbers you can calculate with

For quantitative data the second question is: can it take any value in a range, or only whole steps?

Continuous data can take any value, decimals included. Waiting times (2.5 hours), length of stay (4.2 days), rates per 1,000 bed-days, staff-to-patient ratios. The tell is that between any two values you can always imagine another: between 1.5 and 1.6 metres there is 1.55, and between 1.55 and 1.56 there is 1.555, without end. Percentages belong here too – though penned between 0 and 100, a percentage can in principle be 23.4% or 23.456%.

Discrete data can only take separated values, almost always whole-number counts: the number of incidents, complaints, registered nurses, beds. You can record 5 incidents or 6, never 5.3. The distinction is about how you measure, not what you measure – days counted whole (1, 2, 3) are discrete, but the moment you record 1.5 days they become continuous.

Qualitative: labels and categories

For qualitative data the second question is: do the categories have a natural order?

Nominal data has none. Provider type, region, service type, incident category – “North East” is not more or less than “South West”, merely different. The categories are names, and names do not line up.

Ordinal data does line up. CQC ratings run Inadequate < Requires Improvement < Good < Outstanding; severity runs Low < Medium < High; a Likert item runs Strongly Disagree through to Strongly Agree. The order is genuine – but, as the rating average example hinted, the steps between categories are not guaranteed to be equal in size. That single fact is why ordinal data should not be treated as though it were quantitative without making that assumption explicit, and it is probably the most common type error in statistical analysis.

Put together, that is the whole map:

  • Quantitative – numbers it makes sense to apply direct calculations to
    • Continuous – any value in a range: waiting times, rates, percentages, ratios
    • Discrete – whole-number counts: incidents, complaints, staff, beds
  • Qualitative – labels and categories
    • Nominal – no natural order: region, provider type, incident category
    • Ordinal – ordered, with possibly unequal gaps: CQC ratings, severity, Likert scales

1.3 A Ladder, Not Four Boxes

Those four types are not four equal boxes side by side. They form a ladder – what statisticians call the levels of measurement – and each rung up adds mathematical structure, unlocking things the rungs below cannot do. Read it from the bottom:

  • Nominal is the ground floor: all you can do is tell categories apart and count how often each occurs. Equality and frequency, nothing more.
  • Ordinal adds order: you can now rank cases and ask which is greater, on top of counting them.
  • Quantitative adds arithmetic: the gaps between values are real, so you can add, subtract, and take a mean.

Each rung keeps everything below it and gains something new. That is the single most useful idea in this chapter, because it tells you – before you pick any method – what you are allowed to do. You can count at every level, so the mode works anywhere. Ordering arrives at the ordinal rung, so the median and quantiles open up there. Arithmetic waits until the top, which is why the mean, the standard deviation, and most of the methods in this book need quantitative data. Climbing the ladder is the same as climbing the next chapter on describing data: mode, then median, then mean.

Two footnotes keep this honest. The continuous-versus-discrete split lives inside the top rung – it does not change what arithmetic is allowed, but it does decide which distribution to expect and which chart to reach for, since smooth measurements and whole-number counts behave differently. And the formal names for the top rung are interval and ratio (the difference is whether zero is a true zero); almost all CQC data is ratio – counts, times, and rates all have real zeros – so that distinction rarely earns its keep here.

Note🤔 Rethinking: Data type is sometimes a choice

CQC does not always inherit a data type; sometimes it chooses one. Should days to assessment be counted as discrete whole days or measured as continuous time? Should a rating be kept as an ordinal category or converted to a numeric score? These are not purely statistical decisions, and they are not made on statistical grounds alone:

  • Interpretability – stakeholders grasp “3 days” more readily than “2.7 days”.
  • Fairness – whole days may be fairer where providers cannot control exact timing.
  • Consistency – using the same approach across indicators makes them comparable.
  • Analytical power – continuous data generally carries more statistical information.

When you have a choice, document the reasoning: why you measured it this way, what alternatives you weighed, and what trade-offs you accepted. The “right” data type is the one that best serves the analytical purpose while remaining defensible to the people who will act on it – which is the Code of Practice’s Quality and Value pillars in everyday form.

1.4 From Type to Method

The reason all of this earns its place is that a variable’s rung on the ladder points straight at the methods that fit and, just as usefully, at the ones that do not – the higher it sits, the more the table opens up. The table below is a bridge from this chapter to the rest of the book: find your type, and it tells you where to go.

Two things in that table are worth pausing on. The first is that qualitative data is not a dead end. Nominal and ordinal variables have their own honest methods – cross-tabulations, chi-squared, quantiles, Spearman’s correlation – and the right move is to use them, not to force a category into a quantitative tool because the quantitative tools are more familiar.

The second is the proportions row. A proportion – the share of cases meeting some standard – can be though of as a percentage, and so continuous in principle. But because it is built from a count out of a total, the methods that suit it model that underlying count, which is why it sits among the discrete methods here rather than the continuous ones. 1.5 When the Type Isn’t Obvious

Most variables sort themselves in seconds. The ones that cause trouble are the handful that seem to sit in two places at once – and they cause trouble precisely because the method you reach for hangs on which type you decide they are.

“My data could be two types at once.” Usually this means the raw measurement is being confused with something derived from it. The number of incidents is discrete; the incident rate per 1,000 bed-days, calculated from it, is continuous. The raw type governs what you can do first; once you compute a rate or a percentage, you have created continuous data and a different set of methods opens up.

Percentages built on small counts. A percentage is continuous in principle, but something out of 10 can only ever be 0%, 10%, 20%, and so on – effectively discrete, and it misbehaves in methods that assume a smooth continuum. With small denominators, give a percentage the same caution you would give the bare count beneath it (this is one of the traps the z-scoring chapter handles directly).

Time, measured how? It depends on the ruler. Whole days (1, 2, 3) are discrete; days with fractions (1.5, 2.3) are continuous; banded time (0–7 days, 8–14, 15+) is ordinal. The same underlying quantity yields three different types and three different method sets, depending only on how it was recorded.

Likert scales. Strictly ordinal: 1 to 5, Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree. With many responses, analysts sometimes average them and treat the result as continuous. That is convenient and occasionally defensible, but it quietly assumes the steps are evenly spaced – the one thing ordinal data does not promise. The Overthinking box below weighs when it is and is not safe.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: Treating ordinal data as if it were numbers

The most contested boundary case is whether ordinal scales – Likert items above all – can be averaged and treated as continuous. The argument in favour: with enough responses, the mean of many ordinal values approximates a continuous measure and behaves well enough. The argument against: this assumes equal spacing between categories, which is rarely true and never guaranteed.

In a CQC context, the safe default is to avoid treating ratings as quantitative – a mean rating of 2.7 is something that can invite subjective interpretation from stakeholders, and it imports an equal-spacing assumption the ratings might not support. The more defensible route is to count the proportion of providers in each category and analyse those proportions, which are themselves quantitative and continuous.

A related technical wrinkle: a percentage built on a small denominator is mathematically continuous but behaves discretely. With 10 patients you can only land on 0%, 10%, 20%, and so on, which breaks methods that assume a continuous distribution. The same caution applies to rates from very small counts (say 0–5 incidents), where the rate will not behave like smooth continuous data.

If a variable simply will not settle into one type, and the choice changes which method is valid, that is a good opportunity for a consultation with the Guild to discuss what the most defensible position might be.
