9  Quality Assurance Principles

TipLearning outcomes

After reading this chapter you will be able to:

  • place CQC’s QASQA Framework within its professional context – the Code of Practice for Statistics’ pillars of Trustworthiness, Quality, and Value, and the AQuA Book’s guidance on proportionate analytical assurance;
  • distinguish the four analytical roles – commissioner, analyst, assurer, and approver – and articulate why independence between analyst and assurer is not merely preferable but essential to the protective function of QA;
  • explain the five key areas of the framework – the exemplifying questions and indicators that give each area its practical content – and articulate what each demands of those roles as a piece of work moves through the analytical lifecycle;
  • apply proportionate thinking to your own work: scaling documentation and review depth to the risk and complexity of each analysis rather than treating QA as a single fixed burden;
  • recognise the common failure modes the framework is designed to catch – from vague scoping and unjustified method selection to undocumented analytical choices and overstated conclusions.

9.1 Credibility and the Analytical Obligation

The 2024 independent review of CQC’s operational effectiveness, led by Dame Penelope Dash, raised concerns about inconsistency in how analytical methods were described and how evidence categories were applied. Those concerns were not merely technical. They went to whether CQC’s regulatory conclusions could be traced back to the analysis claimed to support them, and whether that analysis could survive scrutiny – from providers who might challenge ratings, from Parliament, or from the courts. When methods are not documented, chosen without justification, or used to produce conclusions more certain than the evidence warrants, the analysis becomes impossible to defend and difficult to improve.

Quality assurance is the professional response to that risk. The Code of Practice for Statistics, maintained by the UK Statistics Authority, frames the obligation in terms of three pillars: Trustworthiness, Quality, and Value. Trustworthiness concerns the governance, independence, and integrity of the people and organisations producing statistics. Quality concerns the rigour of the methods, the accuracy of the outputs, and the assurance processes that test them – the QASQA framework addresses this pillar directly through Standard 6 (Be rigorous: use suitable data sources and sound methods) and Standard 7 (Be open about quality: prominently explain strengths, limitations, and uncertainty in estimates). Value concerns whether statistics serve the decisions they are meant to serve, communicated so they are easy to use and understandable (Standard 9: Be clear) and accessible (Standard 10). All three pillars apply to quantitative work at CQC; it is Quality and Value that the QASQA framework most directly operationalises.

Quality assurance is not bureaucracy and it is not a final sign-off ritual. It is a discipline of thinking, built into the analytical process from the first conversation with a commissioner to the moment analysis is accepted and signed off. This chapter explains the governing logic of the QASQA framework. Some helpful checklists and method-specific requirements are in the individual methods chapters; the full framework document can be found here.

Note🤔 Rethinking: The Code of Practice and Why It Applies to Us

The Code of Practice for Statistics is maintained by the Office for Statistics Regulation (OSR), the regulatory arm of the UK Statistics Authority. It sets the standards that producers of official statistics – government departments, regulators, and arm’s-length bodies – must meet to deserve public trust. The OSR assesses organisations against the Code and, where standards are not met, can withdraw accreditation – a serious reputational and regulatory consequence.

CQC holds a portfolio of accredited official statistics – the highest designation for official statistics in the UK. Accreditation is not a fixed status: it is maintained by ongoing compliance with the Code, and the OSR monitors compliance actively. The requirement is therefore a live professional obligation for everyone involved in producing those statistics, not a one-time certification.

Beyond the formal requirement, the Code provides a genuinely useful framework for good practice. Its insistence on transparency about limitations, consistency in methods, and honest communication of uncertainty are the same qualities that make statistics defensible in regulatory proceedings. Producers who treat the Code as a checklist miss the point; those who internalise its logic produce better statistics and are better placed to explain and defend them.

The QASQA Framework is CQC’s primary instrument for meeting the Quality pillar of the Code at the level of individual analytical projects. It does not operate in isolation: Trustworthiness is addressed through governance structures, and Value through how analytical work is communicated – both covered in this chapter.

9.2 The Analytical Lifecycle and the Five Key Areas

The AQuA Book – the UK Government’s standing guidance on analytical quality assurance – frames analytical quality as a property of the whole cycle, not a single gate at the end. CQC’s QASQA Framework maps its five key areas directly onto this cycle. The alignment is deliberate: the AQuA Book represents the settled cross-government consensus on what analytical quality assurance requires, built from experience across departments with widely differing analytical contexts. Aligning CQC’s framework to it means our practice can be compared against, and if necessary defended against, that common standard; it also means CQC analysts can draw on a growing body of guidance and case studies developed around the same framework.

Problems originate at specific stages: a vague brief cannot be rescued by careful execution; a method chosen without examining its assumptions will produce misleading results regardless of how correctly it is coded; conclusions overstated at delivery cannot be quietly corrected in the technical annex. Each stage sets up the next, and quality built in at each stage costs far less than quality inspected in at the end.

The framework is also a practical template: teams complete it by mapping their existing processes and documentation to each key area, so the assurer has a coherent view of the work without having to trace evidence across unfamiliar materials – increasing transparency and creating an audit trail that supports accountability when findings are challenged.

The five key areas are not a checklist to be ticked once the analysis is done; they are a frame for thinking at each stage of the work. Each area has an exemplifying question and a set of indicators that aid assessment, giving concrete form to what addressing the area requires. All five must be addressed for every piece of quantitative analysis that informs a regulatory decision. Which indicators within each area apply, and how thoroughly they are documented, depends on the risk and complexity of the work – that calibration is the subject of the proportionality section below.

Key area Exemplifying question
Engagement and Scoping Has the requirement been properly understood and the analytical approach specified?
Design Is the analytical design defensible – method suited to both data and decision?
Analysis Has the work been executed correctly, verified, validated, and documented?
Delivery and Communication Have methods, results, and limitations been communicated clearly and appropriately to their audience?
Fitness for Purpose Does the finished analysis deliver what was needed, supported by sound methods and credible evidence?

9.3 Roles and Responsibilities

Quality assurance requires people to carry distinct accountabilities across the analytical cycle. The framework assigns four roles, adapted from the AQuA Book, whose responsibilities must be agreed before work begins. These roles are common across analytical quality frameworks; what varies between frameworks is how the specific standards within each role are defined. Understanding the roles before engaging with the five key areas is important, because the indicators and exemplifying questions within each area are framed in terms of what analyst, assurer, and approver must each do at every stage.

The key structural principle is the independence of the assurer from the analyst: these two roles must be held by different people, because the assurer’s protective function exists only where they are genuinely separate from the work they are reviewing.

The commissioner defines the need. They set out the analytical requirements, agree that the proposed approach will meet those requirements before work starts, and accept the analysis and assurance when the work is complete. At CQC, commissioners often act on behalf of end-users – a director commissioning analysis to support inspectors carries responsibility for representing end-user needs in the brief, not just their own. Vague or shifting requirements from commissioners are a leading source of analytical failure; the scoping stage exists precisely to pin these down before they can cascade into the analysis.

The analyst designs and executes the analysis. Design includes the assurance plan: from the outset, the analyst is responsible for thinking through how the work will be verified and validated, not treating QA as something to arrange after the findings are ready. The analyst also self-assures their own work – checking their code, verifying key calculations, assessing whether the results make substantive sense – before the work goes to the assurer. Self-assurance is the first line of quality control, not an optional extra – but it cannot be the only one. The assurer exists precisely because even careful analysts have blind spots, and because independence creates a form of accountability that self-review cannot.

The assurer independently reviews the analyst’s work. Independent means not involved in the analysis: a colleague who sat in on analytical discussions, helped with a code section, or informally reviewed a draft during its development does not occupy the assurer role as the framework defines it. The assurer reviews the analyst’s self-assurance, undertakes further verification and validation as appropriate, reports errors and areas for improvement back to the analyst, and confirms to the approver that the work is sound. This confirmation carries weight only because the assurer is genuinely independent.

The approver provides final accountability. They scrutinise the work of analyst and assurer together and confirm that it has been appropriately assured. This is a substantive review, not a ceremonial one; the approver’s sign-off carries organisational accountability for the quality of the output.

Full independence – where the assurer has had no involvement in the analytical work – is the ideal, and for high-stakes work it must be pursued seriously. In practice, capacity constraints, the niche nature of some analyses, and other external pressures mean complete independence is not always achievable. The response is proportionate: the simpler the work and the lower the stakes, the looser the independence requirement can reasonably be; the more complex and high-stakes, the stricter it must be. What is never appropriate is treating self-assurance alone as sufficient for work where an error could have material regulatory consequences.

9.4 The Five Key Areas

The sections below take each key area in turn, describing the thinking it demands, the practical work it calls for, and the warning signs that suggest a stage has been skipped or rushed.

Engagement and Scoping

The first key area covers how well the requirements have been understood and the analytical approach specified. Most analytical problems begin here, at the moment of first contact between a commissioner’s need and an analyst’s capabilities, and most serious failures can be traced to this stage being rushed or left implicit.

Good scoping is not just writing down a research question. It means arriving at a shared, precise understanding of what decision the analysis will inform, which providers, populations, or time periods are in scope, which data sources will be used, and what level of QA is appropriate for the risk involved. Assumptions and limitations belong in the scoping conversation, not the conclusion: if a method requires a data quality that may not be achievable, that is not a problem to disclose at delivery – it is a constraint to surface before the analytical plan is agreed. Commissioners who understand the method’s assumptions can make informed decisions about whether to proceed; commissioners who discover limitations at the end cannot.

The scoping stage also establishes the assurance plan itself: who will review, at what points, and with what documentation. QA is not a stage that follows analysis; it is designed in at the start. Commissioners and analysts must agree on what is being delivered before work begins. This is the moment that surfaces misaligned expectations before they propagate into the work.

Red flags: an analytical question too vague to operationalise; no discussion of limitations or key assumptions before work begins; the analyst uncertain about how findings will be used; no agreement on QA level, timeline, or review responsibilities.

Design

The second key area covers how defensible the analytical design is. Design is where method meets question, and the AQuA Book’s standard is that the method must suit both the data and the decision – not be merely available, not be merely familiar, but genuinely appropriate. Every statistical technique makes assumptions about the data to which it is applied: a method that assumes independence when records are clustered by provider, or normality when the distribution is heavily skewed, produces results that might appear technically correct while being substantively misleading.

Surfacing and examining those assumptions is design work, not something to be deferred until after the analysis is run. The same applies to the data quality questions that affect method choice: what missing data exists and why, whether unusual observations are errors or genuine extremes, whether data sources are comparable across the time period in question. These are not details – they are the conditions under which the method’s assumptions are either met or violated, and they must be assessed before the design is finalised.

Reproducibility is also a design decision. If code will not be version-controlled, if analytical decisions will not be logged, if the data extraction will not be documented, it will prove difficult, if at all possible, to independently replicate the analysis or defend it if challenged. High-risk work in particular – where findings may be scrutinised, where methods are novel, or where the same analysis will be run repeatedly – must be designed with an audit trail from the outset, not retrofitted with one when review arrives.

Note🤔 Rethinking: Researcher Degrees of Freedom

Every analysis involves decisions the design does not fully settle: which variables to include, how to handle unusual observations, what transformations to apply, which model to use when more than one is defensible. These researcher degrees of freedom are not a methodological failure – they are an inevitable feature of applied statistical work. The problem is not the choices themselves but their invisibility.

Invisible choices create two problems. Different analysts making different but defensible choices from the same data can reach different conclusions. And invisibility creates conditions for unconscious bias: the natural tendency to find a path that produces an expected, hoped-for, or convenient result is checked only by the requirement to justify decisions in writing as they are made, not after the results are known.

The remedy is documentation: record each choice as you make it, not after the analysis is complete; justify it explicitly; and for choices that materially affect the findings, test sensitivity to alternatives and report the results. For high-stakes work, pre-specifying the analytical approach before examining the outcome data limits the scope for those degrees of freedom to be exercised unconsciously. Peer review of analytical choices – separate from code review – catches justifications that look reasonable in isolation but would not survive fresh scrutiny.

Red flags: method chosen by habit rather than by fit to the question and data; assumptions not examined; no plan for missing data or unusual observations; no design provision for reproducibility.

Analysis

The third key area covers how well the quantitative analysis has been executed and assured. The core discipline is straightforward: implement as designed, verify the calculations, validate that the results make sense, and document any departures from the plan. In practice, verification and validation address different failure modes and both are needed.

Verification asks whether the calculation is correct. It catches technical errors – miscoded variables, copy-and-paste mistakes, software bugs, formula errors. For scripted analysis in R or Python, code review by a colleague who was not involved in writing the code provides a second pair of eyes; automated testing provides systematic checks; replicating key results by an independent route provides the strongest form of assurance. Verification is not optional for high-stakes work even when the analyst is confident: confidence is not evidence of correctness.

Validation asks whether the result makes sense. It catches substantive errors – results that look technically correct but are implausible, that contradict previous findings without explanation, or that fail basic consistency checks. Comparing findings with previous estimates, with results from a simpler approximation, or with qualitative knowledge from the inspection record are all forms of validation. A technically perfect calculation of the wrong quantity passes verification and fails validation; both checks are needed precisely because they guard against different kinds of failure.

Every analysis involves choices that the design does not fully settle: which observations to exclude, how to handle an unexpected pattern of missing data, what to do when an assumption is borderline rather than clearly met. These choices are legitimate, but they are also exposure points – different defensible choices can lead to different conclusions from the same data. The response is not to eliminate judgement but to document it: record what was decided and why, and for choices that materially affect the findings, test sensitivity to alternatives and report the results.

Red flags: no code review or independent calculation check; results not compared with prior estimates or domain expectations; assumptions violated but not addressed; departures from the analytical plan unrecorded.

Note🤔 Rethinking: What the Penny Dash Review Was Really About

The 2024 independent review of CQC’s operational effectiveness raised concerns about how analytical methods were described, how evidence standards were applied across teams, and whether analytical decisions were adequately documented. Each concern maps directly onto a stage of the framework.

Unclear method description is a design and delivery problem: if methods cannot be explained, they probably were not examined carefully enough to be defensible. Inconsistent evidence standards are a scoping and fitness-for-purpose problem: if the standard varies without reason across teams or analyses, the regulatory conclusions built on them are not comparable. Inadequate documentation is an analysis and reproducibility problem: if decisions are not recorded, the analysis cannot be reproduced, reviewed, or improved.

The QASQA Framework addresses all three – not by adding paperwork, but by embedding the questions at each stage of the analytical cycle rather than asking them retrospectively. Document as you go, not as an afterthought: it is faster, more accurate, and far easier to defend.

Delivery and Communication

The fourth key area covers how clearly and appropriately methods, results, and limitations are communicated. Good analysis that is not understood by its audience is not good analysis, and the Code of Practice’s Value pillar treats accessibility as a professional standard, not an aspiration.

The practical challenge in CQC’s regulatory context is that audiences vary – and that variation demands genuine adaptation, not just simpler sentences. Inspectors making rating decisions, Board members assessing organisational risk, and providers receiving public-facing reports all need different registers, different levels of technical detail, and different entry points into the uncertainty. Communicating statistical uncertainty across different levels of statistical literacy is a distinct analytical skill, and one worth developing deliberately. The language of confidence intervals, p-values, and sensitivity analyses makes no contact with a reader who lacks statistical training; badly translated, it actively misleads. An inspector needs to understand what the evidence does and cannot support; a Board member needs a different framing of the same uncertainty; a provider needs plain language they can act on. None of these requires hiding the uncertainty – but all require expressing it in terms the reader can use. Investing in this translation is not a soft skill; it is a condition of the analysis doing its job.

In each case, the obligation is to communicate honestly – which means communicating uncertainty alongside point estimates, limitations alongside conclusions, and what the analysis cannot tell you alongside what it can. Confidence intervals, caveats about missing data, qualifications about causal interpretation – these are not failures to be buried in technical appendices but necessary context for any reader trying to act on findings responsibly.

Equally, uncertainty should not be communicated in a way that implies the analysis is worthless. The task is calibrated honesty: match the language of confidence to the strength of the evidence, neither overstating certainty nor manufacturing doubt. “We observe a consistent pattern across three years of data, though we cannot rule out confounding from changes in recording practice” is more useful than either a bald assertion or a long list of qualifications that leaves the reader with nothing actionable.

Visualisation is part of communication, and the same standards apply. Charts should clarify rather than obscure, should not imply more precision than the data supports, and should be accessible to readers with colour-vision differences. The principles in Data Visualisation develop the detailed guidance for CQC contexts.

Delivery is also the moment at which the original question, agreed at scoping, is answered. If scope drifted during the analysis, or if the question evolved, the gap between what was commissioned and what is being delivered must be acknowledged rather than papered over with an answer to a related but different question.

Red flags: methods described in jargon without explanation appropriate to the audience; uncertainty communicated identically regardless of the audience’s statistical literacy; limitations omitted or buried at the end; findings presented as more certain than the methods justify.

Fitness for Purpose

The final key area steps back to assess whether the analysis does what it set out to do: whether the findings meet the objectives, are supported by the methods and data, are coherent with other available knowledge, and are caveated in proportion to the strength of the evidence. This is not a formality. It is the check that catches accumulated drift – where every individual decision along the way was reasonable but the overall product has moved away from what was needed, or where correct findings are presented with stronger conclusions than the evidence supports.

Credibility here has a specific meaning. Findings should be supported not just by the analysis at hand but by other available evidence, by expert judgement, and by consistency with what is already known. Internal coherence – do the pieces fit together into a consistent picture? – is a minimum. Coherence with other evidence – do related indicators tell the same story? do inspection findings align? – is the stronger test. A result that is internally coherent but contradicts inspection experience without explanation is not yet credible in the regulatory sense, and a regulatory decision built on it is exposed.

Reproducibility has its final check at this stage: is the documentation sufficient for another analyst to replicate the work? Does the audit trail record the decisions taken, including those that did not work out? This is what makes learning from the analysis possible, and what makes a credible defence of it available if the analysis is challenged.

The strength of conclusions must be matched to the strength of evidence. The language of delivery – “this suggests”, “we observe a consistent pattern”, “we cannot rule out” – should reflect what the analysis actually supports, not what stakeholders might prefer to hear.

Red flags: findings have drifted from the original question; results inconsistent with other evidence without explanation; conclusions stronger than the evidence supports; documentation insufficient for independent replication.

9.5 Proportionality: Matching QA to Risk

Not all analysis needs the same depth of assurance. A one-off descriptive summary prepared for an internal meeting and a risk model used to target enforcement decisions are both quantitative analyses, but they differ enormously in their potential consequences if wrong. The AQuA Book is explicit that the level of assurance and documentation should be proportionate to complexity and risk, and the QASQA framework applies this logic directly.

Risk assessment should be agreed by the analytical team – analyst, assurer, and commissioner – at project outset, and reviewed at key milestones. The factors that bear on it include the business criticality of the decision the analysis informs; whether quantitative analysis is the primary driver of that decision or one input among many; the statistical complexity of the methods; whether the approach is novel at CQC; whether the analysis will be used repeatedly or adapted to new purposes; the precision the decision requires; and whether findings will be public-facing or may be challenged externally.

Three broad risk levels organise these factors for practical purposes. Low risk covers routine descriptive statistics, internal data queries, and exploratory analysis by experienced teams. QA at this level is lightweight – brief analytical plans, verbal feedback, summary notes – though all five questions must still be addressed. Medium risk covers routine but consequential reporting: inspection outcome analysis, cross-functional benchmarking, established survey methods for decision support. Standard documentation is expected, including specification documents, QA logs, and code review records where scripted analysis is involved. High risk covers analysis that informs policy decisions, will be publicly scrutinised, uses advanced or novel methods, involves sensitive data, or sets methodological precedents. Comprehensive documentation is required across all stages, with a full audit trail; State of Care analysis, risk models for inspection targeting, and first uses of novel techniques all sit here.

When there is doubt, err toward the higher category. The cost of additional assurance is time; the cost of insufficient assurance on a high-stakes analysis is a regulatory decision that cannot be defended.

9.6 A Discipline of Checks Before Analysis Begins

Before applying any statistical method, a brief structured review catches problems that propagate expensively if missed. The five checks below run in sequence – they follow the order in which information is gathered and assumptions are built – and together cover the commonest sources of downstream failure.

To make them concrete, consider a running example: you are about to analyse quarterly waiting times for community mental health referrals across providers, with the aim of identifying those where waits have lengthened materially over the past year.

Data provenance comes first, before any other work. Where did the data come from? When was it extracted, from which system, and by whom? Which version is being used, and is access authorised? For the waiting times analysis: confirm the extraction covers the full twelve-month period and that data were pulled from the same system across all quarters. Using data extracted at different points from systems that were updated between extractions will produce apparent trends that are artefacts of the extraction, not of actual waits. This check takes minutes and prevents hours of debugging when results look implausible.

Data structure follows once provenance is confirmed. What does each row represent? What period does the dataset cover? Are duplicates present, and if so, are they legitimate? For the waiting times analysis: confirm that each row is one referral, not one patient – a patient with multiple referrals should appear multiple times. Confusing the unit of observation – analysing at referral level when the question is about provider-level average waits – produces results that are logically wrong in a way that neither verification nor validation reliably catches, because the code runs and the results look plausible to anyone who does not know the subject matter.

Variable definitions follow once structure is understood. What does each column actually measure? What do the codes mean? How were derived variables calculated? For the waiting times analysis: establish exactly how the waiting time is defined – is it calendar days from referral receipt to first appointment, or working days? Is a rejected and re-referred patient’s clock restarted? Misunderstanding a variable’s definition leads to misinterpretation that neither verification nor validation reliably catches: verification confirms the code is correct, validation confirms the result is plausible, but neither checks that the variable was understood correctly in the first place.

Data quality follows once definitions are clear. Are values within expected ranges? How much is missing, and is the missingness patterned in a way that suggests a problem? For the waiting times analysis: check for negative waits, implausibly long waits that likely reflect data entry errors, and for any quarters where a large proportion of referrals have no recorded outcome date. Stop and investigate when you see: values impossible by definition (negative counts, percentages above 100, future dates in historical records); suspicious uniformity (all round numbers, implausibly many zeros, a distribution too neat to be genuine); internal inconsistencies (numerators larger than denominators, fields that should agree but do not); abrupt distributional shifts between periods not explained by a known event; results that perfectly match expectations (genuine data is noisy, and perfect fit is a warning sign, not a reassurance). Each of these is more likely to indicate a data quality problem than a genuine empirical finding. The Handling Missing Data: Foundations chapter provides the conceptual framework for assessing missingness; Unusual Observations covers investigating extreme values.

Analytical appropriateness is the final check, building on all the preceding ones. Is the question defined precisely enough to answer? Does the method suit the data and the question? Are its assumptions understood and assessable from the available data? Is there enough data to support the level of precision the decision requires? For the waiting times analysis: if some providers have fewer than ten referrals per quarter, changes in their average wait will be so imprecise as to be uninformative, and the analysis should plan in advance how to handle them – excluding very small providers, aggregating to half-year periods, or presenting results with explicit precision caveats.

These questions take minutes to ask before analysis begins. Answering them after an analysis is complete, under time pressure, with the mental commitment already invested in the approach, is far more difficult and far less reliable.

9.7 Multiple Lines of Evidence

A single analysis, however carefully executed, is a single perspective. It can be affected by data quality issues specific to one period or source, by analytical choices, by chance patterns in that particular dataset, or by unmeasured confounding. In a regulatory context, building a case from several independent lines of evidence is what turns a suggestive finding into a defensible conclusion.

In CQC practice, triangulating across independent lines means asking whether a pattern holds up across:

  • time – does it persist across months, quarters, or years, or is it specific to one period?
  • data sources – do different indicators or datasets point the same way, or contradict each other?
  • providers – is it a broad pattern across many providers, or an isolated case?
  • methods – do different analytical approaches reach similar conclusions, or is the result sensitive to which method is chosen?
  • qualitative evidence – do inspection findings and stakeholder intelligence align with the quantitative picture?

Returning to the waiting times example: if z-scoring flags a provider as a statistical outlier on wait times, that flag is a prompt for investigation, not a verdict. Check whether the pattern persists over multiple quarters – the Monitoring Trends Over Time: SPC Basics chapter provides tools for this – whether related indicators point the same way, whether the inspection record aligns, and whether the pattern could be a data-quality artefact. A flag corroborated across several of these lines is far stronger evidence than any single result.

Replication and consistency across multiple lines of evidence support a conclusion more strongly than statistical significance in a single analysis. Use statistics to guide investigation, not to settle it. Where different lines of evidence genuinely conflict, or you are unsure how to weight them, raise the question with the Guild.

9.8 When to Escalate

Most analytical problems in CQC quantitative work can be handled with the methods and judgement of an experienced analyst. Some cannot, and knowing the difference is a professional skill in itself. Reaching out for additional support when in doubt is best practice: it is recognition that the work might require expertise beyond what any individual should be expected to bring alone.

Escalate to the Guild/Practice Lead when:

  • the method is novel at CQC and the analytical stakes are high enough that getting it wrong matters;
  • assumptions are violated and you are uncertain which robust alternatives are appropriate for the situation;
  • it is the first use of a method for a particular regulatory purpose, or the analysis will set a precedent that future work will follow;
  • results contradict previous findings in a way you cannot explain, or are politically or operationally sensitive in ways that call for independent methodological backing;
  • findings do not make substantive sense even after thorough checking, and you cannot identify the source;
  • the challenge requires specialist software or techniques that are beyond your current capacity to acquire under realistic time constraints.

Escalation is not appropriate for routine application of established methods, minor analytical decisions your judgement can handle, or questions this guidance already covers. The timing matters: raise a methodological concern at the design stage, not after the analysis is complete. Raising it at delivery – when there is no time to address it – is disclosure, not escalation.

When you escalate, make it easy to help you: provide context (what decision does this analysis inform, and why does the methodological question matter?), be specific about the challenge, and share data and preliminary work where possible. For formal review of complex or high-risk work, contact the Practice Lead for Quantitative Analytics and Statistics.

## Credibility and the Analytical Obligation {#sec-qa-principles}

The 2024 independent review of CQC's operational effectiveness, led by Dame Penelope Dash, raised concerns about inconsistency in how analytical methods were described and how evidence categories were applied. Those concerns were not merely technical. They went to whether CQC's regulatory conclusions could be traced back to the analysis claimed to support them, and whether that analysis could survive scrutiny – from providers who might challenge ratings, from Parliament, or from the courts. When methods are not documented, chosen without justification, or used to produce conclusions more certain than the evidence warrants, the analysis becomes impossible to defend and difficult to improve.

Quality assurance is the professional response to that risk. The Code of Practice for Statistics, maintained by the UK Statistics Authority, frames the obligation in terms of three pillars: Trustworthiness, Quality, and Value. Trustworthiness concerns the governance, independence, and integrity of the people and organisations producing statistics. Quality concerns the rigour of the methods, the accuracy of the outputs, and the assurance processes that test them – the QASQA framework addresses this pillar directly through Standard 6 (Be rigorous: use suitable data sources and sound methods) and Standard 7 (Be open about quality: prominently explain strengths, limitations, and uncertainty in estimates). Value concerns whether statistics serve the decisions they are meant to serve, communicated so they are easy to use and understandable (Standard 9: Be clear) and accessible (Standard 10). All three pillars apply to quantitative work at CQC; it is Quality and Value that the QASQA framework most directly operationalises.

Quality assurance is not bureaucracy and it is not a final sign-off ritual. It is a discipline of thinking, built into the analytical process from the first conversation with a commissioner to the moment analysis is accepted and signed off. This chapter explains the governing logic of the QASQA framework. Some helpful checklists and method-specific requirements are in the individual methods chapters; the full framework document can be found [here](link_to_be_added).

## 🤔 Rethinking: The Code of Practice and Why It Applies to Us

The **Code of Practice for Statistics** is maintained by the **Office for Statistics Regulation (OSR)**, the regulatory arm of the UK Statistics Authority. It sets the standards that producers of official statistics – government departments, regulators, and arm's-length bodies – must meet to deserve public trust. The OSR assesses organisations against the Code and, where standards are not met, can withdraw accreditation – a serious reputational and regulatory consequence.

CQC holds a portfolio of **accredited official statistics** – the highest designation for official statistics in the UK. Accreditation is not a fixed status: it is maintained by ongoing compliance with the Code, and the OSR monitors compliance actively. The requirement is therefore a live professional obligation for everyone involved in producing those statistics, not a one-time certification.

Beyond the formal requirement, the Code provides a genuinely useful framework for good practice. Its insistence on transparency about limitations, consistency in methods, and honest communication of uncertainty are the same qualities that make statistics defensible in regulatory proceedings. Producers who treat the Code as a checklist miss the point; those who internalise its logic produce better statistics and are better placed to explain and defend them.

The QASQA Framework is CQC's primary instrument for meeting the Quality pillar of the Code at the level of individual analytical projects. It does not operate in isolation: Trustworthiness is addressed through governance structures, and Value through how analytical work is communicated – both covered in this chapter.
:::

## The Analytical Lifecycle and the Five Key Areas

The AQuA Book – the UK Government's standing guidance on analytical quality assurance – frames analytical quality as a property of the whole cycle, not a single gate at the end. CQC's QASQA Framework maps its five key areas directly onto this cycle. The alignment is deliberate: the AQuA Book represents the settled cross-government consensus on what analytical quality assurance requires, built from experience across departments with widely differing analytical contexts. Aligning CQC's framework to it means our practice can be compared against, and if necessary defended against, that common standard; it also means CQC analysts can draw on a growing body of guidance and case studies developed around the same framework.

Problems originate at specific stages: a vague brief cannot be rescued by careful execution; a method chosen without examining its assumptions will produce misleading results regardless of how correctly it is coded; conclusions overstated at delivery cannot be quietly corrected in the technical annex. Each stage sets up the next, and quality built in at each stage costs far less than quality inspected in at the end.

The framework is also a practical template: teams complete it by mapping their existing processes and documentation to each key area, so the assurer has a coherent view of the work without having to trace evidence across unfamiliar materials – increasing transparency and creating an audit trail that supports accountability when findings are challenged.

The five key areas are not a checklist to be ticked once the analysis is done; they are a frame for thinking at each stage of the work. Each area has an exemplifying question and a set of indicators that aid assessment, giving concrete form to what addressing the area requires. All five must be addressed for every piece of quantitative analysis that informs a regulatory decision. Which indicators within each area apply, and how thoroughly they are documented, depends on the risk and complexity of the work – that calibration is the subject of the proportionality section below.

| Key area | Exemplifying question |
|---|---|
| Engagement and Scoping | Has the requirement been properly understood and the analytical approach specified? |
| Design | Is the analytical design defensible – method suited to both data and decision? |
| Analysis | Has the work been executed correctly, verified, validated, and documented? |
| Delivery and Communication | Have methods, results, and limitations been communicated clearly and appropriately to their audience? |
| Fitness for Purpose | Does the finished analysis deliver what was needed, supported by sound methods and credible evidence? |

## Roles and Responsibilities

Quality assurance requires people to carry distinct accountabilities across the analytical cycle. The framework assigns four roles, adapted from the AQuA Book, whose responsibilities must be agreed before work begins. These roles are common across analytical quality frameworks; what varies between frameworks is how the specific standards within each role are defined. Understanding the roles before engaging with the five key areas is important, because the indicators and exemplifying questions within each area are framed in terms of what analyst, assurer, and approver must each do at every stage.

The key structural principle is the independence of the assurer from the analyst: these two roles must be held by different people, because the assurer's protective function exists only where they are genuinely separate from the work they are reviewing.

The **commissioner** defines the need. They set out the analytical requirements, agree that the proposed approach will meet those requirements before work starts, and accept the analysis and assurance when the work is complete. At CQC, commissioners often act on behalf of end-users – a director commissioning analysis to support inspectors carries responsibility for representing end-user needs in the brief, not just their own. Vague or shifting requirements from commissioners are a leading source of analytical failure; the scoping stage exists precisely to pin these down before they can cascade into the analysis.

The **analyst** designs and executes the analysis. Design includes the assurance plan: from the outset, the analyst is responsible for thinking through how the work will be verified and validated, not treating QA as something to arrange after the findings are ready. The analyst also self-assures their own work – checking their code, verifying key calculations, assessing whether the results make substantive sense – before the work goes to the assurer. Self-assurance is the first line of quality control, not an optional extra – but it cannot be the only one. The assurer exists precisely because even careful analysts have blind spots, and because independence creates a form of accountability that self-review cannot.

The **assurer** independently reviews the analyst's work. Independent means not involved in the analysis: a colleague who sat in on analytical discussions, helped with a code section, or informally reviewed a draft during its development does not occupy the assurer role as the framework defines it. The assurer reviews the analyst's self-assurance, undertakes further verification and validation as appropriate, reports errors and areas for improvement back to the analyst, and confirms to the approver that the work is sound. This confirmation carries weight only because the assurer is genuinely independent.

The **approver** provides final accountability. They scrutinise the work of analyst and assurer together and confirm that it has been appropriately assured. This is a substantive review, not a ceremonial one; the approver's sign-off carries organisational accountability for the quality of the output.

Full independence – where the assurer has had no involvement in the analytical work – is the ideal, and for high-stakes work it must be pursued seriously. In practice, capacity constraints, the niche nature of some analyses, and other external pressures mean complete independence is not always achievable. The response is proportionate: the simpler the work and the lower the stakes, the looser the independence requirement can reasonably be; the more complex and high-stakes, the stricter it must be. What is never appropriate is treating self-assurance alone as sufficient for work where an error could have material regulatory consequences.

## The Five Key Areas

The sections below take each key area in turn, describing the thinking it demands, the practical work it calls for, and the warning signs that suggest a stage has been skipped or rushed.

### Engagement and Scoping

The first key area covers how well the requirements have been understood and the analytical approach specified. Most analytical problems begin here, at the moment of first contact between a commissioner's need and an analyst's capabilities, and most serious failures can be traced to this stage being rushed or left implicit.

Good scoping is not just writing down a research question. It means arriving at a shared, precise understanding of what decision the analysis will inform, which providers, populations, or time periods are in scope, which data sources will be used, and what level of QA is appropriate for the risk involved. Assumptions and limitations belong in the scoping conversation, not the conclusion: if a method requires a data quality that may not be achievable, that is not a problem to disclose at delivery – it is a constraint to surface before the analytical plan is agreed. Commissioners who understand the method's assumptions can make informed decisions about whether to proceed; commissioners who discover limitations at the end cannot.

The scoping stage also establishes the assurance plan itself: who will review, at what points, and with what documentation. QA is not a stage that follows analysis; it is designed in at the start. Commissioners and analysts must agree on what is being delivered before work begins. This is the moment that surfaces misaligned expectations before they propagate into the work.

_Red flags_: an analytical question too vague to operationalise; no discussion of limitations or key assumptions before work begins; the analyst uncertain about how findings will be used; no agreement on QA level, timeline, or review responsibilities.

### Design

The second key area covers how defensible the analytical design is. Design is where method meets question, and the AQuA Book's standard is that the method must suit both the data and the decision – not be merely available, not be merely familiar, but genuinely appropriate. Every statistical technique makes assumptions about the data to which it is applied: a method that assumes independence when records are clustered by provider, or normality when the distribution is heavily skewed, produces results that might appear technically correct while being substantively misleading.

Surfacing and examining those assumptions is design work, not something to be deferred until after the analysis is run. The same applies to the data quality questions that affect method choice: what missing data exists and why, whether unusual observations are errors or genuine extremes, whether data sources are comparable across the time period in question. These are not details – they are the conditions under which the method's assumptions are either met or violated, and they must be assessed before the design is finalised.

Reproducibility is also a design decision. If code will not be version-controlled, if analytical decisions will not be logged, if the data extraction will not be documented, it will prove difficult, if at all possible, to independently replicate the analysis or defend it if challenged. High-risk work in particular – where findings may be scrutinised, where methods are novel, or where the same analysis will be run repeatedly – must be designed with an audit trail from the outset, not retrofitted with one when review arrives.

## 🤔 Rethinking: Researcher Degrees of Freedom

Every analysis involves decisions the design does not fully settle: which variables to include, how to handle unusual observations, what transformations to apply, which model to use when more than one is defensible. These **researcher degrees of freedom** are not a methodological failure – they are an inevitable feature of applied statistical work. The problem is not the choices themselves but their invisibility.

Invisible choices create two problems. Different analysts making different but defensible choices from the same data can reach different conclusions. And invisibility creates conditions for unconscious bias: the natural tendency to find a path that produces an expected, hoped-for, or convenient result is checked only by the requirement to justify decisions in writing as they are made, not after the results are known.

The remedy is documentation: record each choice as you make it, not after the analysis is complete; justify it explicitly; and for choices that materially affect the findings, test sensitivity to alternatives and report the results. For high-stakes work, pre-specifying the analytical approach before examining the outcome data limits the scope for those degrees of freedom to be exercised unconsciously. Peer review of analytical choices – separate from code review – catches justifications that look reasonable in isolation but would not survive fresh scrutiny.
:::

_Red flags_: method chosen by habit rather than by fit to the question and data; assumptions not examined; no plan for missing data or unusual observations; no design provision for reproducibility.

### Analysis

The third key area covers how well the quantitative analysis has been executed and assured. The core discipline is straightforward: implement as designed, verify the calculations, validate that the results make sense, and document any departures from the plan. In practice, verification and validation address different failure modes and both are needed.

**Verification** asks whether the calculation is correct. It catches technical errors – miscoded variables, copy-and-paste mistakes, software bugs, formula errors. For scripted analysis in R or Python, code review by a colleague who was not involved in writing the code provides a second pair of eyes; automated testing provides systematic checks; replicating key results by an independent route provides the strongest form of assurance. Verification is not optional for high-stakes work even when the analyst is confident: confidence is not evidence of correctness.

**Validation** asks whether the result makes sense. It catches substantive errors – results that look technically correct but are implausible, that contradict previous findings without explanation, or that fail basic consistency checks. Comparing findings with previous estimates, with results from a simpler approximation, or with qualitative knowledge from the inspection record are all forms of validation. A technically perfect calculation of the wrong quantity passes verification and fails validation; both checks are needed precisely because they guard against different kinds of failure.

Every analysis involves choices that the design does not fully settle: which observations to exclude, how to handle an unexpected pattern of missing data, what to do when an assumption is borderline rather than clearly met. These choices are legitimate, but they are also exposure points – different defensible choices can lead to different conclusions from the same data. The response is not to eliminate judgement but to document it: record what was decided and why, and for choices that materially affect the findings, test sensitivity to alternatives and report the results.

_Red flags_: no code review or independent calculation check; results not compared with prior estimates or domain expectations; assumptions violated but not addressed; departures from the analytical plan unrecorded.

::: {.callout-note icon=false}
## 🤔 Rethinking: What the Penny Dash Review Was Really About

The 2024 independent review of CQC's operational effectiveness raised concerns about how analytical methods were described, how evidence standards were applied across teams, and whether analytical decisions were adequately documented. Each concern maps directly onto a stage of the framework.

Unclear method description is a design and delivery problem: if methods cannot be explained, they probably were not examined carefully enough to be defensible. Inconsistent evidence standards are a scoping and fitness-for-purpose problem: if the standard varies without reason across teams or analyses, the regulatory conclusions built on them are not comparable. Inadequate documentation is an analysis and reproducibility problem: if decisions are not recorded, the analysis cannot be reproduced, reviewed, or improved.

The QASQA Framework addresses all three – not by adding paperwork, but by embedding the questions at each stage of the analytical cycle rather than asking them retrospectively. Document as you go, not as an afterthought: it is faster, more accurate, and far easier to defend.
:::

### Delivery and Communication

The fourth key area covers how clearly and appropriately methods, results, and limitations are communicated. Good analysis that is not understood by its audience is not good analysis, and the Code of Practice's Value pillar treats accessibility as a professional standard, not an aspiration.

The practical challenge in CQC's regulatory context is that audiences vary – and that variation demands genuine adaptation, not just simpler sentences. Inspectors making rating decisions, Board members assessing organisational risk, and providers receiving public-facing reports all need different registers, different levels of technical detail, and different entry points into the uncertainty. Communicating statistical uncertainty across different levels of statistical literacy is a distinct analytical skill, and one worth developing deliberately. The language of confidence intervals, p-values, and sensitivity analyses makes no contact with a reader who lacks statistical training; badly translated, it actively misleads. An inspector needs to understand what the evidence does and cannot support; a Board member needs a different framing of the same uncertainty; a provider needs plain language they can act on. None of these requires hiding the uncertainty – but all require expressing it in terms the reader can use. Investing in this translation is not a soft skill; it is a condition of the analysis doing its job.

In each case, the obligation is to communicate honestly – which means communicating uncertainty alongside point estimates, limitations alongside conclusions, and what the analysis cannot tell you alongside what it can. Confidence intervals, caveats about missing data, qualifications about causal interpretation – these are not failures to be buried in technical appendices but necessary context for any reader trying to act on findings responsibly.

Equally, uncertainty should not be communicated in a way that implies the analysis is worthless. The task is calibrated honesty: match the language of confidence to the strength of the evidence, neither overstating certainty nor manufacturing doubt. "We observe a consistent pattern across three years of data, though we cannot rule out confounding from changes in recording practice" is more useful than either a bald assertion or a long list of qualifications that leaves the reader with nothing actionable.

Visualisation is part of communication, and the same standards apply. Charts should clarify rather than obscure, should not imply more precision than the data supports, and should be accessible to readers with colour-vision differences. The principles in [Data Visualisation](02-data-visualisation.qmd) develop the detailed guidance for CQC contexts.

Delivery is also the moment at which the original question, agreed at scoping, is answered. If scope drifted during the analysis, or if the question evolved, the gap between what was commissioned and what is being delivered must be acknowledged rather than papered over with an answer to a related but different question.

_Red flags_: methods described in jargon without explanation appropriate to the audience; uncertainty communicated identically regardless of the audience's statistical literacy; limitations omitted or buried at the end; findings presented as more certain than the methods justify.

### Fitness for Purpose

The final key area steps back to assess whether the analysis does what it set out to do: whether the findings meet the objectives, are supported by the methods and data, are coherent with other available knowledge, and are caveated in proportion to the strength of the evidence. This is not a formality. It is the check that catches accumulated drift – where every individual decision along the way was reasonable but the overall product has moved away from what was needed, or where correct findings are presented with stronger conclusions than the evidence supports.

Credibility here has a specific meaning. Findings should be supported not just by the analysis at hand but by other available evidence, by expert judgement, and by consistency with what is already known. Internal coherence – do the pieces fit together into a consistent picture? – is a minimum. Coherence with other evidence – do related indicators tell the same story? do inspection findings align? – is the stronger test. A result that is internally coherent but contradicts inspection experience without explanation is not yet credible in the regulatory sense, and a regulatory decision built on it is exposed.

Reproducibility has its final check at this stage: is the documentation sufficient for another analyst to replicate the work? Does the audit trail record the decisions taken, including those that did not work out? This is what makes learning from the analysis possible, and what makes a credible defence of it available if the analysis is challenged.

The strength of conclusions must be matched to the strength of evidence. The language of delivery – "this suggests", "we observe a consistent pattern", "we cannot rule out" – should reflect what the analysis actually supports, not what stakeholders might prefer to hear.

_Red flags_: findings have drifted from the original question; results inconsistent with other evidence without explanation; conclusions stronger than the evidence supports; documentation insufficient for independent replication.

## Proportionality: Matching QA to Risk

Not all analysis needs the same depth of assurance. A one-off descriptive summary prepared for an internal meeting and a risk model used to target enforcement decisions are both quantitative analyses, but they differ enormously in their potential consequences if wrong. The AQuA Book is explicit that the level of assurance and documentation should be proportionate to complexity and risk, and the QASQA framework applies this logic directly.

Risk assessment should be agreed by the analytical team – analyst, assurer, and commissioner – at project outset, and reviewed at key milestones. The factors that bear on it include the business criticality of the decision the analysis informs; whether quantitative analysis is the primary driver of that decision or one input among many; the statistical complexity of the methods; whether the approach is novel at CQC; whether the analysis will be used repeatedly or adapted to new purposes; the precision the decision requires; and whether findings will be public-facing or may be challenged externally.

Three broad risk levels organise these factors for practical purposes. _Low risk_ covers routine descriptive statistics, internal data queries, and exploratory analysis by experienced teams. QA at this level is lightweight – brief analytical plans, verbal feedback, summary notes – though all five questions must still be addressed. _Medium risk_ covers routine but consequential reporting: inspection outcome analysis, cross-functional benchmarking, established survey methods for decision support. Standard documentation is expected, including specification documents, QA logs, and code review records where scripted analysis is involved. _High risk_ covers analysis that informs policy decisions, will be publicly scrutinised, uses advanced or novel methods, involves sensitive data, or sets methodological precedents. Comprehensive documentation is required across all stages, with a full audit trail; State of Care analysis, risk models for inspection targeting, and first uses of novel techniques all sit here.

When there is doubt, err toward the higher category. The cost of additional assurance is time; the cost of insufficient assurance on a high-stakes analysis is a regulatory decision that cannot be defended.

## A Discipline of Checks Before Analysis Begins

Before applying any statistical method, a brief structured review catches problems that propagate expensively if missed. The five checks below run in sequence – they follow the order in which information is gathered and assumptions are built – and together cover the commonest sources of downstream failure.

To make them concrete, consider a running example: you are about to analyse quarterly waiting times for community mental health referrals across providers, with the aim of identifying those where waits have lengthened materially over the past year.

_Data provenance_ comes first, before any other work. Where did the data come from? When was it extracted, from which system, and by whom? Which version is being used, and is access authorised? For the waiting times analysis: confirm the extraction covers the full twelve-month period and that data were pulled from the same system across all quarters. Using data extracted at different points from systems that were updated between extractions will produce apparent trends that are artefacts of the extraction, not of actual waits. This check takes minutes and prevents hours of debugging when results look implausible.

_Data structure_ follows once provenance is confirmed. What does each row represent? What period does the dataset cover? Are duplicates present, and if so, are they legitimate? For the waiting times analysis: confirm that each row is one referral, not one patient – a patient with multiple referrals should appear multiple times. Confusing the unit of observation – analysing at referral level when the question is about provider-level average waits – produces results that are logically wrong in a way that neither verification nor validation reliably catches, because the code runs and the results look plausible to anyone who does not know the subject matter.

_Variable definitions_ follow once structure is understood. What does each column actually measure? What do the codes mean? How were derived variables calculated? For the waiting times analysis: establish exactly how the waiting time is defined – is it calendar days from referral receipt to first appointment, or working days? Is a rejected and re-referred patient's clock restarted? Misunderstanding a variable's definition leads to misinterpretation that neither verification nor validation reliably catches: verification confirms the code is correct, validation confirms the result is plausible, but neither checks that the variable was understood correctly in the first place.

_Data quality_ follows once definitions are clear. Are values within expected ranges? How much is missing, and is the missingness patterned in a way that suggests a problem? For the waiting times analysis: check for negative waits, implausibly long waits that likely reflect data entry errors, and for any quarters where a large proportion of referrals have no recorded outcome date. Stop and investigate when you see: values impossible by definition (negative counts, percentages above 100, future dates in historical records); suspicious uniformity (all round numbers, implausibly many zeros, a distribution too neat to be genuine); internal inconsistencies (numerators larger than denominators, fields that should agree but do not); abrupt distributional shifts between periods not explained by a known event; results that perfectly match expectations (genuine data is noisy, and perfect fit is a warning sign, not a reassurance). Each of these is more likely to indicate a data quality problem than a genuine empirical finding. The [Handling Missing Data: Foundations](08-missing-data-foundations.qmd) chapter provides the conceptual framework for assessing missingness; [Unusual Observations](06-unusual-observations.qmd) covers investigating extreme values.

_Analytical appropriateness_ is the final check, building on all the preceding ones. Is the question defined precisely enough to answer? Does the method suit the data and the question? Are its assumptions understood and assessable from the available data? Is there enough data to support the level of precision the decision requires? For the waiting times analysis: if some providers have fewer than ten referrals per quarter, changes in their average wait will be so imprecise as to be uninformative, and the analysis should plan in advance how to handle them – excluding very small providers, aggregating to half-year periods, or presenting results with explicit precision caveats.

These questions take minutes to ask before analysis begins. Answering them after an analysis is complete, under time pressure, with the mental commitment already invested in the approach, is far more difficult and far less reliable.

## Multiple Lines of Evidence

A single analysis, however carefully executed, is a single perspective. It can be affected by data quality issues specific to one period or source, by analytical choices, by chance patterns in that particular dataset, or by unmeasured confounding. In a regulatory context, building a case from several independent lines of evidence is what turns a suggestive finding into a defensible conclusion.

In CQC practice, triangulating across independent lines means asking whether a pattern holds up across:

- time – does it persist across months, quarters, or years, or is it specific to one period?
- data sources – do different indicators or datasets point the same way, or contradict each other?
- providers – is it a broad pattern across many providers, or an isolated case?
- methods – do different analytical approaches reach similar conclusions, or is the result sensitive to which method is chosen?
- qualitative evidence – do inspection findings and stakeholder intelligence align with the quantitative picture?

Returning to the waiting times example: if z-scoring flags a provider as a statistical outlier on wait times, that flag is a prompt for investigation, not a verdict. Check whether the pattern persists over multiple quarters – the [Monitoring Trends Over Time: SPC Basics](13-spc-basics.qmd) chapter provides tools for this – whether related indicators point the same way, whether the inspection record aligns, and whether the pattern could be a data-quality artefact. A flag corroborated across several of these lines is far stronger evidence than any single result.

Replication and consistency across multiple lines of evidence support a conclusion more strongly than statistical significance in a single analysis. Use statistics to guide investigation, not to settle it. Where different lines of evidence genuinely conflict, or you are unsure how to weight them, raise the question with the Guild.

## When to Escalate

Most analytical problems in CQC quantitative work can be handled with the methods and judgement of an experienced analyst. Some cannot, and knowing the difference is a professional skill in itself. Reaching out for additional support when in doubt is best practice: it is recognition that the work might require expertise beyond what any individual should be expected to bring alone.

Escalate to the Guild/Practice Lead when:

- the method is novel at CQC and the analytical stakes are high enough that getting it wrong matters;
- assumptions are violated and you are uncertain which robust alternatives are appropriate for the situation;
- it is the first use of a method for a particular regulatory purpose, or the analysis will set a precedent that future work will follow;
- results contradict previous findings in a way you cannot explain, or are politically or operationally sensitive in ways that call for independent methodological backing;
- findings do not make substantive sense even after thorough checking, and you cannot identify the source;
- the challenge requires specialist software or techniques that are beyond your current capacity to acquire under realistic time constraints.

Escalation is not appropriate for routine application of established methods, minor analytical decisions your judgement can handle, or questions this guidance already covers. The timing matters: raise a methodological concern at the design stage, not after the analysis is complete. Raising it at delivery – when there is no time to address it – is disclosure, not escalation.

When you escalate, make it easy to help you: provide context (what decision does this analysis inform, and why does the methodological question matter?), be specific about the challenge, and share data and preliminary work where possible. For formal review of complex or high-risk work, contact the Practice Lead for Quantitative Analytics and Statistics.
