16  Outlier Detection Methods

TipWhat this is for

You need to find the observations that stand apart from the rest of a dataset – systematically, reproducibly, and at a scale where eyeballing every value is impossible. For example:

  • Which of 350 care homes report staffing ratios extreme enough to check before the main analysis?
  • Which waiting-time submissions look like unit errors rather than long waits?
  • Which providers should an analyst look at first, when there is only time to look at some?

Detection methods turn “that looks odd” into stated, repeatable rules – and combining several rules turns a pile of flags into an ordered investigation queue.

16.1 From Lens to Rule

The chapter on unusual observations did the thinking: “unusual” has no universal definition; a value stands out for one of three reasons (a data error, a genuine extreme, or mixed populations) and each calls for a different response; the line you draw is a choice to declare, not a fact to discover; and the default treatment is to investigate and keep, never to silently delete. This chapter assumes all of that and adds the machinery – the rules that apply those lenses to eight hundred providers as readily as to eight, identically every time they run.

Mechanising the judgement matters for exactly the reasons hand-inspection fails. A rule is reproducible: a colleague running your code flags the same providers, which is what makes a flagged list defensible when a provider asks why they are on it. A rule scales: national datasets cannot be read point by point. And a rule is declarable in advance, which is the discipline that stops threshold-shopping – the researcher degree of freedom ch06 warned about – before it starts. What a rule is not is a judgement: every method below produces candidates for investigation, nothing more.

16.2 Before You Start

Read the conceptual chapter first. The methods here are easy to run and easy to misuse; unusual observations supplies the questions that make their output meaningful – above all why is this value unusual?, asked before anything is done about it.

Data requirements. A quantitative variable, one value per unit. Twenty or more observations before any of the rules below is worth much – with fewer, the summary statistics the rules are built on are themselves too uncertain.

When these methods are the wrong tool. Values arriving over time belong with statistical process control, which respects the time order these methods ignore. Comparing providers against a benchmark where natural variation depends on size belongs with z-scoring and funnel plots – a small provider’s wild rate is often precision, not deviance, and the methods below do not know the difference. And a clearly two-humped distribution is usually mixed populations: stratify first, or the rules will flag one whole group as outliers from the other.

QA framework alignment. Detection work most directly supports QA question 3 (Analysis – has the data been appropriately checked?) and question 5 (Fitness for Purpose – are results defensible given data quality?). The documentation section below sets out the record to keep.

Worked example for this chapter. Example 10: Care Home Staffing Investigation runs the full multi-method workflow with the investigation and treatment decisions documented end to end.

16.3 Three Detectors, Three Personalities

Each rule answers “how far from the middle is too far?” with different definitions of middle and far – and those choices give each a distinct failure mode worth knowing before trusting any of them.

The classic z-score rule flags values more than a chosen number of standard deviations from the mean – \(|x - \bar{x}|/s\) beyond 2.5, or 3 for a stricter line. It is familiar, quantitative, and carries one structural flaw for this particular job: the mean and especially the standard deviation are computed from the very data being screened, so the outliers being hunted inflate the yardstick used to hunt them. One monstrous value can widen \(s\) enough to hide both itself and every lesser extreme – an effect called masking, and not a rare curiosity: the worked example below shows the classic rule missing every impossibly low value in the dataset for exactly this reason.

The modified z-score (Iglewicz and Hoaglin’s robust replacement) swaps in summaries that extremes cannot corrupt: distance from the median, scaled by the median absolute deviation – the median of the absolute distances from the median, a robust stand-in for the standard deviation:

\[M_i = \frac{0.6745\,(x_i - \tilde{x})}{\text{MAD}}\]

where \(\tilde{x}\) is the median and the 0.6745 calibrates \(M\) to read like a z-score when the data are normal. The conventional flag is \(|M| > 3.5\). Because median and MAD barely move when the extremes do, the yardstick stays honest no matter how wild the values being screened – the property the classic rule lacks. (One implementation trap with library MAD functions is real and lurking; the Overthinking box below defuses it.)

Tukey’s fences – the IQR rule, the one box plots apply silently – flags anything below \(Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR}\) or above \(Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR}\). Built entirely from quantiles, it is immune to the extremes by construction; its personality flaws run the other way – it flags generously on skewed data, and the share it flags drifts with sample size, both points the conceptual chapter develops.

No detector is the correct one, so do not choose – run all three and read their agreement. A value flagged by all three is unusual by any reasonable definition and heads the queue; flagged by two, high priority; flagged by one, quite possibly an artefact of that method’s personality – the classic rule’s normality habit, the fences’ generosity with skew. Agreement converts three imperfect rules into one usable triage.

Note🤔 Rethinking: disagreement is information

The instinct, on seeing three methods flag three different lists, is to ask which method is right. The better question is what the disagreement is telling you, because each mismatch has a typical cause and the cause is more useful than the flag.

When the classic z-score flags far fewer values than the robust methods, suspect masking: a handful of severe extremes have inflated the standard deviation and are shielding themselves and others. The robust lists are the ones to trust – and the severe extremes are probably your data errors. When the fences flag far more than both z-rules, look at the shape: on a long-tailed distribution the fences read ordinary tail values as stragglers, and what you may actually need is a stratified comparison or a transformed scale, not a longer investigation list. And when a value is flagged by exactly one method, the flag is usually telling you about the method – which is precisely why a single-rule screening pipeline, however tidy, quietly inherits a single personality’s blind spots.

The regulatory habit this builds: report which methods flagged each value, not just that it was flagged. “Flagged by all three rules” and “flagged only by the fences on a skewed indicator” should start very different conversations – and documenting the difference is what makes the queue defensible when a provider asks why they were on it.

16.4 Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: Look before you compute

A histogram and a box plot, every time: shape, skew, how many candidates, in which tail, isolated or clustered. The methods below should confirm and order what the picture shows, and surprises in either direction deserve attention.

Step 2: Run all three detectors

Compute the classic z-scores, the modified z-scores, and the fences, with thresholds stated in advance (the conventions – 2.5 or 3, 3.5, 1.5 × IQR – are defensible defaults; just declare which you used).

Step 3: Count the agreement

Sum the flags per unit. Sort descending. That ordering is the investigation queue.

Step 4: Investigate down the queue

For each flagged unit, the three origins from ch06 are the checklist: error? – impossible value, wrong units, missing-data code, wrong provider; genuine extreme? – verify against other periods, related indicators, what is known about the provider; mixed populations? – is this unit being compared with the wrong peers? Stop when the queue’s remaining members were flagged by a single method and the first checks find nothing – screening budgets are finite, which is what the ordering is for.

Step 5: Decide treatment, and show your working

The decisions are ch06’s, applied case by case: keep and document (the default), robust summaries, transformation, removal as the evidenced last resort – with a sensitivity analysis whenever a treatment choice could move the conclusion. Detection adds only one new obligation: because the flags came from declared rules, the non-flags are also a statement, so record the rules and thresholds alongside the decisions.

16.5 Worked Example: Care Home Staffing Ratios

Scenario: 80 care homes have submitted staffing returns – qualified staff per 10 residents – and you need to screen them before the main analysis. Because the data are simulated, we know the truth the methods are trying to recover: 65 typical homes around 3.2 staff per 10 residents; five specialist high-dependency units genuinely staffing around 5.5; three genuinely understaffed homes around 1.5; and seven planted problem values – impossible entries (0, 0.02, 0.05), two figures that look like rates entered per 100 residents rather than per 10 (9.8, 11.2), and two high but genuine specialist returns (7.2, 8.5).

Run the three detectors

  • R
  • Python
x <- care_homes$staff_per_10

# Classic z-score: mean/standard-deviation distance, flag |z| > 2.5
care_homes$z <- (x - mean(x)) / sd(x)
care_homes$flag_z <- abs(care_homes$z) > 2.5

# Modified z-score (Iglewicz & Hoaglin): median/MAD distance, flag |M| > 3.5.
# NOTE: R's mad() is already scaled to mimic the standard deviation
# (constant = 1.4826), so use the raw MAD (constant = 1) with the 0.6745
# multiplier - applying both scalings is a common bug that under-flags.
raw_mad <- mad(x, constant = 1)
care_homes$mod_z <- 0.6745 * (x - median(x)) / raw_mad
care_homes$flag_modz <- abs(care_homes$mod_z) > 3.5

# Tukey's fences: quartiles +/- 1.5 IQR
q1 <- quantile(x, 0.25); q3 <- quantile(x, 0.75); iqr <- q3 - q1
fence_lo <- q1 - 1.5 * iqr
fence_hi <- q3 + 1.5 * iqr
care_homes$flag_iqr <- x < fence_lo | x > fence_hi

cat("Classic z flags:", sum(care_homes$flag_z),
    "- modified z flags:", sum(care_homes$flag_modz),
    "- fences flag:", sum(care_homes$flag_iqr), "\n")

from scipy.stats import median_abs_deviation

x = care_homes["staff_per_10"]

# Classic z-score: mean/standard-deviation distance, flag |z| > 2.5
care_homes["z"] = (x - x.mean()) / x.std()
care_homes["flag_z"] = care_homes["z"].abs() > 2.5

# Modified z-score (Iglewicz & Hoaglin): median/MAD distance, flag |M| > 3.5.
# NOTE: scipy's median_abs_deviation returns the RAW MAD by default,
# so the 0.6745 multiplier is required here (unlike R's pre-scaled mad()).
raw_mad = median_abs_deviation(x)
care_homes["mod_z"] = 0.6745 * (x - x.median()) / raw_mad
care_homes["flag_modz"] = care_homes["mod_z"].abs() > 3.5

# Tukey's fences: quartiles +/- 1.5 IQR
q1, q3 = x.quantile(0.25), x.quantile(0.75)
iqr = q3 - q1
fence_lo, fence_hi = q1 - 1.5 * iqr, q3 + 1.5 * iqr
care_homes["flag_iqr"] = (x < fence_lo) | (x > fence_hi)

print(f"Classic z flags: {care_homes['flag_z'].sum()}"
      f" - modified z flags: {care_homes['flag_modz'].sum()}"
      f" - fences flag: {care_homes['flag_iqr'].sum()}")

The three counts disagree exactly as their personalities predict – and the classic z-score’s count is the masking effect in action. The planted extremes inflate the standard deviation so much that the rule’s lower threshold falls below zero: a home reporting literally no qualified staff cannot be flagged by the classic rule on this data. The three values it does flag are only the most extreme high returns. The robust methods, their yardsticks untouched by the extremes, see both tails.

Investigate, and close the loop

The queue works through the three origins home by home, and on this simulated data we can mark the screening against the truth. The three impossible entries (0, 0.02, 0.05 staff per 10 residents) are confirmed data errors – a home cannot run with no qualified staff – to be corrected from the source or set aside with a documented note. The two suspected unit errors (9.8, 11.2) go back to the providers; if they were entered per 100 residents, the corrected values rejoin the dataset. The high genuine returns (7.2, 8.5, and the 6.12 specialist) are kept and explained: they are specialist units, and the right long-term fix is a stratified comparison, not a deletion. The five single-method flags – four specialist units around 5.5 and one low-staffed home at 1.04 – were the fences being generous; the first checks find nothing alarming, and they stay, documented, in the dataset.

Notice the two things the screening did not do. It did not decide anything – every action above came from investigation, with the methods only setting the order of the queue. And it did not catch the three genuinely understaffed homes around 1.5 staff per 10 residents: they sit inside every rule’s limits, which is a reminder that statistical unusualness and regulatory concern are different properties – a domain threshold (“fewer than 2 qualified staff per 10 residents warrants a look”) belongs alongside the statistical rules, not instead of them.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: the robust machinery, and a scaling trap

Why 0.6745, and the trap. For normal data the raw MAD – the median of absolute distances from the median – settles at about 0.6745 of the standard deviation. Multiplying by 0.6745 therefore rescales distance-over-MAD so the modified z-score reads like an ordinary z-score under normality, which is what makes “flag beyond 3.5” a sensible convention. The trap: software disagrees about whose job that rescaling is. R’s mad() already multiplies by 1.4826 (= 1/0.6745) so that its output mimics a standard deviation; scipy’s median_abs_deviation() returns the raw MAD. Apply the 0.6745 formula on top of R’s pre-scaled version and every modified z-score shrinks by a third – the screen silently under-flags, and the two language tabs of an analysis stop agreeing. Use mad(x, constant = 1) with the multiplier (as the code above does), or equivalently (x - median(x)) / mad(x) with none; check any inherited script for this bug before trusting its flags.

Thresholds and variants. The conventions – 2.5 or 3 for z, 3.5 for the modified score, 1.5 × IQR for the fences (3 × IQR for Tukey’s “far out” line) – are defensible defaults, not laws; what matters for QA is declaring them in advance. On skewed data the symmetric fences over-flag the long tail, and skew-adjusted fences exist (widening the fence on the tail side using a robust skewness measure); if an indicator is persistently skewed, though, the better first move is usually a transformed scale or a stratified comparison, which fixes the cause rather than the symptom.

Beyond one variable at a time. Everything in this chapter screens a single indicator. A provider can be unremarkable on every indicator separately and unusual in combination – moderately high incidents and moderately low staffing and moderately high complaints. Multivariate detectors exist for exactly this: Mahalanobis distance (distance from the multivariate centre, accounting for the correlations between indicators), and machine-learning approaches such as Isolation Forest and Local Outlier Factor that find points easy to separate from the crowd. They are powerful, sensitive to their settings, and easy to over-read – raise the need with the Guild rather than reaching for a library default.

16.6 Documenting Your Analysis

For QA purposes, record: the methods run, with their thresholds, declared before results were seen; the flag counts per method and the agreement table; the investigation log – which units were examined, what was checked, what was found; every treatment decision with its justification, the dataset sizes before and after any exclusion, and a sensitivity analysis whenever treatment could move the conclusion; and the screening’s known limits – what the rules cannot catch (the understaffed-but-unflagged homes above) and any domain thresholds applied alongside them. The chapter on quality assurance holds the templates; “we removed five outliers” is, on its own, the sentence QA exists to prevent.

16.8 When to Seek Support from the Guild

Raise your analysis with the Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) at a Quant Clinic session when: the methods disagree wildly and the data’s shape does not explain why; the screening is multivariate (unusual combinations rather than unusual values); the flagged list is too long to investigate and needs principled pruning; a treatment decision could materially affect regulatory action or faces stakeholder challenge; the data are hierarchical or spatial; or systematic flag patterns suggest a data-quality problem upstream rather than unusual providers downstream.

## What this is for

You need to find the observations that stand apart from the rest of a dataset – systematically, reproducibly, and at a scale where eyeballing every value is impossible. For example:

- Which of 350 care homes report staffing ratios extreme enough to check before the main analysis?
- Which waiting-time submissions look like unit errors rather than long waits?
- Which providers should an analyst look at first, when there is only time to look at some?

Detection methods turn "that looks odd" into stated, repeatable rules – and combining several rules turns a pile of flags into an ordered investigation queue.
:::

## From Lens to Rule {#sec-outlier-methods}

The [chapter on unusual observations](06-unusual-observations.qmd) did the thinking: "unusual" has no universal definition; a value stands out for one of three reasons (a data error, a genuine extreme, or mixed populations) and each calls for a different response; the line you draw is a choice to declare, not a fact to discover; and the default treatment is to investigate and keep, never to silently delete. This chapter assumes all of that and adds the machinery – the rules that apply those lenses to eight hundred providers as readily as to eight, identically every time they run.

Mechanising the judgement matters for exactly the reasons hand-inspection fails. A rule is *reproducible*: a colleague running your code flags the same providers, which is what makes a flagged list defensible when a provider asks why they are on it. A rule *scales*: national datasets cannot be read point by point. And a rule is *declarable in advance*, which is the discipline that stops threshold-shopping – the researcher degree of freedom ch06 warned about – before it starts. What a rule is not is a judgement: every method below produces candidates for investigation, nothing more.

## Before You Start

**Read the conceptual chapter first.** The methods here are easy to run and easy to misuse; [unusual observations](06-unusual-observations.qmd) supplies the questions that make their output meaningful – above all *why is this value unusual?*, asked before anything is done about it.

**Data requirements.** A quantitative variable, one value per unit. Twenty or more observations before any of the rules below is worth much – with fewer, the summary statistics the rules are built on are themselves too uncertain.

**When these methods are the wrong tool.** Values arriving over time belong with [statistical process control](13-spc-basics.qmd), which respects the time order these methods ignore. Comparing providers against a benchmark where natural variation depends on size belongs with [z-scoring and funnel plots](12-z-scoring.qmd) – a small provider's wild rate is often precision, not deviance, and the methods below do not know the difference. And a clearly two-humped distribution is usually [mixed populations](06-unusual-observations.qmd): stratify first, or the rules will flag one whole group as outliers from the other.

**QA framework alignment.** Detection work most directly supports QA question 3 (Analysis – has the data been appropriately checked?) and question 5 (Fitness for Purpose – are results defensible given data quality?). The documentation section below sets out the record to keep.

**Worked example for this chapter.** [Example 10: Care Home Staffing Investigation](../examples/example-10-outliers.qmd) runs the full multi-method workflow with the investigation and treatment decisions documented end to end.

*The code below uses pseudo-randomly generated data. The Python tabs read the R-generated dataset via `r.care_homes` so both languages show identical numbers; commented-out code shows how to generate the data in Python independently.*

## Three Detectors, Three Personalities

Each rule answers "how far from the middle is too far?" with different definitions of *middle* and *far* – and those choices give each a distinct failure mode worth knowing before trusting any of them.

**The classic z-score rule** flags values more than a chosen number of standard deviations from the mean – $|x - \bar{x}|/s$ beyond 2.5, or 3 for a stricter line. It is familiar, quantitative, and carries one structural flaw for this particular job: the mean and especially the standard deviation are *computed from the very data being screened*, so the outliers being hunted inflate the yardstick used to hunt them. One monstrous value can widen $s$ enough to hide both itself and every lesser extreme – an effect called **masking**, and not a rare curiosity: the worked example below shows the classic rule missing every impossibly low value in the dataset for exactly this reason.

**The modified z-score** (Iglewicz and Hoaglin's robust replacement) swaps in summaries that extremes cannot corrupt: distance from the *median*, scaled by the *median absolute deviation* – the median of the absolute distances from the median, a robust stand-in for the standard deviation:

$$M_i = \frac{0.6745\,(x_i - \tilde{x})}{\text{MAD}}$$

where $\tilde{x}$ is the median and the 0.6745 calibrates $M$ to read like a z-score when the data are normal. The conventional flag is $|M| > 3.5$. Because median and MAD barely move when the extremes do, the yardstick stays honest no matter how wild the values being screened – the property the classic rule lacks. (One implementation trap with library MAD functions is real and lurking; the Overthinking box below defuses it.)

**Tukey's fences** – the IQR rule, the one [box plots apply silently](06-unusual-observations.qmd) – flags anything below $Q_1 - 1.5\,\text{IQR}$ or above $Q_3 + 1.5\,\text{IQR}$. Built entirely from quantiles, it is immune to the extremes by construction; its personality flaws run the other way – it flags generously on skewed data, and the share it flags drifts with sample size, both points the conceptual chapter develops.

No detector is the correct one, so do not choose – run all three and read their *agreement*. A value flagged by all three is unusual by any reasonable definition and heads the queue; flagged by two, high priority; flagged by one, quite possibly an artefact of that method's personality – the classic rule's normality habit, the fences' generosity with skew. Agreement converts three imperfect rules into one usable triage.

::: {.callout-note icon=false}
## 🤔 Rethinking: disagreement is information

The instinct, on seeing three methods flag three different lists, is to ask which method is right. The better question is what the disagreement is telling you, because each mismatch has a typical cause and the cause is more useful than the flag.

When the classic z-score flags far *fewer* values than the robust methods, suspect masking: a handful of severe extremes have inflated the standard deviation and are shielding themselves and others. The robust lists are the ones to trust – and the severe extremes are probably your data errors. When the fences flag far *more* than both z-rules, look at the shape: on a long-tailed distribution the fences read ordinary tail values as stragglers, and what you may actually need is [a stratified comparison or a transformed scale](05-distribution-shape.qmd), not a longer investigation list. And when a value is flagged by exactly one method, the flag is usually telling you about the method – which is precisely why a single-rule screening pipeline, however tidy, quietly inherits a single personality's blind spots.

The regulatory habit this builds: report *which* methods flagged each value, not just that it was flagged. "Flagged by all three rules" and "flagged only by the fences on a skewed indicator" should start very different conversations – and documenting the difference is what makes the queue defensible when a provider asks why they were on it.
:::

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Look before you compute

A histogram and a box plot, every time: shape, skew, how many candidates, in which tail, isolated or clustered. The methods below should *confirm and order* what the picture shows, and surprises in either direction deserve attention.

### Step 2: Run all three detectors

Compute the classic z-scores, the modified z-scores, and the fences, with thresholds stated in advance (the conventions – 2.5 or 3, 3.5, 1.5 × IQR – are defensible defaults; just declare which you used).

### Step 3: Count the agreement

Sum the flags per unit. Sort descending. That ordering is the investigation queue.

### Step 4: Investigate down the queue

For each flagged unit, the [three origins from ch06](06-unusual-observations.qmd) are the checklist: *error?* – impossible value, wrong units, missing-data code, wrong provider; *genuine extreme?* – verify against other periods, related indicators, what is known about the provider; *mixed populations?* – is this unit being compared with the wrong peers? Stop when the queue's remaining members were flagged by a single method and the first checks find nothing – screening budgets are finite, which is what the ordering is for.

### Step 5: Decide treatment, and show your working

The decisions are ch06's, applied case by case: keep and document (the default), robust summaries, transformation, removal as the evidenced last resort – with a sensitivity analysis whenever a treatment choice could move the conclusion. Detection adds only one new obligation: because the flags came from declared rules, the *non*-flags are also a statement, so record the rules and thresholds alongside the decisions.

### Run the three detectors

:::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}
## R
```{r}
#| label: outlier-detect
x <- care_homes$staff_per_10

# Classic z-score: mean/standard-deviation distance, flag |z| > 2.5
care_homes$z <- (x - mean(x)) / sd(x)
care_homes$flag_z <- abs(care_homes$z) > 2.5

# Modified z-score (Iglewicz & Hoaglin): median/MAD distance, flag |M| > 3.5.
# NOTE: R's mad() is already scaled to mimic the standard deviation
# (constant = 1.4826), so use the raw MAD (constant = 1) with the 0.6745
# multiplier - applying both scalings is a common bug that under-flags.
raw_mad <- mad(x, constant = 1)
care_homes$mod_z <- 0.6745 * (x - median(x)) / raw_mad
care_homes$flag_modz <- abs(care_homes$mod_z) > 3.5

# Tukey's fences: quartiles +/- 1.5 IQR
q1 <- quantile(x, 0.25); q3 <- quantile(x, 0.75); iqr <- q3 - q1
fence_lo <- q1 - 1.5 * iqr
fence_hi <- q3 + 1.5 * iqr
care_homes$flag_iqr <- x < fence_lo | x > fence_hi

cat("Classic z flags:", sum(care_homes$flag_z),
    "- modified z flags:", sum(care_homes$flag_modz),
    "- fences flag:", sum(care_homes$flag_iqr), "\n")
cat("Fences: [", round(fence_lo, 2), ",", round(fence_hi, 2), "]\n")
```

## Python
```{python}
#| label: outlier-detect-py
from scipy.stats import median_abs_deviation

x = care_homes["staff_per_10"]

# Classic z-score: mean/standard-deviation distance, flag |z| > 2.5
care_homes["z"] = (x - x.mean()) / x.std()
care_homes["flag_z"] = care_homes["z"].abs() > 2.5

# Modified z-score (Iglewicz & Hoaglin): median/MAD distance, flag |M| > 3.5.
# NOTE: scipy's median_abs_deviation returns the RAW MAD by default,
# so the 0.6745 multiplier is required here (unlike R's pre-scaled mad()).
raw_mad = median_abs_deviation(x)
care_homes["mod_z"] = 0.6745 * (x - x.median()) / raw_mad
care_homes["flag_modz"] = care_homes["mod_z"].abs() > 3.5

# Tukey's fences: quartiles +/- 1.5 IQR
q1, q3 = x.quantile(0.25), x.quantile(0.75)
iqr = q3 - q1
fence_lo, fence_hi = q1 - 1.5 * iqr, q3 + 1.5 * iqr
care_homes["flag_iqr"] = (x < fence_lo) | (x > fence_hi)

print(f"Classic z flags: {care_homes['flag_z'].sum()}"
      f" - modified z flags: {care_homes['flag_modz'].sum()}"
      f" - fences flag: {care_homes['flag_iqr'].sum()}")
print(f"Fences: [{fence_lo:.2f}, {fence_hi:.2f}]")
```
::::

The three counts disagree exactly as their personalities predict – and the classic z-score's count is the masking effect in action. The planted extremes inflate the standard deviation so much that the rule's lower threshold falls *below zero*: a home reporting literally no qualified staff cannot be flagged by the classic rule on this data. The three values it does flag are only the most extreme high returns. The robust methods, their yardsticks untouched by the extremes, see both tails.

### Investigate, and close the loop

The queue works through the [three origins](06-unusual-observations.qmd) home by home, and on this simulated data we can mark the screening against the truth. The three impossible entries (0, 0.02, 0.05 staff per 10 residents) are confirmed *data errors* – a home cannot run with no qualified staff – to be corrected from the source or set aside with a documented note. The two suspected unit errors (9.8, 11.2) go back to the providers; if they were entered per 100 residents, the corrected values rejoin the dataset. The high genuine returns (7.2, 8.5, and the 6.12 specialist) are *kept and explained*: they are specialist units, and the right long-term fix is a stratified comparison, not a deletion. The five single-method flags – four specialist units around 5.5 and one low-staffed home at 1.04 – were the fences being generous; the first checks find nothing alarming, and they stay, documented, in the dataset.

Notice the two things the screening did *not* do. It did not decide anything – every action above came from investigation, with the methods only setting the order of the queue. And it did not catch the three genuinely understaffed homes around 1.5 staff per 10 residents: they sit inside every rule's limits, which is a reminder that statistical unusualness and regulatory concern are [different properties](06-unusual-observations.qmd) – a domain threshold ("fewer than 2 qualified staff per 10 residents warrants a look") belongs alongside the statistical rules, not instead of them.

## ⚙️ Overthinking: the robust machinery, and a scaling trap

**Why 0.6745, and the trap.** For normal data the raw MAD – the median of absolute distances from the median – settles at about 0.6745 of the standard deviation. Multiplying by 0.6745 therefore rescales distance-over-MAD so the modified z-score reads like an ordinary z-score under normality, which is what makes "flag beyond 3.5" a sensible convention. The trap: software disagrees about whose job that rescaling is. R's `mad()` *already* multiplies by 1.4826 (= 1/0.6745) so that its output mimics a standard deviation; scipy's `median_abs_deviation()` returns the raw MAD. Apply the 0.6745 formula on top of R's pre-scaled version and every modified z-score shrinks by a third – the screen silently under-flags, and the two language tabs of an analysis stop agreeing. Use `mad(x, constant = 1)` with the multiplier (as the code above does), or equivalently `(x - median(x)) / mad(x)` with none; check any inherited script for this bug before trusting its flags.

**Thresholds and variants.** The conventions – 2.5 or 3 for z, 3.5 for the modified score, 1.5 × IQR for the fences (3 × IQR for Tukey's "far out" line) – are defensible defaults, not laws; what matters for QA is declaring them in advance. On skewed data the symmetric fences over-flag the long tail, and skew-adjusted fences exist (widening the fence on the tail side using a robust skewness measure); if an indicator is persistently skewed, though, the better first move is usually [a transformed scale or a stratified comparison](05-distribution-shape.qmd), which fixes the cause rather than the symptom.

**Beyond one variable at a time.** Everything in this chapter screens a single indicator. A provider can be unremarkable on every indicator separately and unusual in *combination* – moderately high incidents *and* moderately low staffing *and* moderately high complaints. Multivariate detectors exist for exactly this: Mahalanobis distance (distance from the multivariate centre, accounting for the correlations between indicators), and machine-learning approaches such as Isolation Forest and Local Outlier Factor that find points easy to separate from the crowd. They are powerful, sensitive to their settings, and easy to over-read – raise the need with the Guild rather than reaching for a library default.
:::

## Documenting Your Analysis

For QA purposes, record: the methods run, with their thresholds, declared before results were seen; the flag counts per method and the agreement table; the investigation log – which units were examined, what was checked, what was found; every treatment decision with its justification, the dataset sizes before and after any exclusion, and a sensitivity analysis whenever treatment could move the conclusion; and the screening's known limits – what the rules cannot catch (the understaffed-but-unflagged homes above) and any domain thresholds applied alongside them. [The chapter on quality assurance](09-qa-principles.qmd) holds the templates; "we removed five outliers" is, on its own, the sentence QA exists to prevent.
