5  Distribution Shape

TipLearning outcomes

After reading this chapter you will be able to:

  • explain why a distribution’s centre and spread can match while its shape differs, and read shape off a plot as a matter of course;
  • name the three questions shape asks – which way it leans (skew), how many peaks it has (modality), how heavy its tails are (tail weight, or kurtosis) – and see that they apply to ordinal data, not only quantitative;
  • read skew from a histogram and from the mean–median gap, while knowing that gap is a fallible heuristic and that several skewness measures exist;
  • use shape both to choose an honest summary (the median and IQR over the mean and standard deviation when skewed) and to judge whether a method’s distributional assumptions hold;
  • tell leptokurtic, mesokurtic and platykurtic apart, and say why heavy or light tails matter for risk and for inference.

5.1 Beyond Centre and Spread

The last two chapters gave you two numbers for a distribution: a centre, from Central Tendency and Position, and a spread, from Measuring Variation. It is tempting to think that pins a distribution down. It does not. Two distributions can share the same mean and the same standard deviation and still look nothing alike.

That gap is what this chapter is about. Shape is everything about a distribution that survives once you have accounted for where it sits and how wide it is: read only the two numbers and you would call these services identical, when one runs steady and the other hides a tail of slow cases.

It helps to have words for the parts of a distribution before naming its shapes. Picture the histogram as a landscape. Most cases pile up in a central bulk around the high point, the peak; from there the counts fall away on each side into the tails – the sparse stretches at the far left and right where values grow rare. A distribution is symmetric when its two tails mirror each other, and shape is simply the form of this landscape: where the bulk sits, how the tails behave, and how many peaks there are.

Shape climbs the ladder of measurement along with everything else: the higher the rung, the more precisely you can pin it down, until at the top real numeric gaps let you put an exact figure on it. Whether one side of a distribution carries more of the cases than the other – the lean – and how much of the data sits out in the extremes – the tail weight or thickness – are matters of order and counting, as much at home on an ordinal scale as a quantitative one. Only a tail’s length truly needs numbers, since length is a distance, and distance wants a scale with real gaps and, usually, no fixed ceiling. So the questions below are sharpest for quantitative data, but you can put them to ordinal data too:

  • Which way does it lean? Its skew – does more of the data bank up at one end, with the opposite tail drawn out?
  • How many peaks does it have? Its modality – one bulk, or more than one?
  • How heavy are the tails? Its tail weight – how much of the data sits far out in the extremes, rather than near the bulk?

At the very bottom of the ladder, nominal categories carry no order at all, and with no line to lay them along there is no side to lean toward and no far edge for a tail to reach – so skew and tail weight fall away entirely. All that remains is counting: whether the cases concentrate in one category or spread across many. That is worth knowing, but it is the diversity of Measuring Variation rather than shape, and even calling a tall category a “peak” is shaky, since nominal bars can be reordered at will. Shape, in the sense this chapter means, begins where order does.

5.2 Which Way It Leans: Skew

The lopsided distribution from the opening figure has a name. When one tail is drawn out longer than the other, the distribution is skewed, and it is named for the side the long tail is on:

  • Right-skewed (or positively skewed): a long tail reaching to the right, toward the high values. This is the common case in regulatory data, because so many quantities are bounded below by zero but unbounded above. Waiting times, length of stay, incident and complaint counts, rates per 1,000 bed-days – most cases sit low, a few run very high.
  • Left-skewed (or negatively skewed): a long tail reaching to the left, toward the low values, with the bulk pressed up to the right.

Left skew deserves more attention than its “rarer” reputation suggests, because so many CQC indicators measure performance, and performance is often bounded above. The share of patients seen within a target, the percentage of records complete, a compliance score out of 100 – none can exceed its ceiling, so when most providers do well the distribution banks up against the top and trails away below. Pushed far enough this becomes a ceiling effect: a pile-up of providers at the maximum value (100%, or 1 for a proportion) that show little to no spread. A floor effect is its mirror image at the bottom. Ceilings are worth reading carefully, because very different things produce them – genuinely high performance, an indicator too easy to max out, or the pull of a financial or contractual incentive that turns the target itself into the goal – and the distribution alone will not tell you which. The shape flags the question; it does not answer it.

The figure below lays the three skewness cases side by side, and marks on each the two centres you already know.

You have two dependable ways to read the lean, and a third to handle with care. Plot it: a histogram shows the asymmetry at a glance, and the look-first habit means you never skip this. Compare the mean with the median: the median splits the cases into two equal halves, so a long right tail of large values drags the mean above it – which is why mean above median points to a right lean, mean below median to a left one. It is a handy check, but only a heuristic, and it fails more often than textbooks admit: in discrete data where the halves either side of the median are uneven, in distributions with more than one peak, and where one tail is long while the other is heavy, the mean and median can sit the “wrong” way round. So let the gap suggest the lean and the plot settle it. The third way is a skewness statistic – but there is no single such number, the family of them is the business of the box below, and like any one-number summary each can mislead. Use one to confirm the picture, never to replace it.

Reading the lean pays off at once, because it settles choices the last two chapters left open. On a skewed distribution the median describes a typical case better than the mean, which the long tail drags away from the bulk – the familiar fact that most people earn below the “average” wage is this effect. The IQR likewise serves better than the standard deviation, which the tail inflates. And the handful of values out in the long tail are the expected shape of the data, not anomalies to flag – a distinction the chapter on unusual observations takes up. Quoting the mean and the median together, and naming any gap, is often the single clearest thing you can do.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: Putting a number on skew

There is no single “skewness”; there is a family of measures, and they do not always agree.

  • The one most software returns is the moment coefficient (Fisher’s, also called Pearson’s): the average cubed standardised distance from the mean. Cubing keeps the sign – which way it leans – and weights far values heavily, which makes it sensitive and easily thrown by one outlier. It needs real numeric distances, so it belongs to quantitative data.
  • Pearson’s median coefficient, \(3(\text{mean} - \text{median})/\sigma\), is just the mean–median gap of the main text put on a scale. The “mean above median means right skew” rule is this measure with its sign read off, which is exactly why, like the rule, it is only a heuristic.
  • Bowley’s quantile coefficient, \((Q_3 + Q_1 - 2Q_2)/(Q_3 - Q_1)\), is built from the central half, so it shrugs off outliers (at the cost of ignoring the tails entirely). It is robust, but note it still does arithmetic on the quartile values, so it needs genuine numeric distances; robustness is not the same as working on ordinal data.

Two cautions close this off. First, even on numeric data no single number captures a shape: the measures can disagree, and the comfortable rule that positive skew means mean above median can fail. Second, none of these measures honestly fit ordinal data such as Likert scales, because every one of them needs real distances between the categories, and scoring the rungs 1 to 5 just assumes equal spacing. You can still read an ordinal lean by eye – from the bar chart and, if you have only one mode, from where this lies with respect to the median. Purpose-built ordinal measures do exist that work from cumulative counts alone, but they are specialist. So for ratings, plot the distribution and describe what you see, and take any attempt to put a number on the shape to the Guild – which is the right instinct whenever measuring shape starts to feel high-stakes.

5.3 How Many Peaks: Modality

The second question is the simplest to ask and one of the most consequential to answer: how many peaks does the distribution have? One clear peak makes it unimodal; two makes it bimodal; more, multimodal. It matters because a second peak often means two different groups have been pooled into one chart – and that is a finding, not a nuisance.

This is where centre and spread fail most plainly. The mean and the median sit close together in the dip between the groups, describing the quick day-cases and the long admissions about equally badly – the “typical” six-and-a-half-day stay belongs to neither. No measure of skew or tail weight rescues this; only noticing the second peak does. It is the same warning the data visualisation chapter raised when a bin width too coarse hid two groups inside one hump – and real data rarely separates as cleanly as a textbook picture, which is exactly why you have to look. When you see a second peak, the response is not a cleverer summary but to disaggregate: find what splits the humps – two services, two sites, two cohorts – and describe each on its own.

5.4 How Heavy the Tails: Extremes

The third question is about the far values: setting aside which way a distribution leans, how often does it throw up a value a long way from the centre? This is its tail weight (the formal name is kurtosis). Two distributions can share a centre, a spread, and even perfect symmetry, and still differ in how often they reach the extremes.

Tail weight is the quiet one of the three, but it carries a real regulatory message: it is about the risk of the rare, serious event. To see why, it helps to have a concrete benchmark. Under the bell – the mesokurtic reference – roughly 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean, about 95% within two, and about 99.7% within three. This is the empirical rule, and it gives the standard deviation a meaning you can act on: a value more than two standard deviations from the centre is a one-in-twenty event; one more than three standard deviations is a one-in-three-hundred event. A heavy-tailed distribution undermines those odds: more than 5% of values land beyond two standard deviations, so the extreme values the bell says should be rare turn up more often than expected. A heavy-tailed quantity – the severity of incidents, say – therefore throws up extremes more often than its centre and spread would suggest, so a mean and a standard deviation that look reassuring can still sit over a distribution that occasionally spikes hard. It also reframes outliers: under heavy tails a far-out value may be the normal product of the shape rather than an error or a true anomaly – a thread the chapter on unusual observations picks up.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: Leptokurtic, mesokurtic, platykurtic

Tail weight has a name, kurtosis, and the three cases have names too: a heavy-tailed distribution is leptokurtic, a light-tailed one platykurtic, and the bell itself is mesokurtic. The measure behind them is the average fourth standardised distance from the mean. The normal bell scores exactly 3 on it – that value is not arbitrary, it is simply what the bell works out to – so kurtosis is almost always quoted as excess kurtosis, kurtosis minus 3, which puts the bell at 0, leptokurtic above it, platykurtic below.

Two cautions and a consequence:

  • It is named for the tails, not the peak. The tall central spike of a leptokurtic curve is a side effect of holding the spread fixed while pushing weight into the tails, not the thing being measured.
  • It is even less robust than skew: built on the fourth power of distance, a single extreme value can swing it wildly.
  • It changes which inferential tools are valid – just as skew does. Many standard tests and intervals assume the bell and use its tail probabilities to turn a result into a p-value or a margin of error. Hand them a leptokurtic distribution, which throws up extreme values far more often than the bell predicts, and those probabilities come out wrong: the test understates how often a large value arises by chance, so the p-value is too small and you over-call significance. Light tails bias it the other way. When the tails matter and you are leaning on normal-theory inference, this is probably the time to look for some support.

5.5 What to Do About Shape

Shape is information, not a defect to scrub out. But it steers what you do next, in two arenas.

Describing the data. This part you have already met: when a distribution is skewed or heavy-tailed, quote the median and the IQR rather than the mean and standard deviation; when it is bimodal, split it; and say which you did, in plain words. No special machinery is needed.

Analysing it. The moment you move from describing data to drawing inferences from it – testing whether two services differ, estimating a value with a margin of error, fitting a model – shape stops being cosmetic. Most of the standard toolbox (the t-test, ordinary linear regression, the usual confidence interval) is built on a roughly symmetric, bell-like shape and leans on it to turn results into probabilities; symmetry is also the mathematically simplest case, which is why so much theory assumes it. Feed those tools a strongly skewed or heavy-tailed quantity and the probabilities can be wrong, as the boxes above describe. You then have three honest routes:

  • Make the data fit the tool. Re-express the quantity on a scale that pulls it toward symmetry – a logarithm for a long right tail, a square root for counts, exponentiating for a left tail, or a more general transform – then use the standard methods on the transformed values. (The mechanics, and the catch on the way back, are in the box below.)
  • Make the tool fit the data. Reach for methods that expect the shape: generalised linear models for skewed or count data, and richer location-scale-shape families that let the shape itself vary. These ask more statistical background but do not distort the data.
  • Lean on the shape as little as possible. Rank-based and other nonparametric methods assume almost nothing about the distribution, trading a little power for robustness – often the quickest safe option.

Which route fits is a method-by-method judgement that the later chapters take up, and Statistical Inference is where it begins. The one thing never to do is hand a strongly non-symmetric quantity to a tool that assumes symmetry and report the result as if the shape were not there.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: The mechanics of a log transform

The most common way to “make the data fit the tool” is to work with the logarithm of a strictly positive, right-skewed quantity – incident rates, waiting times, lengths of stay. A log scale compresses large values far more than small ones, so a long right tail is pulled in and the distribution often comes out close to symmetric, ready for the standard methods. Three strings are attached:

  • It changes the question. Differences on a log scale are multiplicative, not additive: a step of the same size will mean “twice as long”, not “two hours longer”. That is sometimes exactly the right way to think about a rate, and sometimes a quiet distortion of it.
  • It cannot take a zero. The logarithm of zero is undefined, so data with genuine zeros (no incidents this month) needs care – a small offset is the usual fix, but it is a choice to declare, not hide.
  • You must translate back. Stakeholders cannot act on log-days. Convert results to the original scale before reporting, and note that the back-transformed centre is a geometric mean (the natural average on a multiplicative scale, met in Central Tendency and Position), which is always not greater than the ordinary mean.

If a log does not straighten the distribution, or the data has meaningful zeros, do not force it – fall back on robust summaries, or on the shape-aware and nonparametric methods above. When the choice changes a published conclusion, it is worth raising with the Guild.

5.6 Where Shape Leads

Shape is the third and last of the everyday descriptors. Centre says where a distribution sits, spread says how wide it is, and shape – which way it leans, how many peaks it has, how much weight rides in its tails – says whether the first two can be trusted to speak for it, and whether the tools you are about to use will hold. That reach is what makes the chapter a hinge. It points back to the summaries and measures of spread it helps you choose between; it reaches across the ladder of measurement to ordinal scales, which have a lean and a tail-weight of their own; and it points forward twice – to the near question of when a value in the tail is merely the shape and when it is genuinely unusual, which the chapter on unusual observations takes up, and to the deeper one of how shape governs the validity of statistical inference, further on. The habit that carries through all of it is the one this part of the book keeps repeating: plot the distribution and read its shape before you trust any number drawn from it.

```{r}

#| fig-cap: "Two distributions with the identical mean (10 days) and the identical standard deviation (2 days), yet plainly different shapes. Left: symmetric, balanced about the centre. Right: lopsided, with a long tail of slow cases pulling to the right. Centre and spread agree; the shape disagrees. A report quoting only the mean and the standard deviation would treat these two services as the same."
#| fig-alt: "Two histograms side by side of days from referral to first assessment. Left panel, symmetric: a roughly even bell centred near ten days, tailing off equally on both sides. Both have the same mean of ten and the same standard deviation of two."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 8
#| fig-height: 3.4

library(ggplot2)

set.seed(21)

  data.frame(shape = "Symmetric",                x = std(rnorm(2000), 10, 2)),
  data.frame(shape = "Lopsided (long right tail)", x = std(rgamma(2000, shape = 2), 10, 2))

shape_df$shape <- factor(shape_df$shape,
                         levels = c("Symmetric", "Lopsided (long right tail)"))

ggplot(shape_df, aes(x)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 40, fill = "#007EF5", colour = "white", alpha = 0.9) +
  facet_wrap(~shape) +
  labs(x = "Days from referral to first assessment", y = "Providers") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),

## Which Way It Leans: Skew

The lopsided distribution from the opening figure has a name. When one tail is drawn out longer than the other, the distribution is **skewed**, and it is named for the side the long tail is on:

- **Right-skewed** (or *positively* skewed): a long tail reaching to the right, toward the high values. This is the common case in regulatory data, because so many quantities are bounded below by zero but unbounded above. Waiting times, length of stay, incident and complaint counts, rates per 1,000 bed-days – most cases sit low, a few run very high.
- **Left-skewed** (or *negatively* skewed): a long tail reaching to the left, toward the low values, with the bulk pressed up to the right.

Left skew deserves more attention than its "rarer" reputation suggests, because so many CQC indicators measure *performance*, and performance is often bounded above. The shape flags the question; it does not answer it.

  facet_wrap(~type) +
  coord_cartesian(xlim = c(0, 12)) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = c(mean = "#A83DA4", median = "#006560"), name = NULL) +
  labs(x = "Value", y = "Density") +

Cubing keeps the sign – which way it leans – and weights far values heavily, which makes it sensitive and easily thrown by one outlier. It needs real numeric distances, so it belongs to quantitative data.
- **Pearson's median coefficient**, $3(\text{mean} - \text{median})/\sigma$, is just the mean–median gap of the main text put on a scale. The "mean above median means right skew" rule is this measure with its sign read off, which is exactly why, like the rule, it is only a heuristic.
- **Bowley's quantile coefficient**, $(Q_3 + Q_1 - 2Q_2)/(Q_3 - Q_1)$, is built from the central half, so it shrugs off outliers (at the cost of ignoring the tails entirely). It is robust, but note it still does *arithmetic* on the quartile values, so it needs genuine numeric distances; robustness is not the same as working on ordinal data.

Two cautions close this off. First, even on numeric data no single number captures a shape: the measures can disagree, and the comfortable rule that positive skew means mean above median can fail. Second, none of these measures honestly fit **ordinal** data such as Likert scales, because every one of them needs real distances between the categories, and scoring the rungs 1 to 5 just assumes equal spacing. You can still read an ordinal lean by eye – from the bar chart and, if you have only one mode, from where this lies with respect to the median. Purpose-built ordinal measures do exist that work from cumulative counts alone, but they are specialist. So for ratings, plot the distribution and describe what you see, and take any attempt to put a *number* on the shape to the Guild – which is the right instinct whenever measuring shape starts to feel high-stakes.
:::

## How Many Peaks: Modality

The second question is the simplest to ask and one of the most consequential to answer: **how many peaks does the distribution have?** One clear peak makes it **unimodal**; two makes it **bimodal**; more, *multimodal*. It matters because a second peak often means **two different groups have been pooled into one chart** – and that is a finding, not a nuisance.

```{r}
#| label: fig-bimodal
#| fig-cap: "A bimodal distribution, deliberately less tidy than a textbook one: length of stay for a pooled set of patients, with a shorter-stay group around 4–5 days and a longer-stay group around 9–10 that overlap in the middle. The mean (magenta) and median (teal) both fall in the shallow valley between the humps, where comparatively few patients lie – describing neither group well. The honest response is to split the two groups and describe each on its own."
#| fig-alt: "Histogram of length of stay showing two overlapping humps, a taller one centred near four to five days and a second near nine to ten days, with a shallow dip between them around six to seven days rather than an empty gap. A magenta mean line and a teal median line both fall in that dip. The two groups are not cleanly separated."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 3.6

library(ggplot2)

set.seed(12)
los <- c(rnorm(430, 4.5, 1.5), rnorm(370, 9.5, 1.8))
los_mm <- data.frame(measure = factor(c("mean", "median"), levels = c("mean", "median")),
                     val = c(mean(los), median(los)))

ggplot(data.frame(x = los), aes(x)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 32, fill = "#A57703", colour = "white", alpha = 0.9) +
  geom_vline(data = los_mm, aes(xintercept = val, colour = measure), linewidth = 0.9) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = c(mean = "#A83DA4", median = "#006560"), name = NULL) +
  labs(x = "Length of stay (days)", y = "Patients") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        legend.position = "bottom")
```

This is where centre and spread fail most plainly. The mean and the median sit close together in the dip between the groups, describing the quick day-cases and the long admissions about equally badly – the "typical" six-and-a-half-day stay belongs to neither. No measure of skew or tail weight rescues this; only noticing the second peak does. It is the same warning the [data visualisation chapter](02-data-visualisation.qmd) raised when a bin width too coarse hid two groups inside one hump – and real data rarely separates as cleanly as a textbook picture, which is exactly why you have to look. When you see a second peak, the response is not a cleverer summary but to **disaggregate**: find what splits the humps – two services, two sites, two cohorts – and describe each on its own.

## How Heavy the Tails: Extremes

The third question is about the *far* values: setting aside which way a distribution leans, **how often does it throw up a value a long way from the centre?** This is its **tail weight** (the formal name is *kurtosis*). Two distributions can share a centre, a spread, and even perfect symmetry, and still differ in how often they reach the extremes.

```{r}
#| label: fig-tails
#| fig-cap: "Three symmetric distributions with the same mean and the same standard deviation, drawn as density curves and differing only in their tails. The platykurtic curve (gold) is a flat block that ends abruptly near ±1.7 standard deviations – its tails are not merely light but absent. The mesokurtic curve (navy) is the familiar bell. The leptokurtic curve (magenta) has a taller, narrower peak and, crucially, rides above the bell out in the tails: it produces extreme values more often. Same spread, very different tails."
#| fig-alt: "Three density curves on a shared axis of standard deviations from the mean, all centred at zero with the same spread. The platykurtic curve is a low, flat-topped rectangle that drops vertically to zero at about minus and plus 1.7 standard deviations, with no tails at all. The mesokurtic curve is a moderate bell. The leptokurtic curve has the tallest, narrowest peak, crosses the bell on each side, and lies above it far out in the tails, indicating more frequent extreme values."
#| echo: false
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig-width: 7.5
#| fig-height: 3.8

library(ggplot2)

lvk <- c("Platykurtic (light tails)", "Mesokurtic (the bell)", "Leptokurtic (heavy tails)")

# Theoretical densities, each standardised to mean 0 and standard deviation 1,
# so the three differ only in their tails.
nu  <- 4                       # Student-t degrees of freedom -> heavy (leptokurtic)
c_t <- sqrt(nu / (nu - 2))     # rescale t to standard deviation 1
a   <- sqrt(3)                 # uniform half-width for standard deviation 1 (platykurtic)
xx  <- seq(-3.4, 3.4, length.out = 1200)
kurt <- rbind(
  data.frame(k = lvk[1], x = xx, d = ifelse(abs(xx) <= a, 1 / (2 * a), 0)),
  data.frame(k = lvk[2], x = xx, d = dnorm(xx)),
  data.frame(k = lvk[3], x = xx, d = dt(xx * c_t, df = nu) * c_t)
)
kurt$k <- factor(kurt$k, levels = lvk)

ggplot(kurt, aes(x, d, colour = k)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1) +
  coord_cartesian(xlim = c(-3.2, 3.2), ylim = c(0, 0.55)) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = setNames(c("#A57703", "#293241", "#A83DA4"), lvk),
                      name = NULL) +
  labs(x = "Standard deviations from the mean", y = "Density") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11) +
  theme(panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
        legend.position = "bottom")
```

Tail weight is the quiet one of the three, but it carries a real regulatory message: it is about the **risk of the rare, serious event**. To see why, it helps to have a concrete benchmark. Under the bell – the mesokurtic reference – roughly 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean, about 95% within two, and about 99.7% within three. This is the **empirical rule**, and it gives the standard deviation a meaning you can act on: a value more than two standard deviations from the centre is a one-in-twenty event; one more than three standard deviations is a one-in-three-hundred event. A heavy-tailed distribution undermines those odds: more than 5% of values land beyond two standard deviations, so the extreme values the bell says should be rare turn up more often than expected. A heavy-tailed quantity – the severity of incidents, say – therefore throws up extremes more often than its centre and spread would suggest, so a mean and a standard deviation that look reassuring can still sit over a distribution that occasionally spikes hard. It also reframes outliers: under heavy tails a far-out value may be the normal product of the shape rather than an error or a true anomaly – a thread the chapter on [unusual observations](06-unusual-observations.qmd) picks up.

::: {.callout-warning icon=false}
## ⚙️ Overthinking: Leptokurtic, mesokurtic, platykurtic

Tail weight has a name, *kurtosis*, and the three cases have names too: a heavy-tailed distribution is **leptokurtic**, a light-tailed one **platykurtic**, and the bell itself is **mesokurtic**. The measure behind them is the average *fourth* standardised distance from the mean. The normal bell scores exactly **3** on it – that value is not arbitrary, it is simply what the bell works out to – so kurtosis is almost always quoted as **excess kurtosis**, kurtosis minus 3, which puts the bell at **0**, leptokurtic above it, platykurtic below.

Two cautions and a consequence:

- It is named for the *tails*, not the peak. The tall central spike of a leptokurtic curve is a side effect of holding the spread fixed while pushing weight into the tails, not the thing being measured.
- It is even less robust than skew: built on the *fourth* power of distance, a single extreme value can swing it wildly.
- It changes which inferential tools are valid – just as skew does. Many standard tests and intervals assume the bell and use its tail probabilities to turn a result into a p-value or a margin of error. Hand them a leptokurtic distribution, which throws up extreme values far more often than the bell predicts, and those probabilities come out wrong: the test understates how often a large value arises by chance, so the p-value is too small and you over-call significance. Light tails bias it the other way. When the tails matter and you are leaning on normal-theory inference, this is probably the time to look for some support.
:::

## What to Do About Shape

Shape is information, not a defect to scrub out. But it steers what you do next, in two arenas.

**Describing the data.** This part you have already met: when a distribution is skewed or heavy-tailed, quote the median and the IQR rather than the mean and standard deviation; when it is bimodal, split it; and say which you did, in plain words. No special machinery is needed.

**Analysing it.** The moment you move from describing data to *drawing inferences* from it – testing whether two services differ, estimating a value with a margin of error, fitting a model – shape stops being cosmetic. Most of the standard toolbox (the t-test, ordinary linear regression, the usual confidence interval) is built on a roughly symmetric, bell-like shape and leans on it to turn results into probabilities; symmetry is also the mathematically simplest case, which is why so much theory assumes it. Feed those tools a strongly skewed or heavy-tailed quantity and the probabilities can be wrong, as the boxes above describe. You then have three honest routes:

- **Make the data fit the tool.** Re-express the quantity on a scale that pulls it toward symmetry – a logarithm for a long right tail, a square root for counts, exponentiating for a left tail, or a more general transform – then use the standard methods on the transformed values. (The mechanics, and the catch on the way back, are in the box below.)
- **Make the tool fit the data.** Reach for methods that *expect* the shape: generalised linear models for skewed or count data, and richer location-scale-shape families that let the shape itself vary. These ask more statistical background but do not distort the data.
- **Lean on the shape as little as possible.** Rank-based and other *nonparametric* methods assume almost nothing about the distribution, trading a little power for robustness – often the quickest safe option.

Which route fits is a method-by-method judgement that the later chapters take up, and [Statistical Inference](07-statistical-inference.qmd) is where it begins. The one thing never to do is hand a strongly non-symmetric quantity to a tool that assumes symmetry and report the result as if the shape were not there.

::: {.callout-warning icon=false}
## ⚙️ Overthinking: The mechanics of a log transform

The most common way to "make the data fit the tool" is to work with the *logarithm* of a strictly positive, right-skewed quantity – incident rates, waiting times, lengths of stay. A log scale compresses large values far more than small ones, so a long right tail is pulled in and the distribution often comes out close to symmetric, ready for the standard methods. Three strings are attached:

- **It changes the question.** Differences on a log scale are *multiplicative*, not additive: a step of the same size will mean "twice as long", not "two hours longer". That is sometimes exactly the right way to think about a rate, and sometimes a quiet distortion of it.
- **It cannot take a zero.** The logarithm of zero is undefined, so data with genuine zeros (no incidents this month) needs care – a small offset is the usual fix, but it is a choice to declare, not hide.
- **You must translate back.** Stakeholders cannot act on log-days. Convert results to the original scale before reporting, and note that the back-transformed centre is a *geometric* mean (the natural average on a multiplicative scale, met in [Central Tendency and Position](03-describing-data.qmd)), which is always not greater than the ordinary mean.

If a log does not straighten the distribution, or the data has meaningful zeros, do not force it – fall back on robust summaries, or on the shape-aware and nonparametric methods above. When the choice changes a published conclusion, it is worth raising with the Guild.
:::

## Where Shape Leads

Shape is the third and last of the everyday descriptors. Centre says where a distribution sits, spread says how wide it is, and shape – which way it leans, how many peaks it has, how much weight rides in its tails – says whether the first two can be trusted to speak for it, and whether the tools you are about to use will hold. That reach is what makes the chapter a hinge. It points back to the [summaries](03-describing-data.qmd) and [measures of spread](04-variation-uncertainty.qmd) it helps you choose between; it reaches across the [ladder of measurement](01-data-types.qmd) to ordinal scales, which have a lean and a tail-weight of their own; and it points forward twice – to the near question of when a value in the tail is merely the shape and when it is genuinely **unusual**, which the chapter on [unusual observations](06-unusual-observations.qmd) takes up, and to the deeper one of how shape governs the validity of [statistical inference](07-statistical-inference.qmd), further on. The habit that carries through all of it is the one this part of the book keeps repeating: plot the distribution and read its shape before you trust any number drawn from it.
