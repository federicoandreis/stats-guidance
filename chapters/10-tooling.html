10  Analytical Tooling at CQC

TipLearning outcomes

This chapter is an orientation rather than a how-to: it builds the awareness to make sensible tooling choices and take part in the conversations around them, not hands-on proficiency in any one tool. That comes from the learning routes it points you to. After reading it you will be able to:

  • describe the tools available for analytical work at CQC and explain what each is best suited to;
  • explain the direction of travel towards cloud-based, scripted analysis and the rationale for Microsoft Fabric as CQC’s analytical computing environment;
  • explain the purpose of sandbox and project workspaces in Fabric and what each is for;
  • explain what version control is and why it matters for reproducible, auditable analysis;
  • recognise the principles behind Reproducible Analytical Pipelines (RAP) and the practices that build towards them.

10.1 The Computational Context

Analytical work unfolds across many media. It happens in your own reasoning, in conversations with colleagues, in organisational processes and review meetings, on paper, in word processors and spreadsheets, and in computational environments where code runs against data. Statistical thinking is not reducible to keyboard and mouse. But the parts of an analysis that have to be reproduced, audited, and defended eventually need to be captured somewhere that supports those properties – and in practice that means a scripted, computational environment. This chapter sets out the tools available to CQC analysts, explains why D&I is moving towards cloud-based, scripted analysis, and describes what you need to get started.

Note that nothing here requires being a software engineer. The aim is the generation of analysis that can be reproduced, reviewed, and defended – three things that matter in a regulatory context where findings inform decisions about providers and need to be able to withstand scrutiny. The shift towards scripted, version-controlled, cloud-based work is the shift that makes those properties achievable at scale. The tools described below make it practical; the culture described at the end of this chapter is what actually gets you there.

Two boundaries are worth naming early. Data engineers in Analytical Content handle data ingestion, schema design, and pipeline infrastructure in CQC’s Enterprise Data Platform (EDP) – that is not the work this chapter describes. Data scientists work primarily in Azure Machine Learning and Databricks for machine learning and AI work at scale. This chapter is for quantitative aand mixed-methods analysts working on the analyses this book covers.

10.2 The Tool Landscape

Analysts across Data & Insight (D&I) work with several tools, each suited to different tasks. Two of them – R and Python – are the languages this book adopts, and the ones you will spend most time in for the analyses it covers. The others you will meet around them: a query language for getting at data, and a couple of tools for presenting or exploring it that the book does not use but you should know exist. All of them run in CQC’s cloud environment, Microsoft Fabric, introduced in the next section; both R and Python come ready to use there, with nothing to install.

R is a statistical computing environment with a well-established role in CQC quantitative work. It was built for statistics first, and that shows: its defaults, its data structures, and the ecosystem around it are all oriented towards analytical work, from data preparation and descriptive statistics through modelling and significance testing to visualisation and reporting. For most statistical tasks in this book, R does what you need without much ceremony.

Python is a general-purpose language with an analytical ecosystem just as capable. It covers the same full range of quantitative work in D&I – data wrangling, descriptive statistics, significance testing, statistical modelling, process control, visualisation, and reporting – and reaches comfortably beyond statistics into automation, data engineering, and machine learning. Where R is a specialist that happens to be excellent at analysis, Python is a generalist that analysis is one part of. For the work in this book the two are interchangeable; which you reach for is mostly a matter of what you and your team already know.

Both R and Python extend their built-in capabilities through packages (also called libraries): bundles of additional, ready-made functions that someone has written, tested, and published for others to reuse. Coding proficiency in both languages is among the skills the D&I Analytical Competency Framework describes, at a range of levels. The code examples throughout the book are written in both languages, so you can follow along in whichever you are developing; they lean on base language – the functions built into R and Python themselves – wherever it achieves the same result as a package, more on this below.

SQL is the query language for structured data, including CQC’s data estate in the EDP. It is not a substitute for R or Python – it retrieves and filters data rather than analysing it – but some SQL is a useful skill when interrogating data held on the EDP, and knowing enough to get at and filter what you need helps on most projects that draw on EDP sources. Work is underway to provide connections and integrations that will make hand-written SQL less necessary for routine analytical work over time.

Power BI is Microsoft’s business-intelligence and dashboarding tool, and there is a substantial body of Power BI work at CQC – interactive dashboards and reporting in particular. It is worth knowing it exists and is widely used, but it sits outside the analytical work this book covers, and the book does not touch on it.

Excel has genuine uses: building simple outputs, quick one-off look-ups, and interim calculations that do not constitute final findings. What it is not suited to is analysis that will be repeated, shared, or reviewed. Formula chains across worksheets are difficult to audit; manual steps cannot be verified without repeating them; the file format records no version history. For any analysis that contributes to a regulatory decision, the expectation is scripted code in R or Python.

Note🤔 Rethinking: Why Scripted Code Rather Than a Spreadsheet?

The case for scripted analysis is not about complexity or prestige, and it does not need a bespoke argument: government already sets one out. The Quality Assurance of Code for Analysis and Research guidance – the “Duck Book”, the companion to the Aqua Book on quality analysis for government – frames it as three properties that stack on one another.

Reproducible comes first: someone else, starting from the same data, can produce the same output by running your documented code. The Duck Book calls this the one thing you can guarantee about an analysis. In a spreadsheet, the “steps” are cell edits made by a person in an order no one recorded; a formula can be correct, but if a row was filtered before it ran and there is no record of that filter, the result cannot be reproduced.

Auditable builds on it: there is a trail of who made which analytical choice, when, and on what evidence. A version-controlled script carries that trail; a spreadsheet named “Final FINAL v3” does not. The Duck Book’s underlying point is that manual and semi-manual processes are slow and error-prone precisely because the inputs and the outputs are not connected directly – code with version control connects them and leaves an audit trail automatically.

Assured sits on top: a second person can read the logic and check it, and the depth of that checking is matched to the risk of the work – the Aqua Book’s proportionality principle. Reviewing interconnected formulas across worksheets is far harder than reviewing a script.

None of this means Excel is useless, however it falls short when it comes to reproducibility, auditability, and assurance, as well as when advanced analytics are concerned. If you are unsure whether a task calls for code, a simple rule of thumb is: if it will be repeated, reviewed, or defended, it calls for code.

10.3 Working in Microsoft Fabric

Microsoft Fabric is CQC’s cloud environment for analytical computing. It provides R and Python notebooks that run in your browser, storage connected to CQC’s data estate, and built-in integration with Azure DevOps for version control. There is no local software installation required.

Analysts use Fabric in two ways: sandbox workspaces for learning and experimentation, and project workspaces for real analytical work.

Sandbox workspaces

Every D&I function has a dedicated sandbox workspace on Fabric. These are available to all analysts in a function as soon as permissions are in place and staff records are current with the function lead. Sandboxes are the right place to learn Fabric, experiment with code, work through examples in this book, and build skills before applying them on real projects.

Sandboxes hold no EDP data by default, but you can load your own data. They are isolated learning environments, and they are the right starting point for anyone new to Fabric or to scripted analysis.

Project workspaces

When you have a specific analytical use case – work that needs EDP data, will be collaborated on by a team, or will produce a regulatory output – you can request a project workspace. The intention is that project workspaces will increasingly support not just notebook-based analysis but automated analytical pipelines, moving the function towards the reproducibility standards the AQuA Book recommends.

Project workspaces can also be used for work that is better kept compartmentalised – work not accessible to the whole function. The workspace configuration, including data access and permission groups, is agreed before the work starts. Discuss requirements with the Practice Lead before raising a request; the Platform and Solutions team will review the specification and advise on the appropriate configuration.

Data access in project workspaces is set up through shortcuts to relevant EDP sources. Shortcuts make data available without creating copies, which keeps data governance clean and avoids version drift between the source and your analysis.

Getting access

Fabric access requires two steps: a permissions set (raise an IT ticket for “Amend existing Power BI group” – the Power BI label is legacy and grants Fabric access) and, for project workspaces, a separate ticket for “New Workspace Request”. For the sandbox, no workspace ticket is needed: function workspaces already exist and are accessible once permissions are in place.

Discuss access with the Practice Lead before raising either ticket, so the right configuration is agreed upfront.

10.4 Version Control and Code Review

Version control is the practice of tracking every change made to a piece of code, with a record of who made the change, when, and what the intent was. Azure Repos – the repository service within Azure DevOps – provides this at CQC, and can be linked directly to a Fabric workspace so notebooks and scripts are version-controlled without leaving the Fabric environment.

Adopting version control is not optional for serious analytical work. It is what makes the following possible:

  • an assurer can review the code and see what changed between the version they reviewed and the version that produced the final output;
  • a future analyst can understand why a choice was made, not only what was chosen;
  • you can recover a previous version of the analysis if a change turns out to be wrong;
  • the history of an analysis is available as evidence if findings are challenged.

Connecting a repository to a Fabric workspace requires a brief setup – contact the Platform and Solutions team, who will configure the link. Guidance materials are available via the Guild channels. Once connected, commits, branches, and pull requests are managed from within Fabric.

Code review – where someone not involved in writing the analysis reads the code to check for errors, ambiguity, and departures from documented decisions – is part of the assurance process described in the Quality Assurance Principles chapter. For code review to be practical, the code must be in a repository the reviewer can access. Without version control, code review is not systematically possible.

D&I does not yet have a formal code review process. Developing that is part of building the analytical infrastructure that makes work fully auditable. In the meantime, the expectation is that scripted analysis is in a repository and that a second pair of eyes on the code is sought before high-stakes outputs are signed off. The Practice Lead can help identify reviewers and facilitate peer review.

Note🤔 Rethinking: What “Base Code” Means in Practice

The code examples in this book use base R and base Python wherever they achieve the same result as a package. This is not minimalism for its own sake – it is about four practical considerations.

First, base language is readable without knowing a package’s conventions. An analyst unfamiliar with a specific package can follow logic written in base code; they may not be able to follow the same logic written in a package’s domain-specific syntax, which creates a barrier to code review.

Second, Fabric’s pre-installed environment already covers the full analytical range of D&I work without additional packages. Where a package genuinely adds clarity – ggplot2 in R, pandas or matplotlib in Python – the examples use it and say so. Where base language achieves the same result clearly, that is what the examples show.

Third, reducing dependency keeps analyses portable. An analysis that imports ten packages may not run in a future environment where the same versions are not present; base language has no such fragility.

Fourth, every package is third-party code running inside CQC’s environment, and each one is a dependency you are trusting and a part of the analysis’s attack surface. Fewer dependencies means less exposure to supply-chain risk and less to vet. Packages should come from the approved, trusted sources available in Fabric rather than being pulled in ad hoc; when a regulator’s analysis rests on a library, where that library came from is a fair question.

When this guidance uses a package, it will always name it and explain why.

10.5 Towards Reproducible Analytical Pipelines

Reproducible Analytical Pipelines (RAP) is the standard recommended by the AQuA Book and the Government Analytical Function for statistical outputs in the public sector. The core requirement is straightforward: someone else, starting from the same data, can produce the same output by running your documented code. No manual steps, no copy-and-paste between applications, no “you had to be there.”

Building a complete RAP pipeline for every analysis is not the expectation today. CQC is building towards it, and there are a lot of moving parts: data infrastructure, version control adoption, shared coding environments, capability development across the function. That work takes time.

The expectation, from the first day of every project, is a culture of reproducibility:

  • Write code rather than manual steps, whenever possible.
  • Record every analytical decision as you make it – in comments in the code, or in an accompanying log – not after the analysis is complete.
  • Use version control so the history of changes is never lost.
  • Write code that can be run again from scratch, not code that produces a result only when started from a particular saved state.
  • When a step is automated, document the automation.

These habits cost almost nothing when built in from the start and a great deal when retrofitted. Every analysis set up this way is a building block; the function’s analytical infrastructure grows precisely because individual projects are structured correctly from the outset.

The Government Analytical Function’s RAP strategy describes a progression: Minimum RAP (open-source tooling, version control, peer review, no copy-and-paste between tools) leads to Silver RAP (functions, unit tests, documentation) and eventually Gold RAP (automated deployment and testing). Most work in D&I will be working towards Minimum RAP. The tools described in this chapter – Fabric notebooks, Azure Repos, the scripted examples throughout this book – make Minimum RAP reachable from a first project.

10.6 Learning, Support, and Building Together

The direction of travel described in this chapter – from spreadsheets towards scripted, version-controlled, cloud-based analysis – is a genuine shift. It reflects the direction the whole analytical profession is moving, which our new Analytical Competency Framework explicitly references. It also takes time, and no one is expected to arrive knowing how to do it.

CQC has invested in making the shift achievable:

  • Udemy courses on Python, R, and Fabric analytics are available to licensed users and provide structured learning for any starting point;
  • Microsoft Learn and free online courses provide open-access material for Fabric, Python, and statistical computing;
  • Guild activities include peer-learning sessions, resource sharing, and dedicated support for analysts developing these skills;
  • the Platform and Solutions team support Fabric setup, workspace configuration, and DevOps integration.

Most learning in this space happens through doing: starting in the sandbox, taking code from this book, adapting it to a real question, and building from there. Formal training complements that process; it is not a prerequisite for it.

Reach out early. Questions about whether something is achievable in Fabric, how to structure a repository, whether a particular library is available, or how to connect data access – those are worth a five-minute conversation with the Practice Lead or the Platform team now rather than hours of debugging later. The Guild is the right first port of call for peer support and general capability questions; the Practice Lead for project-specific decisions and anything that requires coordinating infrastructure.

The function is building this capacity together. An analysis written in clean, documented, version-controlled code is not just good work for the project at hand – it is a template that the next analyst on a similar question can learn from. That cumulative effect is how a culture of analytical quality compounds.

Most work in D&I will be working towards Minimum RAP. The tools described in this chapter – Fabric notebooks, Azure Repos, the scripted examples throughout this book – make Minimum RAP reachable from a first project.

## Learning, Support, and Building Together

The direction of travel described in this chapter – from spreadsheets towards scripted, version-controlled, cloud-based analysis – is a genuine shift. It reflects the direction the whole analytical profession is moving, which our new Analytical Competency Framework explicitly references. It also takes time, and no one is expected to arrive knowing how to do it.

CQC has invested in making the shift achievable:

- Udemy courses on Python, R, and Fabric analytics are available to licensed users and provide structured learning for any starting point;
- Microsoft Learn and free online courses provide open-access material for Fabric, Python, and statistical computing;
- Guild activities include peer-learning sessions, resource sharing, and dedicated support for analysts developing these skills;
- the Platform and Solutions team support Fabric setup, workspace configuration, and DevOps integration.

Most learning in this space happens through doing: starting in the sandbox, taking code from this book, adapting it to a real question, and building from there. Formal training complements that process; it is not a prerequisite for it.

Reach out early. Questions about whether something is achievable in Fabric, how to structure a repository, whether a particular library is available, or how to connect data access – those are worth a five-minute conversation with the Practice Lead or the Platform team now rather than hours of debugging later. The Guild is the right first port of call for peer support and general capability questions; the Practice Lead for project-specific decisions and anything that requires coordinating infrastructure.

The function is building this capacity together. An analysis written in clean, documented, version-controlled code is not just good work for the project at hand – it is a template that the next analyst on a similar question can learn from. That cumulative effect is how a culture of analytical quality compounds.
