15  Exploring Relationships: Correlation

TipWhat this is for

You need to know whether two things move together, and how strongly. For example:

  • Do practices in more deprived areas report worse patient access?
  • Is staffing level associated with incident rates in care homes?
  • Do providers with longer waits also attract more complaints?

Correlation puts a single number on the strength and direction of a relationship – a starting point for understanding context and generating hypotheses, and a number with a remarkable talent for being over-read.

15.1 Putting a Number on “They Move Together”

The chapter on data visualisation left a promise unpaid: its four scatter plots of identical summary statistics spoke of “the same strength of straight-line relationship” and deferred what that meant. This is where it is made precise. Pearson’s correlation coefficient \(r\) measures how tightly two variables follow a straight line together, on a scale from −1 to +1: at +1 the points lie exactly on a rising line, at −1 exactly on a falling one, and at 0 knowing one variable tells you nothing (linear) about the other. The sign is direction; the absolute value is strength; a correlation of −0.7 is exactly as strong as +0.7.

The construction is worth seeing, because it connects two ideas the book has already built. Convert each variable to z-scores – each value’s distance from its own mean, in its own standard deviations – and \(r\) is simply the average product of the paired z-scores:

\[r = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i} z_{x,i} \, z_{y,i}\]

In words: for each observation, multiply “how unusual it is on x” by “how unusual it is on y”, and average. When high-on-x reliably comes with high-on-y, the products are mostly positive and \(r\) climbs; when the pairings are haphazard, positives and negatives cancel and \(r\) sits near zero. Correlation is standardised co-movement – which is also why it is unitless and why it cannot care about how much access improves per decile of deprivation, only how consistently.

Squaring the coefficient gives the proportion of variation the two variables share: \(r = 0.5\) means \(r^2 = 0.25\), a quarter of the variation in one accounted for by a straight-line relationship with the other – and three-quarters not. Keeping \(r^2\) in view is the quickest cure for over-reading: correlations that sound impressive often share strikingly little.

One number cannot carry a relationship’s shape, though – that was the whole moral of the four scatter plots. The same \(r\) can come from a clean linear trend, a curve, or a single extreme point doing all the work. So the first rule of correlation has nothing to do with formulas: look at the scatter plot first, every time.

Pearson or Spearman

Spearman’s rank correlation \(\rho\) applies the same machinery to the ranks of the values rather than the values themselves. That single change buys three things: it measures any consistently increasing or decreasing (monotonic) relationship, straight or curved; it shrugs off outliers, since the most extreme value is just “rank 1” however extreme it is; and it is the honest choice for ordinal data – one rung down the measurement ladder – such as CQC ratings or deprivation deciles, where distances between categories are not really quantities.

Choose accordingly. Pearson when both variables are genuinely quantitative, the scatter plot looks linear, and nothing extreme is in play – and whenever the specifically linear relationship is what a downstream calculation needs. Spearman when either variable is ordinal, the relationship is monotonic but bent, or outliers are present. For exploratory work, if in doubt, Spearman: it rarely misleads, which is the property you want while exploring. Reporting both is cheap and itself informative – when they roughly agree, the relationship is close to linear and nobody extreme is steering it; when they diverge, the scatter plot will show you why.

How big is “big”? Treat every published banding of correlation strength with suspicion: what counts as strong is a property of the field, not of the number. In complex social and health data, where outcomes have many causes, an \(r\) of 0.4 between two provider-level measures can be a genuinely substantial finding; in a physical measurement context the same value would suggest broken equipment. The question is never “does it clear 0.5?” but “is a relationship of this size large enough to matter for the decision in front of us?” – the same significance-versus-importance discipline as everywhere else, with the extra step that \(r^2\) converts the coefficient into plain shares of variation.

15.2 Before You Start

Have a question, not a fishing rod. Correlation is cheap to compute, which is its main danger: a dataset of twenty variables offers 190 pairs, and at conventional thresholds about nine or ten of them will look “significant” by luck alone. Decide which relationships you have reason to examine – a mechanism you can articulate, a policy question, a claim to check – before computing anything, and treat anything found by browsing as a hypothesis for fresh data, not a finding. The arithmetic of this trap, and the corrections for when verdicts matter, live in the multiplicity section of the t-tests chapter.

Data requirements. Two variables measured on the same units, quantitative or ordinal; a reasonable number of observations (below about 30 the coefficient swings wildly from sample to sample); each unit appearing once (repeated measures of the same providers over time violate independence – Guild territory); and genuine variation in both variables, because a variable that barely varies has nothing to correlate.

When correlation is not the tool. Comparing providers against a benchmark → z-scoring. Monitoring change over time → statistical process control. Two categorical variables (rating by provider type) → chi-squared methods, with the Guild. Predicting one variable from several, or adjusting for confounders → regression, with the Guild.

QA framework alignment. Correlation work most directly supports QA question 2 (Design – is the analytical approach right for the question?) and question 3 (Analysis). Document the method choice, outlier handling, and – above all – the alternative explanations considered.

15.3 Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: Plot the scatter, and read it

One variable per axis, one point per unit. Read four things off it before any number: the overall shape (linear, curved, none); outliers (points far from the cloud – investigate them before they steer the coefficient); clusters (two distinct groups may need analysing separately, or may be the finding); and the variation available (a compressed range on either axis will mute any correlation – more on that below).

Step 2: Choose the coefficient

From the shape and the data types, per the guidance above. Note the choice and the reason – it is a method decision like any other.

Step 3: Calculate, with uncertainty

Report the coefficient, the sample size, and a confidence interval – with 100 observations an \(r\) of 0.5 carries an interval of roughly ±0.15, and with 20 observations the same \(r\) is little more than a gesture. Software supplies the interval for Pearson directly; the Overthinking box covers where it comes from and what to do for Spearman.

Step 4: Interpret all four dimensions

Strength (the coefficient, and \(r^2\) for shares of variation); direction (does the sign make sense against domain knowledge? a “wrong-signed” correlation is often the most informative kind); significance (does the interval exclude zero – and remember what that does and does not license); and context (what, other than the tempting causal story, could produce this association?).

Step 5: Report so the reader cannot over-read

A complete report names the method, the units and sample size, the coefficient with its interval, shows the scatter plot, and states in words both what the relationship is and what it is not – at minimum, that association is not causation, and which confounders are live candidates. The worked example below ends with a model paragraph.

15.4 Worked Example: Deprivation and GP Access

Scenario: you are examining whether GP practices in more deprived areas report worse patient access. For 100 practices you hold the area’s Index of Multiple Deprivation decile (1 = most deprived, 10 = least deprived) and the percentage of patients reporting they could get an appointment when they needed one, from the GP Patient Survey.

This is a contextual question – understanding how access relates to circumstances – not a performance comparison, and that framing matters for what the answer can be used for.

Calculate both coefficients

The deprivation decile is ordinal, which argues for Spearman; the scatter looks linear and well-behaved, which permits Pearson. Run both – agreement is itself a check.

  • R
  • Python
pearson  <- cor.test(gp_access$imd_decile, gp_access$access_pct)
spearman <- cor.test(gp_access$imd_decile, gp_access$access_pct,
                     method = "spearman", exact = FALSE)

cat("Pearson r:", round(pearson$estimate, 2),
    " 95% CI:", round(pearson$conf.int[1], 2), "to",
    round(pearson$conf.int[2], 2), "\n")

Interpret and report

The two coefficients agree (r ≈ 0.52, ρ ≈ 0.51), confirming what the plot showed: a roughly linear, moderately strong association not driven by any single point. A reporting paragraph that resists over-reading:

Across 100 GP practices, patient-reported access was moderately associated with area deprivation (Pearson’s r = 0.52, 95% CI 0.36 to 0.65; Spearman’s ρ = 0.51): practices in less deprived areas tended to report better access. Deprivation accounts for about a quarter of the variation in access (r² = 0.27) – practices vary widely at every level of deprivation, so local factors clearly matter at least as much. This is an association in cross-sectional data: it does not show that deprivation causes poor access, and plausible third factors (workforce availability, list pressure, premises) could contribute to both.

Note what the paragraph does for a regulator: it contextualises – access expectations should not be blind to circumstance – without excusing, because the wide scatter within every decile is exactly the evidence that providers in deprived areas can and do achieve good access.

Note🤔 Rethinking: what a correlation licenses a regulator to do

Suppose the analysis above is right: deprivation and access are genuinely associated. What follows for regulatory practice? Less than it seems, and the discipline of saying exactly what is the point of this box.

It does not follow that a poorly-performing practice in a deprived area is excused – the within-decile spread shows good access being achieved in the most deprived areas, and those practices would be wronged by a blanket allowance. It does not follow that deprivation causes poor access, so it does not follow that any particular adjustment or intervention will work. And it certainly does not follow that an individual deprived patient struggles to get an appointment – the analysis lives at practice level, and reading group-level associations as if they described individuals is the ecological fallacy, one of the oldest traps in area-based statistics.

What a robust association legitimately buys is narrower and still valuable: it informs interpretation (a flag concentrated in deprived areas may partly reflect circumstance, so look closer before concluding), it targets support and attention (if access problems cluster with deprivation, so should help), and it generates hypotheses for properly designed investigation. Association evidence steers where to look and how hard; it does not, by itself, justify treating any provider differently. That distinction – context for judgement, never a substitute for it – is the regulatory version of “correlation is not causation”.

15.5 The Ways Correlations Lie

A single point can invent one

Pearson’s r is built from products of distances, so one extreme point – extreme on both axes at once – contributes an enormous product and can conjure a relationship out of nothing. The pair of panels below is the same set of forty unrelated measurements; the right panel adds one fabricated extreme value.

The defence is the routine you already have: see the point on the scatter plot, investigate it, and report the correlation with and without it. Spearman’s robustness shows clearly here – which is a reason to check with Spearman, not a licence to skip the investigation: the extreme point may be the most important observation in the dataset.

Restricted ranges mute real relationships

A correlation needs variation to measure. Analyse only the practices in the three most deprived deciles and the deprivation–access correlation largely vanishes – not because the relationship is absent, but because deprivation barely varies in the subset, leaving noise to dominate. Range restriction haunts regulatory data in a specific way: any analysis confined to flagged providers, or to one rating band, has pre-compressed the very variation it then tries to correlate. State the range your data covers, and resist generalising beyond it.

Aggregation can reverse the story

Simpson’s paradox is the unsettling fact that a correlation can hold in every subgroup and reverse when the subgroups are pooled – nursing homes and residential homes might each show no staffing–incidents relationship, while the combined dataset shows a strong one, simply because the two types differ in both staffing levels and incident rates. Whenever the units plausibly belong to different families (provider types, sectors, regions), plot the groups distinguishably and check the within-group pattern before trusting the pooled coefficient.

Hunting finds fool’s gold

The multiplicity arithmetic from the t-tests chapter applies with extra force here, because correlation matrices generate comparisons quadratically. Twenty variables yield 190 coefficients; even pure noise hands you nine or ten “significant” ones. Pre-specify, report the full set examined, and treat browsed discoveries as hypotheses awaiting fresh data.

Warning⚙️ Overthinking: the machinery under the coefficient

Where the confidence interval comes from. The sampling distribution of r is awkward – squeezed against the −1/+1 boundaries – so the standard interval works on Fisher’s transformation \(z' = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1+r}{1-r}\), which is approximately normal with standard error \(1/\sqrt{n-3}\); build the interval there and transform back. The \(\sqrt{n-3}\) is the practical message: halving the interval’s width takes roughly four times the data, and below n ≈ 30 the interval is wide enough that the coefficient’s second decimal is decoration. R’s cor.test does this for Pearson automatically; for Spearman no equally simple formula exists, and bootstrap intervals are the usual answer (Guild, if it matters to the conclusion).

Attenuation: noisy measures dilute every correlation. If x and y are measured with error – and every CQC indicator is a noisy proxy for the thing it tracks – the observed correlation is biased towards zero, in proportion to the measures’ unreliability. Two consequences. A modest observed r between, say, a staffing proxy and a harm proxy may understate the true underlying relationship considerably. And correlations between changes (this year minus last year) are hit hardest, since differencing two noisy numbers compounds the noise – year-on-year change scores correlate weakly with almost everything, a fact regularly misread as “nothing matters”.

Non-linear but real. A U-shaped relationship – risk high at both extremes of, say, occupancy – can produce r ≈ 0 and ρ ≈ 0 while being the most important pattern in the data. No coefficient in this chapter detects it; only the scatter plot does, which is the visualise-first rule earning its keep one final time. Modelling curvature is regression territory, with the Guild.

15.6 Documenting Your Analysis

For QA purposes, record: the question and why a relationship analysis answers it; the method (Pearson, Spearman, or both) and the reason; the scatter plot, retained as evidence; outlier handling, with results shown with and without any influential points; the coefficient, interval, and sample size; the full set of correlations examined, not just the reportable ones; and the alternative explanations considered, with the causal claims you are not making stated explicitly. The chapter on quality assurance holds the templates.

15.7 Related Approaches

  • Comparing providers against a benchmark → z-scoring and funnel plots.
  • Two groups’ means rather than two variables’ co-movement → t-tests.
  • Relationships among three or more variables, prediction, or confounder adjustment → regression and partial correlation, with the Guild.
  • Two categorical variables (ratings by provider type) → chi-squared association methods, with the Guild.
  • Foundations → data visualisation for the scatter plot and the four-clouds warning; data types for the ordinal/quantitative distinction that drives the Pearson/Spearman choice; statistical inference for intervals and significance.
  • Complete workflow → Example 9: Staffing and Quality Outcomes.

15.8 When to Seek Support from the Guild

Raise your analysis with the Quantitative Analytics and Statistics Guild (the Guild) at a Quant Clinic session when: a causal question is actually on the table (programme effects, “does staffing drive harm?”) – observational causal inference is a specialism, not a stretch goal; confounding needs adjusting for rather than acknowledging; the relationship is visibly non-linear; the data are hierarchical (patients in practices, practices in systems) or repeated over time; the outcome is categorical; or a Spearman interval, partial correlation, or correlation-matrix-wide error control would change the conclusion.

### Step 4: Interpret all four dimensions

Strength (the coefficient, and $r^2$ for shares of variation); direction (does the sign make sense against domain knowledge? a "wrong-signed" correlation is often the most informative kind); significance (does the interval exclude zero – and remember what that does and does not license); and context (what, other than the tempting causal story, could produce this association?).

### Step 5: Report so the reader cannot over-read

A complete report names the method, the units and sample size, the coefficient with its interval, shows the scatter plot, and states in words both what the relationship is and what it is not – at minimum, that association is not causation, and which confounders are live candidates. The worked example below ends with a model paragraph.

### Calculate both coefficients

The deprivation decile is ordinal, which argues for Spearman; the scatter looks linear and well-behaved, which permits Pearson. Run both – agreement is itself a check.

:::: {.panel-tabset group="language"}
## R
```{r}
#| label: corr-calc
pearson  <- cor.test(gp_access$imd_decile, gp_access$access_pct)
spearman <- cor.test(gp_access$imd_decile, gp_access$access_pct,
                     method = "spearman", exact = FALSE)

cat("Pearson r:", round(pearson$estimate, 2),
    " 95% CI:", round(pearson$conf.int[1], 2), "to",
    round(pearson$conf.int[2], 2), "\n")
cat("Spearman rho:", round(spearman$estimate, 2), "\n")
cat("Shared variation r^2:", round(pearson$estimate^2, 2), "\n")
```

## Python
```{python}
#| label: corr-calc-py
from scipy import stats

pearson = stats.pearsonr(gp_access["imd_decile"], gp_access["access_pct"])
ci = pearson.confidence_interval()
rho, _ = stats.spearmanr(gp_access["imd_decile"], gp_access["access_pct"])

print(f"Pearson r: {pearson.statistic:.2f}"
      f"  95% CI: {ci.low:.2f} to {ci.high:.2f}")
print(f"Spearman rho: {rho:.2f}")
print(f"Shared variation r^2: {pearson.statistic**2:.2f}")
```

### Interpret and report

The two coefficients agree (r ≈ 0.52, ρ ≈ 0.51), confirming what the plot showed: a roughly linear, moderately strong association not driven by any single point. A reporting paragraph that resists over-reading:

> Across 100 GP practices, patient-reported access was moderately associated with area deprivation (Pearson's r = 0.52, 95% CI 0.36 to 0.65; Spearman's ρ = 0.51): practices in less deprived areas tended to report better access. Deprivation accounts for about a quarter of the variation in access (r² = 0.27) – practices vary widely at every level of deprivation, so local factors clearly matter at least as much. This is an association in cross-sectional data: it does not show that deprivation causes poor access, and plausible third factors (workforce availability, list pressure, premises) could contribute to both.

Note what the paragraph does for a regulator: it *contextualises* – access expectations should not be blind to circumstance – without excusing, because the wide scatter within every decile is exactly the evidence that providers in deprived areas can and do achieve good access.

::: {.callout-note icon=false}
## 🤔 Rethinking: what a correlation licenses a regulator to do

Suppose the analysis above is right: deprivation and access are genuinely associated. What follows for regulatory practice? Less than it seems, and the discipline of saying *exactly* what is the point of this box.

It does not follow that a poorly-performing practice in a deprived area is excused – the within-decile spread shows good access being achieved in the most deprived areas, and those practices would be wronged by a blanket allowance. It does not follow that deprivation causes poor access, so it does not follow that any particular adjustment or intervention will work. And it certainly does not follow that an *individual* deprived patient struggles to get an appointment – the analysis lives at practice level, and reading group-level associations as if they described individuals is the **ecological fallacy**, one of the oldest traps in area-based statistics.

What a robust association legitimately buys is narrower and still valuable: it informs *interpretation* (a flag concentrated in deprived areas may partly reflect circumstance, so look closer before concluding), it targets *support and attention* (if access problems cluster with deprivation, so should help), and it generates *hypotheses* for properly designed investigation. Association evidence steers where to look and how hard; it does not, by itself, justify treating any provider differently. That distinction – context for judgement, never a substitute for it – is the regulatory version of "correlation is not causation".
:::

## The Ways Correlations Lie

### A single point can invent one

Pearson's r is built from products of distances, so one extreme point – extreme on both axes at once – contributes an enormous product and can conjure a relationship out of nothing. The pair of panels below is the same set of forty unrelated measurements; the right panel adds one fabricated extreme value.

```{r}

Right: the same forty points plus a single extreme value – r jumps to 0.41, while rank-based Spearman barely moves (ρ = 0.12). If the coefficient changes when one point is removed, the point – not the relationship – is the finding to investigate."

set.seed(11)
base <- data.frame(x = rnorm(40, 50, 10), y = rnorm(40, 50, 10),
                   extreme = FALSE)
with_out <- rbind(base, data.frame(x = 95, y = 95, extreme = TRUE))

                           cor(d$x, d$y), cor(d$x, d$y, method = "spearman"))
base$panel     <- paste0("Without the extreme point\n", lab(base))
with_out$panel <- paste0("With one extreme point\n", lab(with_out))
both <- rbind(base, with_out)
both$panel <- factor(both$panel, levels = unique(both$panel))

ggplot(both, aes(x, y, shape = extreme, colour = extreme)) +
  geom_point(size = 2, alpha = 0.85, show.legend = FALSE) +
  scale_shape_manual(values = c(`FALSE` = 16, `TRUE` = 17)) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = c(`FALSE` = "#007EF5", `TRUE` = "#A83DA4")) +
  facet_wrap(~panel) +
  labs(x = "Indicator one", y = "Indicator two") +
```

The defence is the routine you already have: see the point on the scatter plot, [investigate it](06-unusual-observations.qmd), and report the correlation with and without it. Spearman's robustness shows clearly here – which is a reason to *check* with Spearman, not a licence to skip the investigation: the extreme point may be the most important observation in the dataset.

### Restricted ranges mute real relationships

A correlation needs variation to measure. Analyse only the practices in the three most deprived deciles and the deprivation–access correlation largely vanishes – not because the relationship is absent, but because deprivation barely varies in the subset, leaving noise to dominate. Range restriction haunts regulatory data in a specific way: any analysis confined to flagged providers, or to one rating band, has pre-compressed the very variation it then tries to correlate. State the range your data covers, and resist generalising beyond it.

### Aggregation can reverse the story

**Simpson's paradox** is the unsettling fact that a correlation can hold in every subgroup and reverse when the subgroups are pooled – nursing homes and residential homes might each show no staffing–incidents relationship, while the combined dataset shows a strong one, simply because the two types differ in both staffing levels and incident rates. Whenever the units plausibly belong to different families (provider types, sectors, regions), plot the groups distinguishably and check the within-group pattern before trusting the pooled coefficient.

### Hunting finds fool's gold

The multiplicity arithmetic from [the t-tests chapter](14-t-tests.qmd) applies with extra force here, because correlation matrices generate comparisons quadratically. Twenty variables yield 190 coefficients; even pure noise hands you nine or ten "significant" ones. Pre-specify, report the full set examined, and treat browsed discoveries as hypotheses awaiting fresh data.

## ⚙️ Overthinking: the machinery under the coefficient

**Where the confidence interval comes from.** The sampling distribution of r is awkward – squeezed against the −1/+1 boundaries – so the standard interval works on Fisher's transformation $z' = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1+r}{1-r}$, which is approximately normal with standard error $1/\sqrt{n-3}$; build the interval there and transform back. The $\sqrt{n-3}$ is the practical message: halving the interval's width takes roughly four times the data, and below n ≈ 30 the interval is wide enough that the coefficient's second decimal is decoration. R's `cor.test` does this for Pearson automatically; for Spearman no equally simple formula exists, and bootstrap intervals are the usual answer (Guild, if it matters to the conclusion).

**Attenuation: noisy measures dilute every correlation.** If x and y are measured with error – and every CQC indicator is a noisy proxy for the thing it tracks – the observed correlation is biased *towards zero*, in proportion to the measures' unreliability. Two consequences. A modest observed r between, say, a staffing proxy and a harm proxy may understate the true underlying relationship considerably. And correlations between *changes* (this year minus last year) are hit hardest, since differencing two noisy numbers compounds the noise – year-on-year change scores correlate weakly with almost everything, a fact regularly misread as "nothing matters".

**Non-linear but real.** A U-shaped relationship – risk high at both extremes of, say, occupancy – can produce r ≈ 0 and ρ ≈ 0 while being the most important pattern in the data. No coefficient in this chapter detects it; only the scatter plot does, which is the visualise-first rule earning its keep one final time. Modelling curvature is regression territory, with the Guild.
:::
